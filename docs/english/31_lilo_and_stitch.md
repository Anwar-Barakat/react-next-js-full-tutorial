# Learn English with Lilo & Stitch Guide

## 1. Scene 1 — Adopting Stitch

- **Lilo:** I want something that can defend itself. Something that won't die. Something sturdy, you know.
- **Nani:** The kennel's back this way. Go pick someone out.
- **Lilo:** Hello? Hello? Are there any animals in here?
- **Shelter worker:** Oh yes, all of our dogs are adoptable — except that one.
- **Lilo:** What is that thing?
- **Shelter worker:** A dog, I think. But it was dead this morning.
- **Lilo:** I like him. Come here, boy!
- **Nani:** Wouldn't you like a different dog? We have better dogs, dear.
- **Lilo:** Not better than him. He can talk! Say hello!
- **Shelter worker:** Oh, dogs can't talk, dear.
- **Lilo:** He did. Does it have to be this dog?
- **Lilo:** Yes. He's good. I can tell.
- **Shelter worker:** You'll have to think of a name for him.
- **Lilo:** His name is Stitch.
- **Shelter worker:** Stitch it is. And there's a two dollar license fee.
- **Lilo:** I wanna buy him. Can I borrow two dollars?
- **Shelter worker:** He's all yours.

## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Mischievous** — Having fun doing slightly bad things like making noise, breaking things, or causing trouble in a playful way. Used to describe dogs, children, or characters like Bart Simpson.
  - *Example: Stitch is characterized by his mischievous behavior.*

- **Endear** — To cause someone to be liked or loved. If something "endears you to someone," it makes them like you.
  - *Example: His charisma endeared him to the public.*
  - *Example: Stitch's mischievous nature endeared him to Lilo.*

- **Sturdy** — Strong and self-reliant; not delicate or fragile. When used about objects, it means solidly built and unlikely to break. The opposite is "delicate" or "flimsy."
  - *Example: I want something sturdy, you know.*
  - *Example: This is a sturdy chair, but this one looks flimsy.*

- **Wreck** — To damage something so badly that it cannot be repaired. Often used figuratively to mean "ruin." As a noun, a wreck is a car, plane, or train that has been severely damaged in a crash (e.g., "a train wreck," "a shipwreck").
  - *Example: You wreck everything you touch.*
  - *Example: His drinking habits wrecked his marriage.*

- **Evil** — Extremely bad or cruel; morally wrong.
  - *Example: I think it might be a koala. An evil koala.*

- **Stare** — To look at someone or something for a long time, usually making them feel uncomfortable.
  - *Example: It keeps staring at me like it's gonna eat me.*
  - *Example: Dad, why does she keep staring at me like that?*

- **Spill** — When liquid falls out of a container because it is too full, tilted, or knocked over.
  - *Example: This is your bottle — see, it doesn't spill.*

- **Borrow** — To ask to have or use something temporarily, with the intention of giving it back later.
  - *Example: Can I borrow two dollars?*

## 4. Dog & Animal Vocabulary

- **Kennel** — A small shelter for a dog (similar to a doghouse). Also used for a place where many dogs are kept, such as a dog shelter or boarding facility.
  - *Example: The kennel's back this way.*

- **Puppy** — A young dog.
  - *Example: Good puppy. Now get into bed.*

- **Kitten** — A baby cat. (The equivalent of "puppy" for cats.)

- **Pet (verb)** — To touch or stroke an animal (or sometimes a child) gently in a way that shows affection.
  - *Example: I can't even pet it.*

- **Boy / Girl** — Common terms used when talking to a dog. "Good boy" or "good girl" is a standard way to praise a dog for doing something well.
  - *Example: Come here, boy!*
  - *Example: That's a good boy.*

- **Dog commands:**
  - **Down** — A command to stop jumping or to get off a higher position.
    - *Example: Down! Be down off that!*
  - **Sit** — A command for a dog to sit on its haunches.
  - **Lay down** — A command for a dog to lie flat on the ground.

- **Pick someone/something out** — To choose one from a group.
  - *Example: Go pick someone out.*

- **It vs He/She for animals** — When we don't know the sex of an animal, we use "it," "itself," or "its." Once the animal becomes a member of the family or its sex is known, we use "he" or "she."
  - *Example: "What is that thing?" (it) → "I like him" (he, after Lilo bonds with Stitch)*

## 5. The "-able" Suffix

We sometimes add the suffix **"-able"** to words to create adjectives meaning "can be [verbed]" or "suitable for [verb-ing]":

- **Adoptable** — Can be adopted; available for adoption.
  - *Example: All of our dogs are adoptable.*

- **Believable** — Can be believed; seems true or convincing.
  - *Example: If a story is believable, then you think it's true.*

- **Doable** — Can be done; not too difficult; possible.
  - *Example: Staying on a strict diet is challenging, but it's doable.*

**More examples:**
- **Readable** — easy to read
- **Washable** — can be washed
- **Enjoyable** — can be enjoyed
- **Manageable** — can be managed
- **Breakable** — can be broken (= fragile)

**Tip:** Adding "-able" (or "-ible") to a verb is one of the most productive word-formation patterns in English. If you know the verb, you can often guess the meaning of the adjective.

## 6. Expressions & Idioms

- **I can tell** — I can perceive or know something; I can see or sense it.
  - *Example: He's good. I can tell.*
  - *Example: Physical fitness is very important to Shannon, as you can tell.*

- **[Something] it is** — Used when you are agreeing to a choice or making a final decision between two or more options.
  - *Example: "His name is Stitch." — "Stitch it is."*
  - *Example: "I'd rather go to a restaurant." — "A restaurant it is."*

- **It's all yours / He's all yours** — Something now belongs to the person you're speaking to, or they can use it freely.
  - *Example: He's all yours.*
  - *Example: The victory, the triumph, the glory — it's all yours.*

- **For a change** — To do something different than usual, perhaps because you're bored of always doing the same thing or because a new approach is needed.
  - *Example: Why not try and make something for a change?*
  - *Example: You should stop playing video games. Why don't you play outside for a change?*

- **This way** — Used to show someone the direction to a place. A host at a restaurant might say "Follow me, this way to your table."
  - *Example: The kennel's back this way.*

## 7. Grammar — Emphatic Commands with "Ever"

When you want to tell someone not to do something, you give a command using **"don't."** You can add increasing levels of emphasis:

**Level 1 — Basic command:**
- *Don't touch it.*

**Level 2 — Adding "ever" for emphasis:**
- *Don't ever touch it.* (stronger — means "never do this")
  - *Example from the movie: "Don't ever touch it."*

**Level 3 — Adding "you" for even more emphasis:**
- *Don't you ever touch it.* (even stronger — more personal and threatening)
  - *Example: "Don't you ever call me that."*

**Level 4 — Doubling "ever" for maximum emphasis:**
- *Don't you ever, ever touch it.* (extremely strong)

**Level 5 — Adding "never" for absolute emphasis:**
- *Don't you never ever touch it.* (the strongest possible form — technically a double negative but used in emphatic speech)
  - *Example: "Don't you ever, ever, never want to see another shark on this reef again. Ever."*

**Tip:** Each additional word increases the emotional intensity. This structure is common in emotional or confrontational situations, especially in movies and TV shows.

## 8. Connected Speech — H-Dropping & Linking

**1. H-dropping in pronouns — "him," "her," "he," "here":**
- In natural native speech, the H sound at the beginning of pronouns and common words is often dropped, and the word links to the previous one.
- **"I like him"** → sounds like **"I like-im"** /aɪ laɪkɪm/
- **"come here"** → sounds like **"come-eer"** or **"com-eer"**
- The H in "him," "her," "he," "his," and "here" is frequently dropped in fast speech.

**2. Linking after H-dropping:**
- When the H is dropped, the remaining vowel sound connects directly to the consonant at the end of the previous word.
- **"like him"** → "like-im" (the K links to the I)
- **"come here"** → "com-eer" (the M links to the EE)
- **"tell him"** → "tell-im" (the L links to the I)

**Practice:**
- "I like him" → "I like-im"
- "Come here, boy" → "Com-eer, boy"
- "Give her the book" → "Give-er the book"
- "Tell him I said hello" → "Tell-im I said hello"

**Tip:** H-dropping is one of the most common features of connected speech in English. It happens with function words (him, her, he, his, has, have, here) but NOT with content words (house, happy, help).

## 9. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 9.1 Lilo & Stitch — The Movie

- Lilo & Stitch (2002) is a Disney animated film set in Hawaii about a lonely Hawaiian girl named Lilo who adopts what she thinks is a dog from a shelter — but is actually Experiment 626, a destructive alien creature created by an extraterrestrial scientist.
- Lilo names him "Stitch" and tries to teach him to be good using Elvis Presley songs and the Hawaiian concept of "ohana" (family).
- The movie explores themes of family, belonging, and the idea that even someone "designed to destroy" can learn to love and be loved.
- Unlike most Disney films, Lilo & Stitch is set in a contemporary, realistic location (Kauai, Hawaii) rather than a fantasy world.

---

### 9.2 Hawaiian-American Culture

- Hawaii is the 50th state of the United States, located in the Pacific Ocean. It has a unique blend of native Hawaiian, Asian, and American cultures.
- The concept of **"ohana"** (family) is central to Hawaiian culture and to the movie. The famous line "Ohana means family, and family means nobody gets left behind or forgotten" captures this philosophy.
- Lilo is raised by her older sister Nani after their parents die — a situation that reflects the importance of family bonds in Hawaiian culture.
- The movie authentically portrays Hawaiian life: the characters live modestly, participate in local traditions (like hula dancing), and speak with references to Hawaiian values.

---

### 9.3 Pet Adoption and Dog Shelters

- In the movie, Lilo and Nani visit a **dog shelter** (also called a kennel or animal shelter) to adopt a pet.
- Pet adoption from shelters is very common in the United States and is generally encouraged over buying from pet stores or breeders.
- The shelter worker says "all of our dogs are adoptable" — meaning they are available and suitable for adoption.
- When Lilo chooses Stitch, the worker says "He's all yours" after she pays a **license fee** — in many US states, dog owners are required to register and license their pets.
- The adoption scene teaches several useful phrases: "pick someone out" (choose), "I can tell" (I can perceive), and "[name] it is" (agreeing to a decision).

---

### 9.4 Diminutives in English

- English doesn't use diminutives as commonly as some other languages, but they do exist, especially when talking to children or about pets.
- Lilo says **"dolly"** instead of "doll" — this is a diminutive form that sounds more affectionate and childlike.
- Common diminutives in English:
  - **dog** → **doggy/doggie** (used especially by or with children)
  - **cat** → **kitty** (affectionate term for a cat)
  - **duck** → **ducky/duckie** (affectionate)
  - **bird** → **birdie** (informal, also a golf term)
  - **doll** → **dolly** (childlike)
- These diminutive forms are created by adding **-y**, **-ie**, or **-ey** to the end of the word.
- They are appropriate when speaking to children, about pets, or in casual affectionate contexts — but would sound strange in formal situations.
