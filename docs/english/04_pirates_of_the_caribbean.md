## 6. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Madness** — Something too dangerous or stupid to be done. More literally, the state of being mentally ill.
  - *Example: What are you doing? This is madness!*

- **Brilliance** — The quality of being brilliant; having great intelligence or skill.
  - *Example: Her brilliance as a scientist earned her the Nobel Prize.*

- **Brilliant** — Having great intelligence or skill; exceptionally clever.
  - *Example: That is one of my more brilliant ideas.*

- **Either** — Used to refer to one choice between two possibilities.
  - *Example: There's only one way this ends — either you die, or I do.*

- **Remarkable** — Unusual, special, interesting, or excellent. Remarkable things make you want to make a remark about them.
  - *Example: Getting to meet the president would be pretty remarkable.*

- **Trait** — A particular characteristic, attribute, or quality that something or somebody has.
  - *Example: Speed is one of his traits as a soccer player.*

- **To coincide** — To happen at the same time; to occur together.
  - *Example: It's remarkable how often madness and brilliance coincide.*

- **Often** — Frequently; happening many times.
  - *Example: If you see your friends every day, you see each other often.*

- **To take over** — To assume or take control of something, often without authority and possibly by force.
  - *Example: Cutler Beckett is taking over the seas!*

- **To make it** — To accomplish something; to do it successfully (reaching a place, attending an event, arriving on time). Can also imply surviving a difficult situation.
  - *Example: I can't make it to the concert, I have to work.*

- **Savvy** — Used informally like "you know?" (pirate usage). More commonly: having good knowledge or understanding of a practical subject.
  - *Example: She is very business savvy. / You could say he's computer savvy.*

- **Rash** — Done impulsively without careful consideration of the possible consequences.
  - *Example: Getting married so quickly was a rash decision.*

- **Dauntless** — Showing determination and bravery; having no fear.
  - *Example: The soldiers showed dauntless courage in the war.*

- **Daunting** — Something that seems very difficult or intimidating.
  - *Example: Learning English is a daunting task, but it doesn't have to be boring.*

- **I'd rather** — A phrase used to say you would prefer to do something, or prefer that something happened.
  - *Example: He doesn't want to go out — he'd rather stay at home and play video games.*

- **Hard time** — A period of difficulty; not easy.
  - *Example: I'm having a hard time concentrating.*

- **Without doubt** — Certainly; there is no question about it.
  - *Example: That is without doubt the worst pirate I have ever seen.*

- **To clear up this mess** — To fix a problem or disorder that has been created.
  - *Example: Clear up this mess before the boss arrives.*

- **Range** — A distance within which you can still reach your target.
  - *Example: This radar has great range — it can detect ships more than 200 miles away.*

- **Milady** — An old-fashioned or formal way to address a woman of high social status (from "my lady").
  - *Example: Gentlemen... milady... you will always remember this day.*

- **To disable** — To make something unable to function or work properly.
  - *Example: He's disabled the rudder chain, sir.*

- **Incredible** — Extremely good or impressive; hard to believe.
  - *Example: The ship chase was an incredible scene.*

- **To intercept** — To stop something or someone from reaching their destination.
  - *Example: In American football, when you intercept a pass, you grab the ball your opponent had thrown.*

- **To catch** (someone) — To capture or apprehend someone, especially after chasing them.
  - *Example: You will always remember this as the day that you almost caught Captain Jack Sparrow!*

- **Stay calm** — To remain relaxed and not panic in a stressful situation.
  - *Example: Everyone, stay calm! We are taking over the ship!*

- **Open fire** — A military command meaning to start shooting.
  - *Example: Now will you shoot him? Open fire!*

- **Bring her around** — (Nautical) A command to turn the ship in a different direction.
  - *Example: Bring her around! Bring her around!*

- **We ought to** — Should; expressing obligation, duty, or a suggestion.
  - *Example: We ought to find our own ship.*

- **In the hands of** — Under the control or possession of someone.
  - *Example: I'd rather see her at the bottom of the ocean than in the hands of a pirate.*

- **That's got to be** — Informal expression meaning "that must be" — expressing strong belief or certainty.
  - *Example: That's got to be the best pirate I've ever seen.*

- **So it would seem** — A formal or ironic way to express agreement, often implying reluctance or surprise.
  - *Example: "That's the best pirate I've ever seen." — "So it would seem."*

- **Son** (informal address) — Used to address a younger man, not necessarily your child. Common in authority figures speaking to subordinates.
  - *Example: You'll never make it out of the bay, son!*

- **Dive in** — Figuratively: to start something enthusiastically. Literally: to jump into water.
  - *Example: So, are you ready? Let's dive in!*

- **By ourselves** — Alone; without help from anyone else.
  - *Example: We would have had a hard time of it by ourselves.*

- **Accordingly** — In a way that is appropriate to the circumstances; as a result.
  - *Example: The rest of you will be tried and punished accordingly.*

## 7. Nautical Vocabulary

- **Ship** — A large boat. We only call the larger watercraft "ships" (like the ones in Pirates of the Caribbean).
  - Related terms: **vessel** (formal), **cruise** (passenger ship), **ferry** (transport ship).
  - As a verb: **to ship** means to transport goods (by ship, truck, train, etc.).

- **Bay** — An area of water surrounded by land on three sides, with one way out to the ocean.
  - *Example: You'll never make it out of the bay.*

- **Crew** — An organized group of workers (on a ship, a film set, etc.). Implies cooperation.
  - As a verb: to crew a boat means to become part of the workers helping to sail it.
  - *Example: This ship cannot be crewed by two men.*

- **Sailor** — Someone who works on a boat or ship and follows the orders of an official or commander.
  - *Example: The sailors prepared the ship for departure.*

- **Cabin** — The rooms in a ship where people spend time or sleep.
  - *Example: Search every cabin!*

- **Hold** — The space in a ship where goods are carried.
  - *Example: The cargo was stored deep in the hold.*

- **Bilges** — The bottom inside part of a ship where dirty water collects.
  - *Example: Search every cabin, every hold, down to the bilges.*

- **Sail** — A big piece of material attached to a sailboat that catches wind and helps it move through the water.
  - **Top sails** — The sails located on top of the larger mainsails.
  - **To set sail** — To open the sails so the ship starts moving and traveling.

- **Rudder** — A flat piece of wood or metal at the back of a boat or aircraft, moved from side to side to control direction.
  - *Example: He's disabled the rudder chain, sir.*

- **Stern** — The back part of a ship.
  - *Example: With the wind at quarter of stern, we won't catch them.*

- **Quarter** — (Nautical) Refers to the direction from which the wind is hitting the ship.
  - *Example: Wind at quarter of stern.*

- **Long nines** — An informal term for naval long guns or cannons used for firing at other ships.
  - *Example: Just get them in range of the long nines.*

- **Come about** — A sailing term meaning to change direction.
  - *Example: Come about! Run out the guns!*

- **Run out the guns** — An order to move the guns into firing position (old ship guns were mounted on wheeled wooden structures).
  - *Example: Come about! Run out the guns!*

- **To make way** — (Nautical) To make progress or travel. In everyday English, to allow space for someone to pass.
  - *Example: Thank you for getting us ready to make way.*

- **Commodore** — A naval officer rank above captain and below admiral.
  - *Example: Commodore Norrington ordered the pursuit.*

## 8. Pirate English

In the Pirates of the Caribbean movies, we hear many expressions from "pirate English," which comes mostly from old British and Scottish English.

- **Aye** — Another way to say "yes."
  - *Example: Aye, aye, Captain!*

- **Avast** — A command meaning "stop!"
  - *Example: Avast, ye scallywags!*

- **Savvy** — Used like "you know?" or "do you understand?" at the end of a sentence (pirate usage).
  - *Example: I'm Captain Jack Sparrow. Savvy?*

## 9. Connected Speech — "Make It Out of the Bay"

The line **"You'll never make it out of the bay"** is a great example of connected speech in English:

1. **Silent E:** The word "make" ends in a K sound. The final E is silent (same as: save, pirate, male).

2. **Connecting words:** "Make" and "it" connect into one smooth sound: **make-it** → /meɪkɪt/.

3. **Tap T sound:** The T in "make it" and "out" becomes a soft tap T (like a quick D sound):
   - **make it out** → /meɪkɪɾaʊt/

4. **Pronouncing "of":** Many learners mispronounce "of" with an F sound. The correct pronunciation uses a **V sound**: /əv/.

**Full sentence spoken naturally:**
- "You'll never make it out of the bay" → /juːl ˈnɛvɚ ˈmeɪkɪɾˈaʊɾəv ðə ˈbeɪ/

**Practice examples from Friends:**
- "I have to find a job where I carry a smaller table" — connected: "where-I carry-a"
- "Don't you hate it when you have to cut a tin can?" — tap T on: "cut-a tin"
- "A place that no one will ever get out of alive" — connected: "get-out-of"

## 10. Grammar — The Third Conditional

The third conditional talks about the past. It describes **a situation that did NOT happen** and imagines the result of that situation.

**Structure:**

- **Main Clause (condition):** If + subject + **had** + past participle
- **Conditional Clause (result):** subject + **would have** + past participle

**Examples:**

- **If you had helped me, I would have passed the test.**
  - (You did NOT help me. The result: I did NOT pass.)

- **Jack Sparrow:** "We would have had a hard time of it by ourselves."
  - (Commodore Norrington DID help them unintentionally. If he hadn't, they would have struggled.)

- **Friends:** "If you had told me vegetarian lasagna, I would have made vegetarian lasagna."
  - (You did NOT tell me. The result: I did NOT make it.)

- **Up:** "If we had lost, we would have made them get rid of the birds."
  - (We did NOT lose. The result: we did NOT make them get rid of the birds.)

**Note:** Sometimes only the conditional clause is stated, and the main clause is implied from context — as Jack Sparrow does in the movie.

## 11. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 11.1 Ships Are "She"

- In English sailing tradition, ships are informally referred to as "she" instead of "it."
- Commodore Norrington says: "I'd rather see **her** at the bottom of the ocean" — referring to the Interceptor ship.
- This is an old nautical convention, not common in everyday English.

---

### 11.2 Ship Naming Conventions

- In sailing, it is common to give boats and ships names, often with meaningful adjectives:
  - **Dauntless** — meaning fearless and determined.
  - **Interceptor** — from "to intercept," meaning to stop something from reaching its destination. Named for its speed.
- Ship names reflect the character or purpose of the vessel.

---

### 11.3 Naval Ranks

- The movie features British Royal Navy ranks:
  - **Sailor** — general crew member.
  - **Captain** — commands a ship.
  - **Commodore** — ranks above captain, below admiral.
  - **Admiral** — highest naval rank.
- Jack Sparrow insists on being called "**Captain** Jack Sparrow" throughout the series.

---

### 11.4 Pirate English Origins

- "Pirate English" in the movies comes mostly from old British and Scottish English dialects.
- Words like "aye," "avast," and "savvy" were used by real sailors and have historical roots.
- The distinctive pirate accent in movies was largely popularized by actor Robert Newton in the 1950 film Treasure Island.
