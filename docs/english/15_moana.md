## 1. Scene 1 — Maui Introduces Himself

- **Maui:** Maui — shapeshifter, demigod of the wind and sea. I am Moana—
- **Moana:** Hero of men! What? It's actually: Maui, shapeshifter, demigod of the wind and sea, hero of men.
- **Maui:** I interrupted. From the top — hero of men. Go.
- **Moana:** I am— sorry, sorry, sorry, sorry — and women. Men and women, both.
- **Maui:** Oh, not a guy-girl thing. Uh, you know, Maui is a hero to all. Oh, you're doing great.
- **Moana:** What? No, I'm here—
- **Maui:** Of course, of course, yes, yes, yes. Now — Maui always has time for his fans.
- **Maui:** When you use a bird to write with, it's called tweeting.
- **Maui:** I know — not every day you get a chance to meet your hero.
- **Moana:** You are not my hero. And I'm not here so you can sign my oar. I'm here 'cause you stole the heart of Te Fiti, and you will board my boat, sail across the sea, and put it back.
- **Maui:** Um... yeah. It almost sounded like you don't like me — which is impossible, because I got stuck here for a thousand years trying to get the heart as a gift for you mortals, so you can have the power to create life itself. Yeah. So what I believe you were trying to say is — thank you.
- **Moana:** Thank you?
- **Maui:** You're welcome!

## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Greatness** — The quality of being great; outstanding importance, fame, power, or success.
  - *Example: You're face to face with greatness and it's strange.*

- **Adorable** — Charming and easy to love; very cute or endearing.
  - *Example: You don't even know how you feel — it's adorable!*
  - *Example: Aren't they adorable?*

- **Breathe it in** — Literally: to inhale deeply. Figuratively: to take in and appreciate something fully. Maui uses it to imply Moana is overwhelmed by his presence.
  - *Example: Yes, it's really me — it's Maui! Breathe it in.*

- **Bod** — A short, informal way of saying "body."
  - *Example: I know it's a lot — the hair, the bod.*

- **To stare** — To look at someone or something for a long time with your eyes wide open, often because of surprise or admiration.
  - *Example: When you're staring at a demigod.*

- **Tide** — The regular rising and falling of the sea level, which happens twice a day due to the gravitational pull of the moon and sun.
  - *Example: What can I say except you're welcome — for the tides, the sun, the sky!*

- **Ordinary** — Normal; not special or unusual. The exact opposite of "extraordinary."
  - *Example: I'm just an ordinary demi-guy.*

- **Oar** — A pole with a flat blade at one end, used to row a boat through water.
  - *Example: I'm not here so you can sign my oar.*

- **Stuck** — Unable to move from a certain place; trapped.
  - *Example: I got stuck here for a thousand years.*

- **To steal / Stole** — To take something that belongs to someone else without permission.
  - *Example: You stole the heart of Te Fiti.*

- **To board** — To get on a vehicle (boat, plane, train, bus).
  - *Example: You will board my boat, sail across the sea, and put it back.*

- **To sail** — To travel across water in a boat or ship.
  - *Example: You will board my boat, sail across the sea.*

- **Fan** — An enthusiastic admirer or supporter of someone or something.
  - *Example: Maui always has time for his fans.*

- **To sign** — To write your name on something, especially as an autograph.
  - *Example: I'm not here so you can sign my oar.*

- **To interrupt** — To stop someone while they are speaking or doing something.
  - *Example: I interrupted. From the top — hero of men.*

- **Self-esteem** — Confidence in your own worth or abilities; how you feel about yourself.
  - *Example: Maui has really high self-esteem — he thinks he is better than everyone else.*

- **Indifferent** — Having no particular interest or concern; not caring one way or another.
  - *Example: While Maui assumes Moana is awestruck, she is actually quite indifferent towards his presence.*

- **Humility** — The quality of being humble; not thinking you are better than others.
  - *Example: Maui is showing some fake humility by saying he is just an ordinary demi-guy.*

- **Swollen ego** — An exaggerated sense of self-importance; being overly proud of oneself.
  - *Example: Maui has a really swollen ego.*

- **Face to face** — Directly in front of someone; meeting in person.
  - *Example: You're face to face with greatness.*

## 4. Mythology & Fantasy Vocabulary

- **Demigod** — Someone who has less power than a god but more power than humans. Demigods are common figures in many types of mythology, especially Greek mythology.
  - *Example: Maui — shapeshifter, demigod of the wind and sea.*

- **Shapeshifter / Shape-shifting** — Someone who has the ability to change their physical form at any moment they choose.
  - *Example: Maui is a shapeshifter — he can transform into different animals.*

- **Mortal / Mortals** — A human being who is unable to live forever (opposite of immortal). Men and women are called mortals because they eventually die.
  - *Example: I got stuck here trying to get the heart as a gift for you mortals.*

- **Immortal** — A being that cannot die; living forever. Gods and demigods are typically immortal creatures.
  - *Example: Demigods and gods are immortal creatures — they don't die.*

- **Mythology** — A collection of myths (traditional stories) belonging to a particular culture or religion, often explaining natural phenomena or the origins of the world.
  - *Example: In Hawaiian mythology, Maui is considered to be a hero and an ancient chief.*

- **Heart of Te Fiti** — In the movie, a mystical stone that is the source of all life. Maui stole it, causing darkness to spread across the ocean.
  - *Example: You stole the heart of Te Fiti, and you will put it back.*

## 5. Puns & Wordplay

- **"Tweeting"** — Maui explains that "when you use a bird to write with, it's called tweeting." The pun works on two levels:
  - **Literal:** Birds tweet (make chirping sounds).
  - **Modern:** "Tweeting" refers to posting on Twitter (now X), a social media platform whose logo is a bird.
  - *Example: When you use a bird to write with, it's called tweeting.*

- **"Demi-guy"** — Maui replaces the word "god" in "demigod" with "guy" to create "demi-guy," pretending to be humble and ordinary. The humor comes from the fact that Maui is anything but humble.
  - *Example: I'm just an ordinary demi-guy.*

## 6. Expressions & Idioms

- **From the top** — An expression meaning "from the beginning." Used when you want someone to start over.
  - *Example: I interrupted. From the top — hero of men. Go.*

- **Not a (guy-girl / this-that) thing** — Used to dismiss a distinction between two groups; saying it applies to everyone equally.
  - *Example: Oh, not a guy-girl thing — Maui is a hero to all.*

- **Face to face with** — Directly in front of; meeting in person.
  - *Example: You're face to face with greatness and it's strange.*

- **What I believe you were trying to say is** — A phrase used (often sarcastically or arrogantly) to reinterpret what someone said to mean something different.
  - *Example: So what I believe you were trying to say is — thank you.*

- **Not every day you get a chance to** — Used to emphasize that something is rare and special.
  - *Example: Not every day you get a chance to meet your hero.*

- **Got stuck** — Became trapped; was unable to leave or move from a place.
  - *Example: I got stuck here for a thousand years.*

## 7. Connected Speech — The American T (Flap T)

When the **T sound** falls between two vowel sounds in American English, it morphs into a **soft D sound** (called the flap T or tap T).

**From the movie — "Breathe it in":**
- The pronoun **"it"** is an unstressed function word that connects to the next word **"in."**
- The T in **"it"** falls between two vowel sounds (the "ee" of "breathe" and the "i" of "in"), so it becomes a soft D.
- **breathe it in** → sounds like /briːðɪɾɪn/ ("breathe-id-in")

**More examples:**
- **water** → /wɑɾər/ ("wadder")
- **better** → /bɛɾər/ ("bedder")
- **got it** → /ɡɑɾɪt/ ("god-it")
- **wait with** → /weɪɾwɪð/ ("wade-with")
- **do it** → /duːɪɾ/ ("do-id")

**Tip:** This sound change is extremely common in American English. Knowing it is crucial for both understanding native speakers and sounding more fluent yourself.

## 8. Connected Speech — Content Words vs Function Words

In English, **content words** (nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs) are **stressed**, while **function words** (pronouns, prepositions, articles, auxiliary verbs) are **unstressed**.

When we speak naturally, unstressed function words often **hook onto** or **connect to** the nearest stressed content word, creating smooth, linked speech.

**From the movie — "Breathe it in":**
- **Content words (stressed):** "breathe" and "in"
- **Function word (unstressed):** "it"
- The pronoun "it" hooks onto the following word "in," and all three words flow together as one smooth phrase.

**Why this matters:**
- Native speakers don't pause between every word — they group words together.
- If you stress every word equally, your speech will sound unnatural.
- Learning to identify content words vs function words will dramatically improve both your listening comprehension and your speaking fluency.

## 9. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 9.1 Moana — The Movie

- Moana (2016) is a Disney animated film set in ancient Polynesia.
- The story follows Moana, the daughter of a village chief, who sets sail across the ocean to find the demigod Maui and convince him to return the stolen Heart of Te Fiti.
- The film features music by Lin-Manuel Miranda, including the hit song "You're Welcome" performed by Dwayne "The Rock" Johnson as Maui.
- Moana was praised for its respectful portrayal of Polynesian culture and mythology.

---

### 9.2 Maui in Hawaiian Mythology

- In Hawaiian mythology, Maui is a legendary hero and ancient chief known for incredible feats.
- Disney took inspiration from this mythological figure to create the character in the movie.
- In the film, Maui is a demigod with the power to shape-shift into different animals using his magical fishhook.
- According to the movie's mythology, Maui created many things for humanity — the tides, the sun, the sky — which is why he sings "What can I say except you're welcome."

---

### 9.3 Maui's Ego and Fake Humility

- Maui has an enormously swollen ego — he genuinely believes he is the greatest being alive and that everyone should worship him.
- When he meets Moana, he immediately assumes she is a fan who is starstruck by his greatness.
- His song "You're Welcome" is essentially him listing all the amazing things he has done and expecting gratitude.
- The humor comes from the contrast between Maui's self-absorption and Moana's complete indifference — she doesn't care about his fame; she just wants him to return the Heart of Te Fiti.

---

### 9.4 The "Demi-Guy" Wordplay

- In the song, Maui sings "I'm just an ordinary demi-guy" — replacing "god" in "demigod" with "guy."
- This is a moment of **fake humility** — Maui pretends to be modest by calling himself just a regular "guy," but everything else about the song screams the opposite.
- The wordplay works because "demi-guy" sounds natural and funny, while also highlighting the contrast between what Maui says (I'm ordinary) and what he means (I'm extraordinary).
