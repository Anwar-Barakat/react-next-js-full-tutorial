# Learn English with Finding Dory Guide

A comprehensive English lesson based on scenes from Disney Pixar's Finding Dory (2016), covering vocabulary related to memory and childhood (short-term memory loss, pretend, hide and seek), nautical and informal expressions (ahoy there, floor it, fishtail), texture vocabulary (squishy), reassuring phrases (we're good, all good), connected speech reductions ("what do I do" → "wha do ya do," "going to" → "gonna," "you" → "ya," "to" → "ta"), phrasal verbs with multiple meanings (get back), and cultural themes about Dory's memory condition and Finding Nemo's legacy.

## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Short-term memory loss** — The inability to remember things for more than a short period of time. "Short-term" describes something that does not last long, so Dory's ability to remember things only lasts a short time.
  - *Example: I suffer from short-term memory loss.*
  - *Example: Short-term memory loss? I don't believe this.*

- **Squishy** — Soft and wet, or soft to the touch. You can use this word to describe things like wet sand, certain toys, or soft foods.
  - *Example: I like sand. So, I'm a squishy.*
  - *Example: Squishy. What is this stuff?*

- **Floor it** — An informal way of telling someone to drive as fast as possible. Dory wants Hank to get to the ocean as quickly as they can before they get caught.
  - *Example: The ocean! Get straight ahead! Floor it!*

- **Floored** — A related but different meaning: to be so surprised or confused that you're unable to think of what to say or do next.
  - *Example: When I saw the picture, I was completely floored.*

- **Fishtail** — When a vehicle's rear end slides from side to side, losing traction. This often happens when driving too fast or on slippery surfaces.
  - *Example: They're going to fishtail.*

## 4. Childhood & Play Vocabulary

- **Pretend** — To behave as if something is true when you know that it is not. Also used to refer to things that are imaginary or not real, such as fairy tales or cartoons.
  - *Example: We'll pretend to be the other kids now.*
  - *Example: Here, pretend that that's a scene. — It's a rock. — Oh, I know it's a rock, but let's just pretend for a minute that it's a seed, alright?*

- **Hide and seek** — A children's game where one person covers their eyes and counts while the other players hide, and then the counter tries to find them.
  - *Example: Ahoy there! Want to play hide and seek?*
  - *Example: Okay, we'll hide and you count and come find us.*

- **Count** — To say numbers in order. In hide and seek, the seeker counts (usually to ten) while the other players hide.
  - *Example: Now count to ten. — One, two, three, four...*

## 5. Expressions & Idioms

- **Ahoy there** — A nautical term used to attract attention or say hello, especially when someone is on a ship or boat. It is especially common among sailors and pirates in films and TV shows. Dory's dad uses it playfully — which is funny because instead of being on a ship, they're already underwater.
  - *Example: Hi, Dory. Ahoy there! Want to play hide and seek?*

- **We're good / All good** — An informal expression meaning "everything is fine" or "don't worry." It can be used to reassure someone in a stressful situation.
  - *Example: Okay, we're good.*
  - *Example: Easy, easy. Oh, we're good. We're good.*

- **Now we're talking** — An expression used to agree with a suggestion or to show that you like someone's offer or idea.
  - *Example: Floor it! — Oh, now we're talking.*

- **No way out** — There is no way to escape a situation; you are trapped.
  - *Example: Oh, there's no way out. It's over.*

- **I'm okay with crazy** — An informal way of saying you're willing to try something unusual, risky, or unconventional.
  - *Example: I'm going to ask you to do something crazy. — I'm okay with crazy.*

## 6. Connected Speech — Reductions & Linking

**1. "What do I do" — Multiple reductions:**

- When spoken quickly, several sounds change:
  - The **T** at the end of **"what"** is almost silent, sounding like **"wha."**
  - The **O** at the end of **"do"** becomes a schwa sound, like **"duh."**
  - The word **"you"** (in similar phrases) is reduced to **"ya."**
  - Full: **"What do I do?"**
  - Connected: **"Wha do I duh?"** (spoken as one rapid unit)

**2. "I'm going to ask you to" — Contracting "going to" and reducing "you":**

- **"Going to"** is contracted to **"gonna"** — this is extremely common in everyday speech.
- **"You"** is shortened to **"ya."**
- The **O** in **"to"** becomes a schwa sound, like **"ta."**
  - Full: **"I'm going to ask you to do something crazy."**
  - Connected: **"I'm gonna ask ya ta do something crazy."**

**3. General reduction patterns in this lesson:**

- **"Gonna"** for "going to" — One of the most common reductions in English.
- **"Ya"** for "you" — Very common in casual speech. Make sure to pronounce it as **"ya"** and not "yeah" (which means "yes") or "y'all" (which means "you all").
- **Schwa sounds** — Unstressed vowels in function words like "to," "do," and "of" are frequently reduced to a neutral schwa sound (uh).

**Tip:** These reductions are extremely common in everyday English. Recognizing patterns like "gonna" for "going to" and "ya" for "you" is essential for understanding native speakers in movies and real conversations.

## 7. Phrasal Verb — "Get Back" (Multiple Meanings)

The phrasal verb **"get back"** has several different meanings depending on context:

- **Meaning 1 — Move away (from danger):** Used as a warning to tell someone to move away because they are in danger.
  - *Example: Fire! Get back!*

- **Meaning 2 — Return to a place:** To go back to where you were before.
  - *Example: It's late, I need to get back home.*

- **Meaning 3 — Receive something again:** To have something returned to you after it was lost or stolen.
  - *Example: I forgot my bag on the train and don't know how to get it back.*

**Tip:** Phrasal verbs like "get back" are challenging because their meaning changes completely depending on the situation. Pay attention to the context — is someone in danger? Are they talking about returning somewhere? Or about retrieving a lost object? The surrounding words will always tell you which meaning is intended.

## 8. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 8.1 Finding Dory — The Movie

- Finding Dory (2016) is a Disney Pixar animated film and the sequel to Finding Nemo (2003). The story follows Dory, a blue tang fish who suffers from short-term memory loss, voiced by the talented Ellen DeGeneres.
- The one thing Dory remembers clearly is that she was separated from her parents as a child. When fragments of her past start coming back to her, she decides to go on an epic adventure to find them.
- The film takes Dory, Marlin, and Nemo to the Marine Life Institute in California, where Dory teams up with Hank, a grumpy but resourceful seven-legged octopus (technically a "septopus"), to navigate the facility and find her family.
- Finding Dory was a massive commercial success, earning over $1 billion worldwide, and was praised for its heartfelt story about family, memory, and perseverance.

---

### 8.2 Dory's Memory Loss — Comedy & Heart

- Dory's short-term memory loss is both the film's primary source of comedy and its emotional core. The condition causes her to forget things "almost instantly," which leads to many humorous situations — like forgetting she's playing hide and seek with her parents and getting distracted by sand.
- However, the film also treats her condition with genuine emotional depth. The flashbacks to baby Dory show her parents patiently teaching her strategies to cope with her memory loss, like practicing what to say when meeting new people: "I suffer from short-term memory loss."
- Her parents' love and patience — pretending to be other children so Dory can practice socializing — is one of the most touching aspects of the film.
- For English learners, Dory's tendency to forget and repeat things actually makes the dialogue easier to follow, as key phrases and vocabulary are naturally repeated throughout the film.

---

### 8.3 Nautical Language & Underwater Wordplay

- Like Finding Nemo before it, Finding Dory plays with the fact that the characters are underwater creatures using human expressions:
  - **"Ahoy there"** — A nautical greeting typically used on ships, which is humorous because the characters are already underwater.
  - **"Fishtail"** — In driving, this means a vehicle's rear end sliding from side to side. In the context of fish characters driving a truck, the word takes on a double meaning.
  - **"Floor it"** — An expression about driving fast, used in a scene where an octopus and a fish are illegally driving a truck through traffic.
- This tradition of adapting human expressions to fit an underwater world is a signature of the Finding Nemo/Dory franchise and makes the films particularly useful for English learners who want to discover everyday expressions in a playful context.

---

### 8.4 "Remembery" — How Children Invent Words

- In the scene with baby Dory, she uses the word **"remembery"** — which is not a real English word. She is mistakenly combining "memory" and "remember" because they have similar meanings and sounds.
- This is a very common phenomenon in child language development called **"overgeneralization"** — children apply rules or patterns they've learned to new situations, sometimes creating words that don't exist.
- Other examples of words children commonly invent:
  - "Goed" instead of "went" (applying the regular past tense -ed rule)
  - "Mouses" instead of "mice" (applying the regular plural -s rule)
  - "Gooder" instead of "better" (applying the regular comparative -er rule)
- For English learners, this scene is a useful reminder that "memory" (noun) and "remember" (verb) are related words but are used differently: "I have a bad memory" vs. "I can't remember." The correct term for Dory's condition is **"memory loss"** — not "remembery loss."
