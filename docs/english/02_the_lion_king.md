## 2. Clip 2 — Hakuna Matata / "What's a Motto?"

- **Timon:** Repeat after me — Hakuna Matata.
- **Simba:** What?
- **Timon:** Hakuna Matata. It means no worries.
- **Timon & Pumbaa:** ♪ Hakuna Matata, Hakuna Matata — ain't no passing craze. It's our motto! ♪
- **Simba:** What's a motto?
- **Timon:** Nothing! What's a motto with you?
- **Pumbaa:** (laughs) It'll solve all your problems!
- **Timon:** That's right!

## 8. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Intimately** — Having a very deep or close knowledge of something or someone.
  - *Example: She knew the city intimately after living there for 20 years.*

- **To endure** — To continue to exist; to last over time.
  - *Example: The reason the Lion King has endured is because its message is universal.*

- **Remains true** — Continues to be relevant, honest, or faithful.
  - *Example: It's a story that remains true to everybody.*

- **Motto** — A short sentence or phrase that expresses a belief or rule used to guide behavior.
  - *Example: "Be prepared" is the motto of the Boy Scouts.*

- **To inherit** — Literally: to receive money/property after someone dies. Figuratively: to gain or benefit from something that came before.
  - *Example: She inherited a fortune from her father.*

- **Tremendous** — Very great in amount, scale, or intensity; used to emphasize how positive or negative something is.
  - *Example: There was a tremendous opportunity to retell the story.*

- **A set of (things)** — A group or collection of related things.
  - *Example: A new set of tools and a new technology.*

- **To have it all** — To be very talented and capable of doing everything necessary.
  - *Example: John just seems to have it all.*

- **To hold it all together** — To keep all different parts of a larger whole running and working together.
  - *Example: It's impressive how he just sort of holds that all together.*

- **Vast** — Very great in size, amount, degree, or intensity.
  - *Example: A vast array of talent has been assembled.*

- **Array** — A large group of things or people organized together.
  - *Example: The vast array of talent assembled for this film.*

- **To assemble** — To come together in a single place; to bring parts together for a common purpose.
  - *Example: Nick Fury assembled the Avengers to save the world.*

- **Cub** — A young carnivorous mammal (bears, tigers, lions).
  - *Example: No, son. You stay with the other cubs.*

- **Problem-free** — Having no problems; not creating any problems.
  - *Example: So far the trip has been trouble-free.*

- **Invested (adjective)** — Having a strong interest in something as a result of a commitment to it.
  - *Example: She's deeply invested in this project and wants it to succeed.*

- **Compelling** — So interesting or exciting that you want to keep watching, reading, or looking at it. Also: a compelling argument effectively convinces you something is true.
  - *Example: His eyes were strangely compelling.*

- **To capture** — Literally: to trap or take by force. Figuratively: to accurately represent or express something.
  - *Example: She hopes the photo will capture the excitement of her trip.*

- **Combo** — A combination of different things, usually implying a good combination.
  - *Example: This place serves a great burger and milkshake combo.*

- **Red carpet** — A long red carpet put down for important guests; refers to celebrity arrival events.
  - *Example: The Met Gala red carpet is always full of stunning outfits.*

- **To dress up** — To wear particularly elegant clothing for a special event, or to wear costumes.
  - *Example: My daughter gets to dress up and my husband.*

- **To play (a part)** — To act as a character in a movie or performance.
  - *Example: Beyonce plays the lion Nala.*

- **To star in** — To be a principal actor in a movie or show.
  - *Example: Emma Watson starred in the Harry Potter movies.*

- **Appearance** — The act of going to a place, especially briefly (used for celebrities or important people).
  - *Example: Obama is making an appearance at the charity fundraiser.*

- **Grew up (doing something)** — To have done something often or habitually as a child.
  - *Example: I grew up camping a lot during the summer.*

- **Universal** — Applying to all people or situations everywhere; shared by everyone.
  - *Example: The inspiration we're getting from it is universal.*

- **To relive** — To experience something again, especially something from the past.
  - *Example: Getting to relive it in a new way is really exciting.*

- **Ain't** — Informal contraction of "am not," "is not," "are not," "has not," or "have not." Common in casual and musical English.
  - *Example: It ain't no passing craze.*

- **Passing craze** — A temporary trend or fashion that quickly disappears.
  - *Example: Some people thought the internet was just a passing craze.*

- **To retell** — To tell a story again, often in a new or different way.
  - *Example: There was a tremendous opportunity to retell the story.*

- **Privilege** — A special honor or advantage; something you feel fortunate to have or do.
  - *Example: It's a privilege to be counted among these actors.*

- **To be counted among** — To be included as part of a respected group.
  - *Example: She's proud to be counted among the world's top scientists.*

- **To admire** — To respect and approve of someone or something.
  - *Example: I'd really admire the vast array of talent assembled for this film.*

- **Rich tradition** — A long-standing, valuable heritage or custom with deep history.
  - *Example: There was such a rich tradition surrounding the songs.*

- **Imagery** — Visual images collectively; the use of vivid descriptions or visuals to represent ideas.
  - *Example: We wanted to capture people's memory with both the music and the imagery.*

- **Spirit (of something)** — The essential quality, character, or feeling of something.
  - *Example: When you can capture the spirit but show them something new — it's the best combo.*

- **To rediscover** — To find or experience something again after a period of time.
  - *Example: I love the idea of audiences rediscovering something.*

- **Sort of** — Somewhat; used to soften a statement or make it less definite. Common filler in spoken English.
  - *Example: He just sort of holds that all together.*

- **Incredible** — Extremely good or impressive; hard to believe.
  - *Example: This has been a really incredible experience.*

- **Sophisticated** — Advanced, complex, or highly developed (referring to technology or ideas).
  - *Example: We now have access to more sophisticated technologies.*

- **Figuratively** — In a metaphorical or symbolic way, not literally.
  - *Example: Figuratively, the Lion King is inheriting John's experience from The Jungle Book.*

- **No worries** — Don't worry; everything is fine. An informal, reassuring phrase.
  - *Example: Hakuna Matata — it means no worries.*

- **Impressive** — Causing admiration because of skill, quality, or scale.
  - *Example: It's really impressive how he just sort of holds that all together.*

- **Performer** — Someone who acts, sings, dances, or entertains an audience.
  - *Example: It's nice to have actors who are both the performers and the singers.*

- **Musician** — Someone who plays a musical instrument or composes music professionally.
  - *Example: These are real musicians bringing their creative voices to this.*

- **Inspiration** — The feeling of being motivated or stimulated to do something; something that inspires you.
  - *Example: The inspiration that we're getting from it is universal.*

- **Met Gala** — An annual fundraising event for the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York City, known for its celebrity fashion and red carpet.
  - *Example: The Met Gala for me is exciting because it's one of the only red carpets that I do with my family.*

- **Obviously** — Clearly; in a way that is easy to see or understand.
  - *Example: This year it's Lion King, which obviously I played Nala.*

- **Classic** — Something of lasting quality and recognized excellence; a timeless work.
  - *Example: The Lion King is a classic or timeless film.*

- **Timeless** — Not affected by the passage of time; always relevant.
  - *Example: The Lion King's themes make it a timeless story.*

## 9. Connected Speech — "What's a Motto" vs "What's the Matter"

The joke in the Hakuna Matata scene relies on connected speech:

- **"What's a motto"** — Simba asking about the meaning of the word "motto."
- **"What's the matter"** — A common English expression meaning "What's wrong?" or "What's the problem?"

With natural connected speech, both phrases sound almost identical:

- **What's a motto** → wʌts ə ˈmɑɾoʊ
- **What's the matter** → wʌts ðə ˈmæɾɚ

So Timon responds with **"What's a motto with you?"** — which sounds like **"What's the matter with you?"** — a phrase that can be a genuine question about someone's problem or, if said sarcastically, an insult implying the person is problematic or abnormal.

## 10. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 10.1 The Lion King as a Timeless Classic

- The original Lion King (1994) is described as a story that has "endured" and "remains true to everybody" — meaning its themes of family, responsibility, and identity are universal.
- The cast describes it as having "everything you want from a Disney movie": great music, talented voice actors, a fascinating story, and impressive visuals.

---

### 10.2 Remake Culture

- The 2019 Lion King is a remake of the 1994 classic, similar to how Jon Favreau also remade The Jungle Book (2016).
- Favreau describes "inheriting" his experience from The Jungle Book, using new tools and technology to retell the story.
- The goal was to "capture the spirit" of the original while showing audiences "something new and surprising" — described as "the best combo."

---

### 10.3 Red Carpet Culture

- Beyonce discusses the Met Gala as a family event, highlighting the tradition of red carpet appearances in Western celebrity culture.
- "Red carpet" events are formal occasions where celebrities walk a literal red carpet while being photographed and interviewed.

---

### 10.4 "Grew Up Doing" — Expressing Childhood Habits

- Beyonce says the Lion King is "something I grew up watching," using the common English pattern **"grew up + gerund"** to describe habitual activities from childhood.
- This structure is widely used in everyday English to talk about formative experiences.
  - *Example: I grew up playing soccer every weekend.*
