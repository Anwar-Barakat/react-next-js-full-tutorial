## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Psycho** — Short for "psychotic." An informal way to say someone is crazy or mad. A person considered a psycho might behave in a violent or strange way. Villains in movies often depict a psycho.
  - *Example: I guess they were always scared that I'd be a psycho.*

- **Psyched** — Excited and full of anticipation. Despite looking similar to "psycho," this word has a completely different, positive meaning.
  - *Example: I'm psyched about this, but if I'm gonna mentor you, I need to know you're psyched about this too.*

- **Statement** — Something that you state (say). The collocation "to make a statement" means to show people what you think or believe by behaving in a particular way.
  - *Example: I was ready to make a statement.*

- **Saying** — A short statement or sentence that people commonly say, often expressing a wise or popular idea. Common sayings include "a picture is worth a thousand words" and "actions speak louder than words."
  - *Example: How does the saying go?*

- **Roar** — The loud, deep noise that a lion or similar animal makes. Used figuratively to mean expressing yourself powerfully.
  - *Example: I am woman, hear me roar.*

- **Brilliant** — Extremely clever or skillful.
  - *Example: The thing is, I was born brilliant.*

- **To gauge** — To estimate, judge, or figure out something.
  - *Example: It's been interesting to kind of gauge the tone and how different they are.*
  - *Example: You have to gauge whether they actually are reassured.*

- **Cruel** — Enjoying making people suffer or feel unhappy; causing pain without caring.
  - *Example: Cruella de Vil — she's a cruel devil.*

- **To attain** — A more formal alternative to "achieve" or "accomplish."
  - *Example: It is literally the highest possible honor that a regional salesman can attain.*

- **Unattainable** — Something that cannot be done or achieved. Often used to describe dreams, goals, or standards that are impossible to reach.
  - *Example: I'm not saying your dreams are unattainable, but you can pursue them while you're still gainfully employed.*

## 4. Personality & Character Vocabulary

- **Sweet** — Kind, gentle, and friendly when describing a person.
  - *Example: I like your company. You're sweet.*

- **Feisty** — Having a strong, determined character and being willing to argue with people. Often used as a compliment for someone who stands up for themselves.
  - *Example: She's feisty and smart and is a con artist.*
  - *Example: I've proven what you've done. Wow, you're feisty!*

- **Vim and vigor** — An old-fashioned expression meaning someone has a lot of energy and enthusiasm.
  - *Example: She's full of vim and vigor.*

- **Con artist** — Someone who tricks or deceives people in order to get money from them. Short for "confidence artist."
  - *Example: She's feisty and smart and is a con artist.*
  - *Example: I'm an abstract expressionist. No, you're a con artist.*

- **Devil** — A person who does cruel or evil things. Often collocated with an adjective that specifies a bad trait in someone's character.
  - *Example: She's a lying devil, so be careful.*
  - *Example: You sneaky devil!*

- **Personification** — The representation of an abstract idea or quality as a human character or figure. Emma Stone describes Cruella's two-toned hair as the personification of her dual personality.
  - *Example: It's the personification of those two sides of her hair.*

## 5. Expressions & Idioms

- **Doesn't sit well with (someone)** — You do not agree with it or do not like it. Used when something makes you uncomfortable or unhappy.
  - *Example: That didn't sit well with some people.*
  - *Example: As you can imagine, that didn't sit too well with the captain.*

- **To make a statement** — To show people what you think or believe by behaving in a particular way; to do something bold that draws attention.
  - *Example: I was ready to make a statement.*

- **How does the saying go?** — Used to ask about the words of a saying, song, or story. The verb "go" in this context means "to consist of" or "what are the words."
  - *Example: How does the saying go? I am woman, hear me roar.*
  - *Example: As the song by the Beatles goes, "let it be, let it be."*

- **The thing is** — Used when you are about to explain something, give a reason, or offer an opinion.
  - *Example: The thing is, I was born brilliant.*
  - *Example: The thing is, the shops close early on Sundays.*

- **It's one thing... but it's another** — Used to compare two things where the second is more important, serious, or difficult than the first.
  - *Example: It's one thing to read it on the page, but it's another to really try to do it day in and day out.*
  - *Example: It's one thing to believe in sacrifice, it's another thing to live with it.*

- **Day in and day out** — Continuously or repeatedly over a long period of time; every single day without change.
  - *Example: It's another to really try to do it day in and day out.*
  - *Example: Every day the same thing, day in and day out.*

- **I'm just getting started** — Used to say that you have only begun and there is much more to come; a confident declaration that the best is yet to come.
  - *Example: I'm just getting started, darling.*

## 6. Connected Speech — Assimilation in "Do You Have"

In natural speech, the phrase **"do you have"** undergoes several changes due to connected speech:

**1. Assimilation — "Do you" becomes "d'you":**
- The Y sound in **"you"** changes how it sounds when linked with **"do."**
- **"do you"** → sounds like **"d'you"** or **"dʒuː"** (a "joo" sound)

**2. Reduction — "Have" becomes "'av" or "of":**
- **"have"** is reduced to a weak form, sounding like **"əv"** (similar to "of")

**3. All together:**
- **"Do you have"** → sounds like **"d'you-'av"** /dʒuːəv/
- The three words merge into one smooth sound unit

**Practice:**
- Full form: "Do you have any questions?"
- Connected: "D'you-'av any questions?"

**Tip:** This type of assimilation — where sounds change because of neighboring sounds — is extremely common in English. The D+Y combination creating a J sound is the same pattern seen in "did you" → "didju" and "would you" → "wouldju."

## 7. Grammar — "It's One Thing... But It's Another"

This structure is used to **compare two things** where the second is more important, serious, or difficult than the first:

**Structure:**
- **It's one thing to** + [first action/idea] + **but it's another (thing) to** + [second action/idea]

**From the interview:**
- *"It's one thing to read it on the page, but it's another to really try to do it day in and day out."*
- Emma Stone is saying that reading the script is easy, but actually performing the role every day is much harder.

**More examples:**
- *It's one thing to believe in sacrifice, it's another thing to live with it.*
- *It's one thing to make yourself miserable, but you're making everyone around you miserable too.*

**Shortened version:**
- The expression can be shortened by dropping the second "thing":
- Full: *"It's one thing to say it, it's another thing to show it."*
- Short: *"It's one thing to say it, but it's another to show it."*

**Tip:** This expression is very useful in debates and discussions when you want to point out that something is harder in practice than in theory.

## 8. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 8.1 Cruella — The Movie

- Cruella (2021) is a Disney live-action film that tells the origin story of Cruella de Vil, the iconic villain from 101 Dalmatians.
- Set in 1970s London during the punk rock revolution, the film follows Estella, a clever and creative young woman who dreams of becoming a fashion designer.
- After being taken in by a pair of thieves as a child, Estella eventually catches the eye of the Baroness von Hellman, a fashion legend. Their relationship triggers a series of events that lead Estella to embrace her wicked side and become Cruella.
- The film stars Emma Stone as Cruella/Estella and Emma Thompson as the Baroness. Notably, Emma Stone is an American actress but speaks with a British accent throughout the film.

---

### 8.2 Cruella de Vil — The Name Pun

- The name "Cruella de Vil" is a clever pun (play on words) combining two words:
  - **Cruel** — enjoying making people suffer or feel unhappy
  - **Devil** — a person who does cruel or evil things
- So "Cruella de Vil" sounds like "cruel devil" — a name that perfectly describes the character.
- In English, "devil" is often collocated with an adjective that specifies a bad trait: "sneaky devil," "lying devil," "lucky devil" (this last one can actually be positive — used when someone has unexpected good luck).

---

### 8.3 "I Am Woman, Hear Me Roar"

- The saying Cruella references — "I am woman, hear me roar" — comes from a 1971 song by Helen Reddy called "I Am Woman."
- The song became an anthem for women's empowerment and the feminist movement in the 1970s.
- A "roar" is the powerful noise a lion makes — so "hear me roar" means "listen to my power; take me seriously."
- Cruella uses this saying as a phrase of encouragement, reminding herself (and women in general) that anything is possible.
- The fact that Cruella's story is set in the 1970s — the decade of this song — makes the reference particularly fitting.

---

### 8.4 The Duality of Estella and Cruella

- Emma Stone describes playing the two sides of the character: Estella (the "light" side) and Cruella (the "dark" side).
- She says this duality is the **personification** of Cruella's famous two-toned black-and-white hair — one side represents each personality.
- Importantly, even the "good" side (Estella) isn't a "pure, sweet, unattainably perfect creature." She's "full of vim and vigor," "feisty," "smart," and even "a con artist."
- This complexity is what makes the character interesting: she's not a simple good-versus-evil story. The line between Estella and Cruella is blurry, and the movie explores how much of each exists in the other.
- Emma Stone notes that bringing this to life "day in and day out" was the real challenge — it's one thing to understand a character on paper, but another to perform that duality convincingly every day.
