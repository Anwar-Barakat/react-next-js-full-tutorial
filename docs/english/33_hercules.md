# Learn English with Hercules Guide

A comprehensive English lesson based on scenes from Disney's Hercules (1997), covering vocabulary related to emotions and personality (sentimental, gloomy, stiff), Greek mythology references (Pandora's box, Trojan horse), sarcasm and informal language (gig, sucker, creep, babe), phrasal verbs (lounge about, run off, swear off, leap at), connected speech (pronunciation of "ought to" in American vs British English, T-dropping and H-reduction), and cultural themes about Greek mythology and wordplay.

- **Hades:** How sentimental. You know, I haven't been this choked up since I got a hunk of moussaka caught in my throat. Ha!
- **Hades:** So is this an audience or a mosaic? Hey, how you doing? Looking good, nice dress.
- **Zeus:** So Hades, you finally made it.
- **Hades:** Our things in the Underworld? Well, they're just fine. A little dark, a little gloomy, and there's always, hey, for the dead people, what are you gonna do?
- **Hades:** Oh, there's the little sunspot. Little schnookums. And here is a sucker for the little sucker. Here you go, you're just...

- **Hades:** Love to, babe. But unlike you guys lounging about up here, I regrettably have a full-time gig that you, by the way, so charitably bestowed on me, Zeus. So... can't. Love to, but can't.

## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Sentimental** — Strongly influenced by emotional feelings rather than logic or reason. The opposite of being a rational person.
  - *Example: How sentimental.*
  - *Example: I've already had too much to drink and I'm feeling sentimental.*

- **Choked up** — Unable to speak or almost beginning to cry because of sadness or strong emotion. Different from "choke" (to stop breathing because something is blocking your throat), which is followed by the preposition "on."
  - *Example: I haven't been this choked up since I got a hunk of moussaka caught in my throat.*
  - *Example: Phil, this is great. You're really choked up about this, aren't you?*

- **Hunk** — A large, thick piece, especially of food. We can say a hunk of bread, cheese, or meat.
  - *Example: I got a hunk of moussaka caught in my throat.*

- **Gloomy** — When describing a place: dark and hard to see in. When describing a person: unhappy and without hope.
  - *Example: A little dark, a little gloomy.*
  - *Example: Not a very cheery place. One might say it was rather gloomy.*

- **Sucker** — Informally, a name for a lollipop. Also used to call someone a fool or an idiot.
  - *Example: And here is a sucker for the little sucker.* (a lollipop for the little fool — wordplay)
  - *Example: Do I look like a sucker to you?*

- **Sheesh** — An interjection used to express frustration or annoyance.
  - *Example: Sheesh!* (after baby Hercules squeezes Hades' finger)

- **Tyke** — A small child, especially one who behaves badly.
  - *Example: Yeah, a powerful little tyke.*

- **Stiff** — Formal and controlled in a way that is not friendly or relaxed.
  - *Example: Come on Hades, don't be such a stiff.*
  - *Example: We don't seem to have anything in common. The guy's stiff!*

- **Regrettably** — Used to say that you are sad or sorry about something.
  - *Example: I regrettably have a full-time gig.*

- **Gig** — Informally: a job. If you have a full-time gig, you work for all the hours that are requested, usually 40 hours a week. Also used to describe a single performance by a musician or group of musicians.
  - *Example: I regrettably have a full-time gig.*
  - *Example: Being a regular dog is like the greatest gig in the world, okay?*

- **Charitably** — Comes from "charity." If you do something charitably, your intention is to help someone in need. If you are kind and like to help people, you can be described as charitable.
  - *Example: That you, by the way, so charitably bestowed on me, Zeus.* (Hades is being sarcastic)
  - *Example: People tend to be more charitable at Christmas time.*

- **Bestow** — To give valuable property or an important right or honor to someone. Often followed by "on" or "upon."
  - *Example: So charitably bestowed on me, Zeus.*
  - *Example: It is with great honor and humble heart that I bestow this pot of honey upon our dear friend.*

- **Weakness** — The fact or state of not being strong or powerful. The opposite of strength.
  - *Example: He's got to have a weakness because everybody's got a weakness.*
  - *Example: The true path to victory is to find your opponent's weakness and make him suffer for it.*

- **Creep** — An unpleasant person; someone who makes you feel uncomfortable.
  - *Example: And how does this creep thank you?*

- **Babe** — Used informally to describe an attractive person.
  - *Example: By running off with some babe.*
  - *Example: Couldn't have done it without you, sugar. You're one hot babe.*

## 4. Greek Mythology & Cultural References

- **Pandora's Box** — An artifact in Greek mythology. Curiosity led Pandora to open a container she was told not to, consequently releasing physical and emotional curses upon humanity. As an expression: if you open a Pandora's box, you do or start something that will cause a lot of problems.
  - *Example: Pandora was the box thing.*

- **Trojan Horse** — The wooden horse used by the Greeks during the Trojan War to enter the city of Troy and win the war. As an expression: a Trojan horse refers to something you accept because it seems good, but is actually intended to do harm.
  - *Example: For the Trojans — hey, they bet on the wrong horse, okay?*

- **Moussaka** — A Greek dish consisting of layers of potato, aubergine (eggplant), minced meat, and a bechamel sauce on top.
  - *Example: I got a hunk of moussaka caught in my throat.*

- **The Underworld** — In Greek mythology, the place where souls go after death. Hades is the god who rules over it.
  - *Example: Our things in the Underworld? Well, they're just fine.*

- **Wonder Boy / Wonder Woman** — Megara refers to Hercules as "Wonder Boy" because he is successful and has extraordinary abilities, similar to the superhero Wonder Woman.
  - *Example: Wonder Boy's hitting every curve you throw at him.*

## 5. Expressions & Idioms

- **Make it** — To complete something successfully; to arrive or succeed.
  - *Example: So Hades, you finally made it.*
  - *Example: We made it!*

- **Lounge about / around** — To spend time relaxing or doing nothing.
  - *Example: Unlike you guys lounging about up here.*

- **Work yourself to death** — To work to the point of exhaustion. Zeus is also making a joke because Hades is the god of the dead.
  - *Example: You work yourself to death!*
  - *Example: So you'll just work us to death? We'll sure try.*

- **Throw someone a curveball** — From baseball: to surprise someone with something that is difficult or unpleasant to deal with. When Megara says Hercules is "hitting every curve," she means he has been able to combat all of Hades' attempts.
  - *Example: Wonder Boy's hitting every curve you throw at him.*
  - *Example: Sometimes life throws a curve at you.*

- **Don't even go there** — A warning to someone not to bring up a particular topic; a firm refusal to discuss something.
  - *Example: Don't even go there.*

- **Bet on the wrong horse** — To make a bad choice or support the wrong person or idea.
  - *Example: For the Trojans — hey, they bet on the wrong horse.*

- **Do your part** — To do something that helps to achieve a goal; to contribute your share.
  - *Example: I've done my part. Get your little imps.*
  - *Example: If we all stick together and we do our part, we can win this thing.*

- **Handle** — To have the ability to deal with a problem, situation, or person successfully.
  - *Example: I need somebody who can handle him as a man.*
  - *Example: This is Lightning McQueen. I can handle anything.*

- **Swear off** — To promise to stop doing or using something.
  - *Example: Hey, I've sworn off man handling.*
  - *Example: Ann decided to swear off social media because it was consuming too much of her time.*

- **In a jam** — In a difficult situation. Common collocations: "be in a jam" and "get out of a jam."
  - *Example: That's what got you into this jam in the first place.*
  - *Example: I was in a jam after losing all my money on a bet.*

- **Sell your soul** — To do anything, even something wrong or dishonest, in order to get something you want.
  - *Example: You sold your soul to me to save your boyfriend's life.*

- **Run off** — A phrasal verb meaning to leave a place unexpectedly.
  - *Example: By running off with some babe.*
  - *Example: So what are you going to do? Just go running off to the wild by yourself?*

- **Learn your lesson** — To acquire a great understanding of a situation by going through difficult experiences.
  - *Example: Look, I learned my lesson, okay?*
  - *Example: I learned an important lesson. You can't count on anyone, especially your heroes.*

- **Leap at** — To enthusiastically accept the chance to do or have something.
  - *Example: I got a feeling you're going to leap at my new offer.*
  - *Example: She leaped at the offer for a better job.*

## 6. Connected Speech — "Ought to" & T-Dropping

**1. "Ought to" — American vs British Pronunciation:**

- The modal verb **"ought to"** (meaning the same as "should") is pronounced differently in American and British English.
- "Ought" ends with a T and "to" also starts with a T. When this happens, we tend to pronounce only **one consonant sound** to facilitate linking with the next word.

- **American English:** Instead of "ought-to," Americans say **"oughta"** with a **flap T** (sounds like a soft D).
  - *Example: You oughta slow down.* → sounds like "you aw-da slow down"
  - *Example: All joking aside, you really ought to be yourself.* (American)

- **British English:** You will hear a **true T** or a **glottal T**.
  - *Example: Those little sticklers think this bit of rubbish ought to do.* (British)

**2. "Didn't he" — T-Dropping and H-Reduction:**

- When connecting "didn't" with "he," two changes occur:
  - The **T sound is dropped** from "didn't"
  - The **H sound in "he" is reduced** to just the vowel "e"
- **"Didn't he"** → sounds like **"didn'-ee"**
  - *Example: You're hurt real bad, didn't he, Meg?* → sounds like "didn'-ee, Meg?"
  - *Example: He had you, didn't he?*

**Tip:** These connected speech features — consonant merging with "ought to" and T-dropping with H-reduction — are extremely common in natural English. Recognizing them will dramatically improve your listening comprehension.

## 7. Grammar — Interjections

**Interjections** are words or short phrases that express sudden feelings or reactions. They don't follow normal grammar rules and are usually followed by an exclamation point.

**From the movie:**

- **Sheesh!** — Expresses frustration or annoyance.
  - *Example: Sheesh!* (Hades, after baby Hercules squeezes his finger)

**Other common interjections and their functions:**

- **Wow!** — Expresses amazement or admiration.
  - *Example: Everything the light touches is our kingdom. — Wow.*

- **Ugh!** — Expresses disgust or annoyance.
  - *Example: Reading these cool things. I got to go to work. Life is so unfair. Ugh.*

- **Yay! / Woo!** — Expresses excitement or celebration.
  - *Example: We did it!*

- **Hmm** — Expresses thinking or hesitation.

- **Oops** — Expresses recognition of a small mistake.

- **Phew** — Expresses relief.

**Tip:** Interjections are one of the most natural parts of spoken English. Using them correctly makes your English sound more authentic and conversational. The key is matching the interjection to the emotion — "sheesh" for frustration is very different from "wow" for admiration.

## 8. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 8.1 Hercules — The Movie

- Hercules (1997) is a Disney animated film based on the legendary hero Heracles from Greek mythology (Hercules is the Roman name).
- The story follows Hercules, the son of Zeus and Hera, who is turned mortal by Hades' minions but retains his godlike strength.
- Raised by mortal parents on Earth, Hercules must prove himself a "true hero" to regain his place among the gods on Mount Olympus.
- The film is known for its humor, gospel-inspired musical numbers, and the character of Hades — one of Disney's most sarcastic and entertaining villains, voiced by James Woods.

---

### 8.2 Greek Mythology — Zeus, Hera, Hades & Mount Olympus

- **Zeus** is the sky and thunder god who rules as king of the gods of Mount Olympus. In the movie, he is Hercules' father.
- **Hera** is the goddess of women and family, and the sister and wife of Zeus. In the movie, she is Hercules' mother (though in actual mythology, Hercules' mother was the mortal Alcmene).
- **Hades** is the god of the Underworld — the realm of the dead. In the movie, he is Zeus' brother and the main villain who wants to overthrow Zeus and rule Mount Olympus.
- **Mount Olympus** is the highest mountain in Greece and famous in mythology for being the home of the Greek gods.
- The movie takes creative liberties with these myths for storytelling purposes, but the core characters and settings are rooted in ancient Greek tradition.

---

### 8.3 Pandora's Box & the Trojan Horse

- **Pandora's Box:** In Greek mythology, Pandora was the first human woman created by the gods. She was given a box (actually a jar in the original myth) and told never to open it. Curiosity got the better of her, and when she opened it, all the evils of the world escaped — leaving only Hope inside. Today, "opening a Pandora's box" means starting something that causes many unforeseen problems.
- **The Trojan Horse:** During the Trojan War, the Greeks built a giant wooden horse and hid soldiers inside it. The Trojans brought the horse into their city as a trophy, and at night the Greek soldiers emerged and opened the gates for the Greek army. Today, a "Trojan horse" refers to something that appears harmless or beneficial but is actually designed to cause harm (this is also the origin of the computer term "Trojan" for malicious software).
- Hades references both of these when listing ways that people have been defeated: "Pandora was the box thing, for the Trojans — hey, they bet on the wrong horse."

---

### 8.4 Hades' Sarcasm & Wordplay

- Hades is one of Disney's most verbally clever villains. Almost everything he says contains sarcasm, puns, or double meanings:
  - **"Choked up" pun:** He says he hasn't been this "choked up" (emotionally moved) since he got food stuck in his throat (literally choked) — mocking the emotional moment.
  - **"Sucker" double meaning:** He gives Hercules "a sucker for the little sucker" — a lollipop for the little fool.
  - **"Work yourself to death" irony:** Zeus tells Hades to slow down or he'll "work himself to death." Since Hades is the god of the dead, this is unintentionally hilarious, and Hades' response — "I kill myself!" — adds another layer of death-related humor.
  - **"Charitably bestowed":** Hades sarcastically thanks Zeus for "charitably" giving him the job of ruling the Underworld — a job he clearly hates.
  - **Meg's "sworn off man handling":** When Hades says he needs someone who can "handle" Hercules "as a man," Meg responds with "I've sworn off man handling" — a pun on "manhandling" (treating someone roughly) and literally "handling a man."
- This style of rapid-fire sarcasm and wordplay is very characteristic of American comedy and is excellent material for advanced English learners to study.
