## 1. Scene 1 — The Fairy Godmother

- **Cinderella:** My dress. I can't go in this dress.
- **Fairy Godmother:** Can you mend it? Mend it. You know, I'll turn it into something new.
- **Cinderella:** Oh no, please don't. This was my mother's, and I'd like to wear it when I go to the palace. It's almost like taking her with me.
- **Fairy Godmother:** I understand. But she wouldn't mind if I... She— get off of me! Wouldn't mind a nice blue.

## 2. Scene 2 — The Stepmother Forbids Cinderella

- **Cinderella:** I don't want to ruin anything. I don't even want to meet the prince.
- **Stepmother:** Oh, and you won't, because there's no question of you're going.
- **Cinderella:** But all of the maidens of the land are invited by order of the king.
- **Stepmother:** It is the king I am thinking of. It would be an insult to the royal personage to take you to the palace dressed in these old rags.
- **Cinderella:** Rags? This was my mother's.
- **Stepmother:** Oh, I'm sorry to have to tell you, but your mother's taste was questionable. This thing is so old-fashioned. It's practically falling to pieces. Oh, the shoulder's frayed. Oh, it's falling. It's a ridiculous, old-fashioned joke.
- **Cinderella:** How could you?
- **Stepmother:** How could I otherwise? I will not have anyone associate my daughters with you. It would ruin their prospects to be seen arriving with a ragged servant girl, because that is what you are and that is what you will always be. Now mark my words, you shall not go to the ball.

## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Mend** — To repair something that is damaged or broken.
  - *Example: Can you mend it? I'll turn it into something new.*

- **Maiden** — An unmarried young woman. A somewhat old-fashioned or literary word.
  - *Example: All of the maidens of the land are invited by order of the king.*

- **Personage** — A distinct, famous, or important person. A formal word used to show respect for someone's status.
  - *Example: It would be an insult to the royal personage to take you to the palace.*

- **Rags** — Old, torn, and worn-out clothes.
  - *Example: It would be an insult to take you to the palace dressed in these old rags.*

- **Questionable** — Doubtful; not socially acceptable or appropriate. When applied to "taste," it means the things a person likes or wears are strange or not widely approved.
  - *Example: Your mother's taste was questionable.*

- **Old-fashioned** — Not contemporary; not fashionable in modern society. Can also describe someone who holds outdated ideas or beliefs.
  - *Example: This thing is so old-fashioned.*

- **Frayed** — Describes clothes or fabric that are worn at the edges, especially if old or used many times. The threads become loose and unraveled.
  - *Example: Oh, the shoulder's frayed.*

- **Ruin** — To destroy or severely damage something. Can refer to physical destruction or damage to someone's reputation or chances.
  - *Example: It would ruin their prospects to be seen arriving with a ragged servant girl.*

- **Prospects** — The chances or possibilities of future success or advancement.
  - *Example: It would ruin their prospects to be seen arriving with a ragged servant girl.*

- **Ragged** — The adjective form of "rags"; wearing old, torn clothes; looking untidy or worn out.
  - *Example: A ragged servant girl.*

- **Servant** — Someone who does domestic services for another person. Not a common word in modern English — nowadays people say "attendants" or "domestic workers."
  - *Example: That is what you are — a servant girl.*

- **Ball** — A large, formal social gathering for dancing. Different from a regular party in that it is elegant and often associated with royalty or high society.
  - *Example: You shall not go to the ball.*

- **Dowry** — An amount of money or property paid by the bride's family to the prospective husband prior to marriage. This was and still is customary in some cultures.
  - *Example: I have no carriage, no parents, no dowry.*

- **Slippers** — Easy-to-wear shoes, often for indoor use. In the Cinderella story, refers to the iconic glass shoes.
  - *Example: Cinderella's glass slippers are an important part of the story.*

- **Posh** — Elegant, stylish, or luxuriously upper-class. Can describe people, places, accents, or behavior.
  - *Example: They speak in a quite posh way.*

- **Country girl** — A girl from a rural, pastoral area, away from big cities. Implies simplicity and lack of sophistication.
  - *Example: An honest country girl.*

- **Trade** — A job that demands special training and practical skills, such as carpentry, plumbing, or electrical work.
  - *Example: Still learning his trade.*

- **Apprentice** — A person who is still learning the skills to perform a certain job from a more experienced person.
  - *Example: An apprentice still learning his trade.*

## 4. Social Status & Appearance Vocabulary

- **Orphan** — A child whose parents have died.
  - *Example: Cinderella tells the story of an orphan girl.*

- **Stepmother / Stepsisters** — A stepmother is the woman who marries your father after your biological mother has died or left. Stepsisters are the daughters of your stepmother from a previous relationship.
  - *Example: She is made a servant by her stepmother and stepsisters.*

- **Kingdom** — A country or territory ruled by a king or queen.
  - *Example: The king invites all the single ladies in the kingdom.*

- **Royalty** — People of royal blood; the king, queen, prince, princess, and their family.
  - *Example: Her daughters' chances of becoming a part of the royalty.*

- **Palace** — The official residence of a king, queen, or other royal person.
  - *Example: It would be an insult to the royal personage to take you to the palace.*

- **Prince** — The son of a king or queen.
  - *Example: The prince can choose one to be his wife.*

- **Insult** — A disrespectful or offensive remark or action.
  - *Example: It would be an insult to the royal personage.*

- **Late (mother)** — A respectful way of referring to someone who has recently died. "Her late mother" means her mother who has passed away.
  - *Example: Cinderella fixes an old dress of her late mother.*

## 5. Expressions & Idioms

- **From rags to riches** — An expression describing someone who grew up in poverty and now has great wealth. Cinderella's story is the classic "rags to riches" tale.
  - *Example: Cinderella is a rags to riches tale of a servant girl who becomes a princess.*

- **Falling to pieces / Falling to bits** — Describes something that is being physically ruined or falling apart. Can also describe a person who is emotionally unstable or very upset.
  - *Example: It's practically falling to pieces.*
  - *Example: After the breakup, she was falling to pieces.*

- **How could you?** — A phrase used to express surprise or strong disapproval of another person's behavior or actions.
  - *Example: How could you? (said after the stepmother destroys her dress)*

- **Mark my words** — An expression used when you want someone to pay close attention and remember what you are saying. Often used as a warning or a strong prediction.
  - *Example: Now mark my words, you shall not go to the ball.*

- **No question of** — There is no chance or possibility of something happening; it is not even up for discussion.
  - *Example: There's no question of you're going.*

- **Take me as I am** — Accept me for who I am, including my flaws and qualities. Often used in the context of romantic relationships.
  - *Example: Will you take me as I am?*

- **Associate with (someone)** — To be connected to and have a relationship with that person, whether through family, friendship, or business.
  - *Example: I will not have anyone associate my daughters with you.*

## 6. Connected Speech — Flap T & Linking

**1. "But only" — Flap T before a vowel:**
- When **"but"** is followed by a word starting with a vowel (like "only"), the T in "but" often becomes a **soft D sound** (flap T) in American and Australian English.
- **"but only"** → sounds like **"bud-only"** /bʌɾoʊnli/
- This happens because the T falls between two vowel sounds.

**2. "As I" — S becomes Z and links:**
- The S in **"as"** is pronounced as a **Z sound** /z/ and connects directly to **"I"**.
- **"as I"** → sounds like **"az-I"** /æzaɪ/
- **Warning:** Be careful with the pronunciation of "as" — mispronouncing the S can lead to confusion with a different, inappropriate word.

**3. General linking pattern — Consonant to vowel:**
- When a word ends in a consonant and the next word starts with a vowel, the sounds link together smoothly.
- **"take me as I am"** → "take-me-az-I-am" (flows as one connected phrase)

## 7. Connected Speech — "Do Not" vs "Don't"

In older or more formal settings (like the Cinderella movie, which is set in a historical period), characters clearly articulate **"do not"** as two separate words. In modern fast speech, this is almost always contracted:

**Formal / Old-fashioned:**
- *"I do not wish to go."* — Each word is clearly pronounced.

**Modern / Casual:**
- *"I don't wish to go."* — Contracted to "don't."
- In American English, the T in "don't" is often dropped or barely pronounced, and the N sound connects to the next word.
- **"don't"** → sounds like /doʊn/ (the T is almost silent)

**From the movie:**
- *"You shall not go to the ball."* — "Shall not" is fully articulated for emphasis and formality.
- In modern casual speech, this would be: *"You shan't go"* (rare) or more likely *"You're not going to the ball."*

**Tip:** Recognizing the difference between formal uncontracted speech and modern contracted speech helps you understand both period dramas and everyday conversation.

## 8. Grammar — "Shall" for Formal Certainty

**"Shall"** is a formal and somewhat old-fashioned modal verb used to express that something will certainly or must happen. It carries more weight and authority than "will."

**From the movie:**
- *"You shall not go to the ball."* — The stepmother uses "shall not" to make an absolute, authoritative declaration.

**Modern usage:**
- "Shall" is rarely used in everyday American English but is still common in British English for suggestions and offers.
- **Formal/emphatic:** *"I shall do it."* (I will definitely do it.)
- **Suggestion (British):** *"Shall we go?"* (Should we go?)
- **Legal language:** *"The tenant shall pay rent on the first of each month."*

**Famous examples:**
- *"You shall not pass!"* (Gandalf, The Lord of the Rings)
- *"You shall not go to the ball."* (Cinderella's stepmother)

**Tip:** When you hear "shall" in movies, it often signals a formal, old-fashioned, or authoritative context. In modern conversation, "will" or "going to" are used instead.

## 9. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 9.1 Cinderella — The Movie

- Cinderella is one of the most well-known fairy tales in the world, with versions in many cultures dating back centuries.
- Disney first adapted the story as an animated film in 1950, which became a classic.
- In 2015, Disney released a live-action version starring Lily James as Cinderella and Cate Blanchett as the stepmother.
- The story follows an orphan girl who is made a servant by her cruel stepmother. When the king invites all the maidens in the kingdom to a ball so the prince can choose a wife, Cinderella's stepmother forbids her from going. With help from her fairy godmother and the house mice, Cinderella attends the ball, dances with the prince, but must leave before midnight — leaving behind only a glass slipper.

---

### 9.2 From Rags to Riches

- Cinderella's story is the quintessential "rags to riches" tale — a servant girl who becomes a princess.
- The expression "from rags to riches" is widely used in English to describe anyone who goes from poverty to wealth or from obscurity to fame.
- The word "rags" (old, torn clothes) represents poverty, while "riches" represents wealth — the contrast captures a dramatic transformation.
- Many real-life stories are described as "rags to riches" — from entrepreneurs who started with nothing to celebrities who grew up poor.

---

### 9.3 The Dowry System

- A "dowry" is money, property, or goods that the bride's family gives to the groom or his family before or at the time of marriage.
- This practice has existed in many cultures throughout history, including European, South Asian, and Middle Eastern societies.
- In the Cinderella story, Cinderella mentions having "no dowry" — meaning she has nothing of material value to offer in marriage, which would have been a serious social disadvantage in the historical period depicted.
- In many countries today, dowry practices have been banned or are declining, but they still exist in some cultures.

---

### 9.4 Posh English and Old-Fashioned Language

- The characters in Cinderella speak in a "posh" way — using formal, elegant English that reflects the aristocratic setting.
- Words like "shall," "maiden," "personage," and phrases like "mark my words" are old-fashioned and not commonly used in everyday modern English.
- The stepmother's language is deliberately formal and cutting — she uses sophisticated vocabulary ("questionable taste," "insult to the royal personage") to make Cinderella feel inferior.
- The contrast between the formal language of the palace and modern English is one reason period dramas are valuable for English learners — they expose you to a wider range of vocabulary and registers.
