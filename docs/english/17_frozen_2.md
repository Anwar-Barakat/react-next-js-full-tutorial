## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **To whisper** — To speak very quietly, using your breath instead of your normal voice. You might whisper when telling someone a secret.
  - *Example: Ignore your whispers, which I wish would go away.*

- **Ringing in your ear** — A high-frequency sound that appears after being exposed to a loud noise, such as an explosion or concert speakers. Only you can hear it.
  - *Example: It's just a ringing in my ear.*

- **Spoken for** — Unavailable; already committed. Can mean someone is in a relationship (not single) or is committed to something else.
  - *Example: And if I heard you, which I don't — I'm spoken for, I fear.*

- **I fear** — A collocation meaning "I'm sorry about" or "I'm afraid that." Different from the common meaning of being scared.
  - *Example: I fear I will be a few minutes late.*
  - *Example: I'm spoken for, I fear.*

- **Within** — Another way of saying "inside."
  - *Example: Everyone I've ever loved is here within these walls.*

- **Siren** — In mythology, a creature that used her singing to attract sailors into dangerous waters, similar to a mermaid. Figuratively: something tempting that leads to trouble.
  - *Example: I'm sorry, secret siren, but I'm blocking out your calls.*

- **To risk** — To expose yourself to danger or to the possibility of losing something important. Similar to gambling money at a casino.
  - *Example: I am afraid of what I'm risking if I follow you.*
  - *Example: You cannot risk going out there again!*

- **Risky** — Involving the possibility of danger or loss.
  - *Example: Following the mysterious voice into the unknown is very risky.*

- **The unknown** — A place or situation that has not been explored; something unfamiliar or mysterious.
  - *Example: Elsa follows the voice into the unknown without knowing what she will find.*

- **To lose (someone)** — In this context, to experience someone's death. Not about being unable to find them.
  - *Example: I can't lose you, Elsa.*

- **To rise / Rising** — To move from a lower position to a higher one. Figuratively: to increase in intensity.
  - *Example: I'm sensing rising anger in me.*

- **Mad** — In American English, commonly used instead of "angry."
  - *Example: You have every right to be very, very mad at her.*

- **To sense / Sensing** — To feel or detect something without direct evidence; to have an intuition about something.
  - *Example: I'm sensing surprising anger.*

## 4. Phrasal Verbs

- **Go about** — To do something as you would normally do; to continue with your regular activities.
  - *Example: There's a thousand reasons I should go about my day.*
  - *Example: This may take a while — go about your business.*

- **Go away** — To leave; to disappear.
  - *Example: I wish your whispers would go away.*

- **Block out** — To stop light, sound, or thoughts from reaching you.
  - *Example: I'm sorry, secret siren, but I'm blocking out your calls.*
  - *Example: Install better curtains to block out the morning sun.*

- **Push (someone) away** — To reject someone's help or attention; to distance yourself from someone emotionally.
  - *Example: Elsa pushed me away too, and didn't even say goodbye.*

- **Hang on** — Two meanings: (1) to wait, (2) to hold tightly to something.
  - *Example: I'm holding on tight to you.*

- **Come on** — Multiple uses. In this scene, it expresses anger or frustration.
  - *Example: Come on, Anna!*

## 5. Expressions & Idioms

- **Give me a hand** — An idiom meaning "help me." Also used literally in the movie for humor.
  - *Example: Olaf, help me! Stop! Give me a hand!*

- **Have every right to** — To have a good reason to feel a certain way or do something.
  - *Example: You have every right to be very, very mad at her.*
  - *Example: You have every right to be sad.*

- **Have no right** — The opposite of "have every right to"; meaning you have no valid reason or authority.
  - *Example: You have no right to enter that mountain.*

- **Done nothing but (change)** — Means "only." Used to emphasize that something has been happening continuously.
  - *Example: Everything's done nothing but change.* (= Everything has only changed.)

- **Holding your hand** — Figuratively: giving someone emotional support. Also used literally in the movie for humor.
  - *Example: I'm still here holding your hand.*

- **I'm spoken for** — I'm unavailable; I'm already committed (to someone or something).
  - *Example: I'm spoken for, I fear.*

## 6. Puns & Double Meanings

- **"Give me a hand"** — Anna says this meaning "help me," but Olaf responds by literally detaching his hand and giving it to her. The humor comes from taking the figurative idiom literally.
  - *Example: Olaf, help me! Give me a hand!*

- **"I'm still here holding your hand"** — Anna says this meaning "I'm here supporting you" (figurative), but she is literally holding Olaf's detached hand. The same literal-vs-figurative wordplay as above.
  - *Example: I know, but look — I'm still here holding your hand.*

## 7. Connected Speech — W-Insertion Between Words

When a word ends in an **"o"** or **"u"** sound and the following word begins with a **vowel sound**, native speakers often insert a **W sound** /w/ to connect them smoothly.

**From the movie — "go away":**
- **go away** → /ɡoʊwəweɪ/ ("go-w-away")

**More examples:**
- **you always** → /juːwɔːlweɪz/ ("you-w-always")
- **who is it** → /huːwɪzɪt/ ("who-w-is-it")
- **do it** → /duːwɪt/ ("do-w-it")
- **so excited** → /soʊwɪkˈsaɪtɪd/ ("so-w-excited")

**Why this happens:**
- English speakers naturally avoid having two vowel sounds next to each other without a consonant between them.
- The W sound acts as a smooth bridge between the two vowels.

## 8. Connected Speech — T+Y to CH and D+Y to J

**1. T + Y → CH sound /tʃ/:**
When a word ending in **T** is followed by a word starting with **Y** (or "you/your"), the sounds merge into **CH**.

**From the movie:**
- **"out your"** → "ou-chur" — *"I'm blocking out your calls"* sounds like *"blocking ou-chur calls"*

**More examples:**
- **can't you** → "can'tchu"
- **won't you** → "won'tchu"
- **what you** → "whatchu"
- **got you** → "gotcha"

**2. D + Y → J sound /dʒ/:**
When a word ending in **D** is followed by a word starting with **Y**, the sounds merge into **J**.

**From the movie:**
- **"heard you"** → "hear-ju" — *"And if I heard you, which I don't"* sounds like *"heard-ju"*

**More examples:**
- **would you** → "would-ju"
- **did you** → "did-ju"
- **could you** → "could-ju"
- **had your** → "had-jur"

## 9. Connected Speech — Phrasal Verb Stress

Phrasal verbs consist of a **verb** (content word) and a **particle** (function word). Natives **stress the verb** and **de-stress the particle**, connecting them into one smooth sound.

**From the movie — "come on":**
- Stress **"COME"**, de-stress **"on"** → sounds like /kʌmɑn/ ("cuh-mon")

**Why this matters:**
- Over-stressing function words like particles makes your speech sound unnatural.
- Connecting the verb and particle into one smooth sound makes you sound more fluent.

**More examples:**
- **give up** → stress "GIVE," de-stress "up"
- **hang on** → stress "HANG," de-stress "on"
- **block out** → stress "BLOCK," de-stress "out"
- **push away** → stress "PUSH," de-stress "away"

## 10. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 10.1 Frozen 2 — The Movie

- Frozen 2 (2019) is the sequel to Disney's Frozen (2013).
- The story follows Elsa as she hears a mysterious voice calling to her from the north, leading her on a journey to discover the origin of her magical powers.
- Anna, Kristoff, Olaf, and Sven join her on the adventure, which takes them into an enchanted forest beyond Arendelle.
- The film explores themes of self-discovery, change, and the courage to face the unknown.

---

### 10.2 The Voice and The Unknown

- The theme song "Into the Unknown" captures Elsa's internal conflict — she hears a mysterious voice but is afraid to follow it.
- She calls the voice a "secret siren," comparing it to the mythological creatures that lured sailors to danger with their beautiful singing.
- She lists all the reasons she should stay: her loved ones are "within these walls," she's "spoken for," and she's afraid of what she's "risking."
- Despite all this resistance, Elsa eventually gives in and follows the voice "into the unknown" — a metaphor for embracing change and facing your fears.

---

### 10.3 "Mad" vs "Angry" in American English

- In American English, **"mad"** is commonly used as a synonym for **"angry"** in everyday conversation.
- In British English, "mad" more often means "crazy" or "insane."
- Anna says: *"You have every right to be very, very mad at her"* — using "mad" in the American sense of "angry."
- Both words are perfectly acceptable, but "mad" is more informal and casual.

---

### 10.4 Olaf's Literal Humor

- Throughout the Frozen franchise, much of Olaf's humor comes from taking **figurative expressions literally**.
- In this scene, two puns play on this:
  - **"Give me a hand"** — Anna asks for help, but Olaf literally gives her his detachable snowman hand.
  - **"Holding your hand"** — Anna says this to express emotional support, but she's literally holding Olaf's detached hand.
- This is consistent with Olaf's character — he's innocent, literal-minded, and often misunderstands idioms and metaphors, creating humor for both children and adults.
