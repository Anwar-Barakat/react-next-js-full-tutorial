# Learn English with Big Hero 6 Guide

A comprehensive English lesson based on scenes from Disney's Big Hero 6 (2014), covering vocabulary related to healthcare and the body (distress, stub your toe, injuries, sustain injuries), emotional and developmental terms (mood, mood swings, adolescence, puberty), formal and investigative vocabulary (apprehend, assess, high profile), collocations with "injury," connected speech (reducing "didn't," dropping T in "don't even know," silent H in "annihilator," multiple reductions in "rules don't apply to"), and cultural themes about robotics, San Fransokyo, and comic book storytelling.

## 1. Scene 1 — Hiro Meets Baymax

- **Baymax:** Hello. I am Baymax, your personal healthcare companion.
- **Hiro:** Uh, hey. Uh, Baymax, I didn't know you were still active.
- **Baymax:** I heard a sound of distress. What seems to be the trouble?
- **Hiro:** Oh, I just stubbed my toe a little. I'm fine.
- **Baymax:** On a scale of 1 to 10, how would you rate your pain?
- **Hiro:** A zero. I'm okay, really. Thanks. You can shrink now.
- **Baymax:** Does it hurt when I touch it?
- **Hiro:** Yeah, it's okay. No, no touching.
- **Hiro:** (falls) Ah!
- **Baymax:** You have fallen.
- **Hiro:** You think?
- **Baymax:** On a scale of one to ten, how would you rate your pain?
- **Hiro:** Zero.
- **Baymax:** It is all right to cry.
- **Hiro:** No, no crying.
- **Baymax:** Crying is a natural response to pain.
- **Hiro:** I'm not crying.
- **Baymax:** I will scan you for injuries.
- **Hiro:** Don't scan me.
- **Baymax:** Scan complete. Unbelievable. You have sustained no injuries. However, your hormone and neurotransmitter levels indicate that you are experiencing mood swings common in adolescence. Diagnosis: puberty.
- **Hiro:** Whoa, what?!

## 2. Scene 2 — Identifying the Masked Villain

- **Fred:** The guy in the mask was carrying something with this symbol on it.
- **Baymax:** Apprehending the man in the mask will improve Hiro's emotional state.
- **Hiro:** Apprehend him?
- **Fred:** We don't even know who he is. I have a theory.
- **Fred:** Dr. Slaughter, M.D. — actually millionaire weapons designer Malcolm Chazeltick. The Annihilator — behind the mask, industrialist Reid Axworthy. Baron Von Destruct—
- **Hiro:** Oh, just get to the point!
- **Hiro:** Don't you guys get it? The man in the mask who attacked us is none other than — voilà — Alistair Krei.
- **Hiro:** What? Think about it. Krei wanted your microbots, and you said no.
- **Fred:** The rules don't apply to a man like Krei.
- **Wasabi:** There's no way. The guy's too high profile.
- **GoGo:** And who was that guy in the mask?
- **Hiro:** I don't know. We don't know anything about him.
- **Baymax:** His blood type is AB negative. Cholesterol levels—
- **Hiro:** Baymax, you scanned him?
- **Baymax:** I am programmed to assess everyone's healthcare needs.
- **Hiro:** Yes! I can use the data from your scan to find him.

## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Distress** — A feeling of extreme worry, sadness, or pain. Baymax uses this word because he detected Hiro complaining about his pain.
  - *Example: I heard a sound of distress. What seems to be the trouble?*
  - *Example: I regret any distress I may have caused.*

- **Rate** — To give something a particular value or position on a scale or in a list; to rank something.
  - *Example: On a scale of 1 to 10, how would you rate your pain?*
  - *Example: This restaurant has a five-star rating.*
  - *Example: His performance was rated as good by the evaluators.*

- **Shrink** — To reduce in size; to become smaller. When Baymax is deactivated, his size shrinks because he is an inflatable robot.
  - *Example: I'm okay, really. Thanks. You can shrink now.*
  - *Example: Be careful when you use the washing machine — your clothes might shrink.*

- **Apprehend** — A formal word meaning to arrest or capture someone for not obeying the law. Synonyms: arrest, detain, capture.
  - *Example: Apprehending the man in the mask will improve Hiro's emotional state.*
  - *Example: Let's go apprehend him.*

- **High profile** — Describing a person who attracts a lot of attention from the public and the media.
  - *Example: There's no way. The guy's too high profile.*
  - *Example: We just need somebody high profile to take Dalton's place in an NFL pregame interview.*

- **Assess** — To evaluate something; to judge or determine the nature, quality, or importance of something.
  - *Example: I am programmed to assess everyone's healthcare needs.*
  - *Example: The teacher made a test to assess the students' performance.*
  - *Example: She would assess her situation, then she'd evaluate, and she would analyze her options.*

## 4. Healthcare & Body Vocabulary

- **Healthcare companion** — A person or robot whose purpose is to help take care of someone's health. Baymax introduces himself as Hiro's "personal healthcare companion."
  - *Example: Hello. I am Baymax, your personal healthcare companion.*

- **Stub your toe** — To hurt your toe by accidentally hitting it against something hard.
  - *Example: Oh, I just stubbed my toe a little. I'm fine.*
  - *Example: He stubbed his toe on the revolving door on the way in.*

- **Injury** — Physical harm or damage to the body. This is a useful alternative to the word "hurt."
  - *Example: I will scan you for injuries.*
  - *Example: The player had to leave the game early because he got injured.*

- **Mood** — Your emotional state at a particular time. When you're happy, you are in a good mood. When you're sad or angry, you are in a bad mood.
  - *Example: Your hormone and neurotransmitter levels indicate that you are experiencing mood swings.*

- **Mood swings** — Constant, often unpredictable changes in a person's mood — from happy to sad, from calm to angry.
  - *Example: You are experiencing mood swings common in adolescence.*
  - *Example: Side effects may cause dry mouth, mood swings, and nighttime drowsiness.*

- **Adolescence** — The period in someone's life when they are a teenager. It's common for teenagers to experience mood swings because of the development of their bodies and minds.
  - *Example: Mood swings common in adolescence.*

- **Puberty** — The period in a child's life when their body starts changing in order for them to become an adult. Baymax humorously diagnoses Hiro's puberty as if it were a medical condition.
  - *Example: Diagnosis: puberty.*

## 5. Collocations with "Injury"

Collocations are groups or combinations of words that frequently go together in English. The word **"injury"** has several common collocations that all communicate the same basic idea — to get hurt:

- **Sustain injuries** — A formal way to say someone has been injured.
  - *Example: You have sustained no injuries.*

- **Suffer from injuries** — To experience pain or harm from injuries.
  - *Example: The athlete suffered from injuries during the match.*

- **Get injured** — An informal and very common way to express getting hurt.
  - *Example: The player had to leave the game early because he got injured.*

**Tip:** Learning collocations is an excellent way to improve your vocabulary in English. Instead of learning individual words, learning which words naturally go together will make your English sound much more natural and fluent.

## 6. Expressions & Idioms

- **Fist bump** — A greeting or gesture where two people lightly tap their closed fists together, often done when excited or "pumped up."
  - *Example: Yeah! Fist bump!*
  - *Example: Fist bump is not in my fighting database.*

- **Get to the point** — Used to tell someone to be clear and direct, and to say whatever they're trying to say without unnecessary detail.
  - *Example: Oh, just get to the point!*
  - *Example: Do you know what the hardest part about being a king is? Doing what's right, no matter what. — Get to the point. — Point being, I need your help.*

- **Don't you get it?** — Used when you want someone to understand something that seems obvious to you.
  - *Example: Don't you guys get it? The man in the mask who attacked us is none other than Alistair Krei.*

- **None other than** — An emphatic way to introduce or reveal someone's identity, often used for dramatic effect.
  - *Example: The man in the mask who attacked us is none other than — voilà — Alistair Krei.*
  - *Example: And who should I spot? None other than Kent Brackman, the local news guy.*

- **You think?** — A sarcastic response used when someone states something painfully obvious. When Baymax says "You have fallen" and Hiro replies "You think?", he means "Obviously I fell — I already know that."
  - *Example: You have fallen. — You think?*

## 7. Connected Speech — Reductions & Silent Letters

**1. "I didn't know" — Reducing "didn't":**

- The word **"didn't"** gets so reduced in fast speech that the **final T** and the **middle D** essentially disappear.
- What you hear sounds more like **"I di'n know"** — the word is compressed into a much shorter sound.
  - Full: **"Baymax, I didn't know you were still active."**
  - Connected: **"Baymax, I di'n know you were still active."**

**2. "We don't even know" — Dropping T and linking N:**

- The **T** in **"don't"** is dropped, and the word links smoothly to **"even."**
- The **K** in **"know"** is silent, so the **N** in "even" and the **N** in "know" merge into one single N sound.
  - Full: **"We don't even know who he is."**
  - Connected: **"We don' even-no who he is."**

**3. "Annihilator" — Silent H:**

- The letter **H** in **"annihilator"** is silent. The word is pronounced more like **"uh-NY-uh-lay-ter."**
  - *Example: The Annihilator — behind the mask, industrialist Reid Axworthy.*

**4. "Rules don't apply to a man like Krei" — Multiple reductions:**

- The **T** in **"don't"** is dropped.
- The **N** in **"don't"** joins with the vowel **A** in "apply": **"don' apply."**
- The **T** in **"to"** falls between two vowels and becomes a soft **D** sound, and the word is reduced to **"duh."**
  - Full: **"The rules don't apply to a man like Krei."**
  - Connected: **"The rules don' apply duh a man like Krei."**

**Tip:** These reductions happen naturally in fast speech. You don't need to copy them perfectly, but recognizing them is essential for understanding native speakers in movies, TV shows, and real conversations.

## 8. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 8.1 Big Hero 6 — The Movie

- Big Hero 6 (2014) is a Disney animated film loosely based on the Marvel comic book series of the same name. It tells the story of Hiro Hamada, a 14-year-old robotics genius who lives in the futuristic city of San Fransokyo.
- Hiro spends most of his time hustling in illegal robot fights. His older brother Tadashi takes him to the San Fransokyo Institute of Technology and introduces him to Baymax, an inflatable healthcare robot that Tadashi created.
- After a devastating fire at the institute claims Tadashi's life, Hiro discovers that a mysterious masked villain is using his stolen microbots. With the help of Baymax and Tadashi's friends — GoGo, Wasabi, Honey Lemon, and Fred — Hiro transforms them into a superhero team to stop the villain.
- The film explores themes of grief, revenge versus justice, and the healing power of friendship and connection.

---

### 8.2 Baymax — The Healthcare Companion

- Baymax is one of the most beloved Disney characters of the 2010s. He is a large, inflatable, marshmallow-like healthcare robot designed by Tadashi to help people.
- Much of the film's humor comes from Baymax's literal, clinical approach to everything. He treats puberty like a medical diagnosis, announces scan results at inappropriate times, and cannot understand social cues.
- The scene where Baymax diagnoses Hiro with "puberty" is especially funny because Baymax — as a healthcare robot — treats normal teenage mood swings as if they were a disease requiring medical attention.
- Baymax's catchphrase, "I am Baymax, your personal healthcare companion," and his gentle personality have made him an iconic character in modern animation.

---

### 8.3 San Fransokyo — The Setting

- The film is set in **San Fransokyo**, a fictional city that blends San Francisco and Tokyo. The cityscape combines the iconic geography of San Francisco (the Golden Gate Bridge, steep hills, Victorian houses) with Japanese architectural elements, neon signs, and cultural influences.
- This fusion reflects the film's themes of cross-cultural connection and was inspired by the history of Japanese immigration to San Francisco.
- The San Fransokyo Institute of Technology (SFIT), where Tadashi studied and created Baymax, is the film's version of a prestigious tech university — reflecting the real-world importance of Silicon Valley and technological innovation in the San Francisco Bay Area.

---

### 8.4 Comic Book Storytelling & Fred's Theories

- Fred, one of Hiro's friends, is a comic book enthusiast who sees the world through the lens of superhero stories. In the villain-identification scene, he presents elaborate theories with dramatic comic-book-style names: "Dr. Slaughter, M.D.," "The Annihilator," "Baron Von Destruct."
- His theatrical approach frustrates the others, leading Hiro to say "Just get to the point!" — a common real-life reaction when someone is being overly dramatic or taking too long to say something simple.
- This scene is also a gentle parody of how comic books and superhero stories often feature villains with exaggerated, dramatic names and secret identities.
- The humor works because Fred's wild theories are contrasted with the more practical, straightforward thinking of the other characters — a classic comedy dynamic between the dreamer and the realists.
