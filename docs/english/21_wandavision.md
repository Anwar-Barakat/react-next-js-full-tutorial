## 6. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **To fit in** — To be accepted by the other people in a group; to blend in without standing out.
  - *Example: This is our home now. I want us to fit in.*
  - *Example: It wasn't so hard to fit in after all.*

- **Fine** — Very good or of a very high standard. A "fine" man or woman is someone worthy of respect.
  - *Example: He's a fine, real gentleman.*

- **Unusual** — Not common; different from what is expected. The opposite of "usual."
  - *Example: We are an unusual couple, you know?*
  - *Example: Here, you have an unusual problem.*

- **Unusually** — The adverb form; to a degree that is not common or expected.
  - *Example: Not even your unusually attractive aunt?*

- **In question** — Being doubted or considered uncertain.
  - *Example: I don't think that was ever in question.*

- **Anniversary** — The date on which an event occurred in a previous year, especially a wedding anniversary.
  - *Example: We don't have an anniversary, or a song, or even wedding rings.*

- **To remedy** — To provide a solution for something; to fix or correct a problem.
  - *Example: Well, we could remedy that.*

- **Newlywed** — A person who has recently married; a just-married couple.
  - *Example: All victims were newlywed couples.*

- **To fulfill** — To achieve something that you hoped for, wished for, or aimed to accomplish.
  - *Example: The boy fulfilled his dream of visiting Disneyland.*

- **Fundraiser** — An event organized to collect money for a cause, charity, or organization.
  - *Example: The talent show fundraiser is the most important event of the season.*

- **Neighborly** — Friendly and helpful, as a good neighbor should be.
  - *Example: It's our neighborly duty to participate.*

- **To assure** — To tell someone something positively to remove any doubt.
  - *Example: I assure you, I don't mean anyone any harm.*

- **Harm** — Physical or emotional damage or injury.
  - *Example: I don't mean anyone any harm.*

- **To mean (to)** — To intend to; to have the intention of doing something.
  - *Example: I think what my wife means to say is...*
  - *Example: I don't mean to make things difficult.*

- **Lost for words / Rendered speechless** — Unable to think of anything to say, usually because of surprise or shock.
  - *Example: She's lost for words — she doesn't have anything to say.*

- **Dear / Darling** — Terms of endearment used between married couples or close family members.
  - *Example: Have a good day, dear darling.*

## 7. Investigation & Technical Vocabulary

- **Relic** — Something old and interesting from the past; a surviving object from an earlier time.
  - *Example: That's relic radiation dating back to the Big Bang.*

- **To date back to** — To have started or originated at a particular time in the past.
  - *Example: This radiation dates back to the Big Bang.*
  - *Example: This old picture dates back to 1900.*

- **Entwined** — Closely connected or unable to be separated; wound or twisted together.
  - *Example: Entwined with a broadcast frequency.*

- **Vintage** — Old but of good quality; from the past and valued for its age and character.
  - *Example: I had your goons pick me up a sweet vintage TV.*

- **Goon** — A slightly derogatory term for someone, sometimes meaning a criminal or a person who behaves in a silly way. Used informally to refer to subordinates or helpers.
  - *Example: I had your goons pick me up a sweet vintage TV.*

- **Bad boy** — A humorous, informal way to refer to a product or item, especially if it's impressive.
  - *Example: When I plug this bad boy in — voila!*

- **Cockamamie** — Ridiculous, unbelievable, or not making sense.
  - *Example: Some cockamamie social experiment?*

- **Scarcity** — Having very little of something; a shortage. Comes from the adjective "scarce."
  - *Example: We're working with the same scarcity of intel.*
  - *Example: Food was often scarce in the winter.*

- **Intel** — Short for "intelligence." In this context: information about secret activities, military plans, enemy operations, or the specifics of a case being investigated.
  - *Example: We're working with the same scarcity of intel.*

- **Sitcom** — Short for "situation comedy." A comedy TV show with recurring characters in everyday situations.
  - *Example: It's a sitcom. A 1950s sitcom.*

- **Working theory** — A preliminary explanation or idea that is being tested or investigated.
  - *Example: It's a working theory.*

- **Alternate reality** — A different version of the world or universe; another possible existence.
  - *Example: Is it an alternate reality? Time travel?*

- **Broadcast** — To transmit a program on television or radio.
  - *Example: Entwined with a broadcast frequency.*

- **Transmitter** — A device that sends out radio or television signals.
  - *Example: This transmitter will mimic the frequency of the broadcast.*

- **To mimic** — To copy or imitate something closely.
  - *Example: This transmitter will mimic the frequency.*

## 8. Expressions & Idioms

- **Fit in** — To be accepted by a group; to blend in.
  - *Example: I want us to fit in.*

- **Going to be a gas** — An outdated expression meaning something is going to be fun or entertaining.
  - *Example: Oh, this is going to be a gas!*

- **Why would you think that?** — Used when you find it surprising what someone thinks or says.
  - *Example: Am I dead? — No. — Why would you think that?*

- **What my wife/husband means to say** — A phrase used to reinterpret or correct what someone has said, often to smooth over an awkward moment.
  - *Example: I think what my wife means to say is that we moved for...*

- **Dinner is served** — Literally: the meal is ready. Used humorously to present something with flair.
  - *Example: Voila, sound and picture. Dinner is served.*

- **I don't mean anyone any harm** — A way of reassuring someone that your intentions are not bad.
  - *Example: I assure you, I don't mean anyone any harm.*

- **Pick something up** — To get, buy, or collect something.
  - *Example: I had your goons pick me up a sweet vintage TV.*
  - *Example: I picked up this pair of shoes on my way home.*

- **Sweet** — Slang synonym for "cool" or "great."
  - *Example: Pick me up a sweet vintage TV.*

## 9. Connected Speech — Shadowing Practice

The neighbor's rapid-fire questions provide excellent shadowing practice. Here are the connected speech patterns to notice:

**1. "Where did you two move from?"**
- **"did you"** merges into **"didju"** /dɪdʒuː/ (D+Y → J sound)
- Practice: "Where didju two move from?"

**2. "What brought you here?"**
- **"brought you"** becomes **"broughtchu"** /brɔːtʃuː/ (T+Y → CH sound)
- Practice: "What broughtchu here?"

**3. "How long have you been married?"**
- **"have you"** reduces to **"havyou"** — connected smoothly
- Practice as one chunk

**4. "And why don't you have children yet?"**
- **"don't you"** becomes **"don'tchu"** /doʊntʃuː/ (T+Y → CH sound)
- Practice: "Why don'tchu have children yet?"

**Tip:** Shadowing means repeating speech as closely to the original as possible. This is one of the most effective techniques for improving your pronunciation and fluency.

## 10. Connected Speech — Reductions & Linking

**"I want us to fit in":**
- **"want us"** → the T is softened, sounds like "wan-us"
- **"to"** → reduced to "tuh" /tə/
- **"fit in"** → the T in "fit" becomes a soft D (flap T): "fid-in"
- Full phrase: sounds like "I wan-us tuh fid-in"

## 11. Grammar — "Why Would You" for Surprise

We use **"why would you"** + verb when we find it surprising what someone thinks, says, or does:

- *Am I dead? — No. Why would you think that?*
- *Why would I do that?*
- *Why would you help my brother escape?*

This structure expresses that the speaker finds the action or thought unlikely or unexpected.

---

## 12. Grammar — "Have Someone Do Something"

When you **"have someone do something,"** you ask them to do it or make them do it:

- *I had your goons pick me up a sweet vintage TV.* (= I asked/told them to get me a TV.)
- *The boss had his employees work extra hours.* (= The boss made them work extra hours.)

**"Pick up" in this context** means to get, buy, or collect something:
- *I picked up this pair of shoes on my way home.*

## 13. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 13.1 WandaVision — The Show

- WandaVision (2021) is a Marvel TV series set in the Marvel Cinematic Universe after the events of Avengers: Endgame.
- The show follows Wanda Maximoff (Scarlet Witch) and Vision, who find themselves living inside a sitcom-style reality that changes decades with each episode.
- The first three episodes are styled as a 1950s sitcom, complete with a laugh track, black-and-white visuals, and period-appropriate humor.
- Outside this sitcom reality, agents are investigating the mysterious "Westview Anomaly" and trying to understand what's happening.

---

### 13.2 The 1950s American Dream

- The 1950s in America was the decade following World War II and is often described as one of the most prosperous economic times in American history.
- The "American dream" of this era centered on: owning a big house in the suburbs, driving a nice car, having kids, and even having a dog — a symbol of the typical happy American family.
- WandaVision uses this cultural backdrop deliberately — Wanda and Vision are trying to live the "perfect" 1950s life while hiding their true identities.
- The show's intro song emphasizes this: "a regular husband and wife" — the key word being "regular," as their lives are anything but.

---

### 13.3 Sitcoms and Their Role

- A **sitcom** (situation comedy) is a TV genre featuring recurring characters in everyday situations, designed to make audiences laugh.
- Classic American sitcoms include I Love Lucy, The Dick Van Dyke Show, and Bewitched — all of which WandaVision directly references.
- The fact that the mysterious anomaly is broadcasting as a sitcom is a central mystery of the show — agents find it bizarre that the universe seems to have "created a sitcom starring two Avengers."

---

### 13.4 Fitting In and Keeping Secrets

- "Fitting in" is a central theme of WandaVision — Wanda and Vision have superpowers but want to appear normal.
- This mirrors the experience of many people in real life who feel they need to hide parts of their identity to be accepted by a group.
- The neighbor's rapid-fire questions ("Where did you move from? How long have you been married? Why don't you have children?") represent the social pressure to conform — and the awkward situation of not having the "right" answers.
- Wanda being "lost for words" and Vision jumping in with "what my wife means to say" is a classic sitcom trope of couples covering for each other in awkward social moments.
