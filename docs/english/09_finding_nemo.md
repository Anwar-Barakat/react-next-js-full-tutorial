## 1. Scene — Marlin's Story Spreads Across the Ocean

- **Dory:** What?
- **Marlin:** You see, my son was taken — my son was taken away from me.
- **Fish:** No way!
- **Marlin:** Well, okay. I live on this reef, a long, long way from here.
- **Dory:** Oh boy, this is gonna be good — I can tell!
- **Marlin:** And my son Nemo — he was mad at me. And maybe he wouldn't have done it if I hadn't been so tough on him. I don't know.
- **Marlin:** Anyway, he swam out in the open water to this boat, and when he was out there, these divers appeared, and I tried to stop them — but the boat was too fast.
- **Fish:** He scared away the sharks by blowing them up?! Golly, that's amazing!
- **Fish:** But then he just dove straight down into the deep! It's like, wicked dark down there — you can't see a thing!
- **Fish:** And the only thing they can see is this big, horrible creature with razor-sharp teeth!
- **Turtle:** He may be on his way here right now — Sydney Harbour — in a matter of days! I mean, it sounds like this guy's gonna stop at nothing till he finds his son.
- **Fish:** Sure is. That's one dedicated father.

## 2. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **To be taken away** — To be captured and removed from someone by force.
  - *Example: My son was taken away from me.*

- **Mad at (somebody)** — Angry with somebody (informal American English).
  - *Example: My son Nemo — he was mad at me.*

- **Tough on (somebody) / Hard on (somebody)** — To be strict and treat someone severely.
  - *Example: Maybe he wouldn't have done it if I hadn't been so tough on him.*

- **To tell** — Common meaning: to say something to someone. Less common meaning: to know, recognize, or be certain of something.
  - *Example: A mother can normally tell if her kids are hungry.*
  - *Example: Oh boy, this is gonna be good — I can tell!*

- **To appear** — To become visible; to come into sight.
  - *Example: When he was out there, these divers appeared.*

- **To scare away / scare off** — To cause someone or something to go away because of fear.
  - *Example: He scared away the sharks by blowing them up!*

- **To blow up** — To explode.
  - *Example: Marlin and Dory escaped from three sharks because of an explosion.*

- **Razor-sharp** — Extremely sharp (like a razor blade).
  - *Example: This big, horrible creature with razor-sharp teeth!*

- **Horrible** — Very bad, unpleasant, or frightening.
  - *Example: The only thing they can see is this big, horrible creature.*

- **A thing** — When used negatively (can't see a thing, don't know a thing), means "anything at all."
  - *Example: It's wicked dark down there — you can't see a thing!*

- **Dedicated** — Devoted to a task, purpose, or cause with loyalty and commitment.
  - *Example: That's one dedicated father.*

- **A matter of (days/seconds/minutes)** — Only; just. Used to emphasize how short a time period is.
  - *Example: In a matter of seconds, the building was in flames.*
  - *Example: He may be here in a matter of days!*

- **To stop at nothing** — To be willing to do anything, including extreme or risky actions, to achieve a goal.
  - *Example: He will stop at nothing to save the princess.*
  - *Example: This guy's gonna stop at nothing till he finds his son.*

- **To make it** — To succeed in achieving something difficult, or to succeed in arriving somewhere on time.
  - *Example: The wedding! We'll never make it in time!*
  - *Example: If either of you make it across that sinkhole, you get the sloth.*

- **To bump into (somebody)** — Literally: to hit someone with your body by accident. Figuratively: to meet someone unexpectedly.
  - *Example: I bumped into Joanna on the street yesterday.*

- **Ferocious** — Very aggressive and violent.
  - *Example: A ferocious animal or person is very aggressive and violent.*

- **Countless** — Too many to count; very many.
  - *Example: I watched it countless times as a kid.*

- **Packed with** — Full of; containing a lot of something.
  - *Example: This scene is packed with vocabulary related to the sea.*

- **To fascinate** — To attract and hold someone's interest intensely.
  - *Example: His story fascinates other fish and animals.*

- **To spread** — To share or distribute widely.
  - *Example: They quickly share and spread the story to their friends all over the ocean.*

- **To regret** — To feel sorry about something you did or didn't do.
  - *Example: Marlin regrets not treating Nemo more kindly.*

- **To capture** — To catch and take someone or something by force.
  - *Example: Nemo was captured by divers.*

- **Journey** — A long trip from one place to another.
  - *Example: Marlin's journey to find Nemo takes him across the ocean.*

- **On his way** — Currently traveling toward a destination.
  - *Example: He may be on his way here right now.*

- **To survive** — To continue to live through a dangerous or difficult situation.
  - *Example: If you make it, you are successful in surviving through a very difficult period.*

## 3. Sea & Ocean Vocabulary

- **Reef** — A raised area on the ocean floor made of rock, coral, or sand. Attracts diverse ocean life.
  - *Example: I live on this reef, a long, long way from here.*

- **Open water** — An area in the ocean that is deep and not close to any terrain like a reef or land.
  - *Example: He swam out in the open water.*

- **Diver / Scuba diver** — A person who dives underwater as a sport or profession, typically using scuba equipment.
  - *Example: These divers appeared and took Nemo away.*

- **Ocean current** — A continuous, directed movement of seawater. In the movie, Marlin uses the East Australian Current to travel faster.
  - *Example: The ocean current carried them toward Sydney.*

- **Harbour / Harbor** — A protected area along the shore where boats can be anchored or docked.
  - *Example: He may be on his way to Sydney Harbour right now.*

- **The deep** — The deepest parts of the ocean, far below the surface.
  - *Example: He just dove straight down into the deep!*

- **Whale** — A very large marine mammal.
  - *Example: I speak whale!*

- **Krill** — Tiny shrimp-like creatures that whales eat.
  - *Example: Whales don't eat clown fish — they eat krill.*

- **Clown fish** — A small, brightly colored fish (orange with white stripes) — Nemo and Marlin's species.
  - *Example: Don't worry, whales don't eat clown fish.*

- **Shark** — A large predatory fish with sharp teeth.
  - *Example: He scared away the sharks by blowing them up!*

- **Marine** — Relating to the sea.
  - *Example: The reef attracts diverse marine animals.*

## 4. Slang & Interjections

- **Oh boy** — An interjection with two uses: (1) excitement or pleasant surprise, (2) annoyance or disappointment.
  - *Excited: Oh boy, this is gonna be good!*
  - *Annoyed: Oh boy, how do I explain this...*

- **Golly** — An interjection used to express excitement or surprise.
  - *Example: Golly, that's amazing!*

- **No way** — An exclamation of disbelief or surprise.
  - *Example: My son was taken away from me! — No way!*

- **Wicked** — (Slang) Used as an adjective for emphasis, meaning "very" or "extremely."
  - *Example: It's like wicked dark down there!*

- **Nope** — An informal way to say "no."
  - *Example: Have you seen Nemo? — Nope!*

## 5. Idioms & Expressions

- **Love the idea of (something)** — To imagine you would enjoy something you haven't experienced.
  - *Example: Olaf loves the idea of summer.*

- **A long way from here** — Very far away.
  - *Example: I live on this reef, a long, long way from here.*

- **I can tell** — I can sense or recognize something without being told directly.
  - *Example: This is gonna be good — I can tell!*

- **Can't see a thing** — Cannot see anything at all.
  - *Example: It's wicked dark down there — you can't see a thing!*

- **Stop at nothing** — To be willing to do anything to achieve a goal.
  - *Example: This guy's gonna stop at nothing till he finds his son.*

- **In a matter of (time)** — In just; only (emphasizes shortness of time).
  - *Example: In a matter of days, he'll be in Sydney.*

## 6. Connected Speech — Dropping Sounds

Marlin's line **"Maybe he wouldn't have done it if I hadn't been so tough on him"** demonstrates several native speech patterns:

**1. Dropping the T in NT clusters:**
- **wouldn't** → /wʊdən/ (T is dropped)
- **international** → /ɪnərnæʃənəl/
- **interesting** → /ɪntrəstɪŋ/

**2. Dropping the H in pronouns:**
- **him** → /ɪm/
- **her** → /ɜr/
- **have** → /əv/ (H dropped, connected to previous word)
- **wouldn't have** → sounds like "wooden-uv"

**3. Connecting "done" + "it":**
- **done it** → /dʌnɪt/ (reduced and connected as one sound)

**Full natural pronunciation:**
- "Maybe he wooden-uv dunnit if I hadn't been so tough on 'im"

## 7. Grammar — Irregular Verb "To Swim"

"To swim" is one of the trickiest irregular verbs — even natives sometimes confuse the past and perfect forms:

- **Present:** I swim
- **Past:** I **swam** (NOT "swimmed")
- **Perfect:** I have **swum** (NOT "swam")

*Example from the movie: "He swam out in the open water."*

**Other tricky irregular verbs with similar patterns:**
- sing → sang → sung
- ring → rang → rung
- drink → drank → drunk
- begin → began → begun

## 8. Pronunciation — Words Ending in "-ous"

Words ending in **-ous** are commonly mispronounced. Most share the same simple ending sound: /əs/.

- **ferocious** → /fəˈroʊʃəs/ (note: the C becomes an SH sound)
- **courageous** → /kəˈreɪdʒəs/
- **mysterious** → /mɪˈstɪriəs/
- **gorgeous** → /ˈɡɔːrdʒəs/
- **dangerous** → /ˈdeɪndʒərəs/

**Tip:** Don't over-pronounce the "-ous" ending. Keep it short and unstressed: /əs/.

## 9. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 9.1 The Great Barrier Reef

- The reef in Finding Nemo is based on the famous **Great Barrier Reef** in Australia — the world's largest coral reef system.
- This is reflected in the Australian accents heard throughout the movie.
- A reef is a raised area on the ocean floor made of rock, coral, or sand that attracts diverse marine life.

---

### 9.2 American vs Australian Accents

- Finding Nemo features both American accents (Marlin, Dory) and Australian accents (the turtles, fish near Sydney).
- This makes it a great movie for practicing listening to different English accents.
- Ellen DeGeneres (American) voices Dory, while many Australian voice actors voice the local sea creatures.

---

### 9.3 Storytelling and How Stories Spread

- This scene is perfect for learning how to tell a story in English.
- Marlin tells his story using past tenses, and as the story spreads from fish to fish across the ocean, it gets exaggerated and more dramatic.
- This reflects the real-life concept of stories changing as they are retold — similar to the "telephone game."

---

### 9.4 The Two Meanings of "Tell"

- Most learners know "tell" as giving information: *"Tell me your name."*
- The less common meaning is **to know or recognize something**: *"I can tell you're tired."*
- Dory uses this second meaning when she says "This is gonna be good — I can tell!" meaning she can **sense** it will be a good story.
- Other examples: *"A mother can normally tell if her kids are hungry."*
