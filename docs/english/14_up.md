## 1. Scene 1 — Meeting Doug

- **Carl:** Hey — okay, you were talking to a rock.
- **Russell:** Hey, that one looks like a turtle! Look at that one — that one looks like a dog!
- **Russell:** Oh — it is a dog! What? Oh, we're not allowed to have dogs in my apartment.
- **Russell:** Hey, I like dogs! We have your dog!
- **Carl:** Whoa — I wonder who he belongs to. Sit, boy!
- **Russell:** Hey look, he's trained! Shake! Uh-huh. Speak!
- **Doug:** Hi there!
- **Russell:** Did that dog just say "hi there"?
- **Doug:** Oh yes. My name is Doug. I have just met you and I love you.
- **Doug:** My master made me this collar. He is a good and smart master, and he made me this collar so that I may talk. Squirrel!

## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **To allow** — To give permission for someone to do something.
  - *Example: I allow you to read my diary.*
  - *Example: We're not allowed to have dogs in my apartment.*

- **Apartment** — A set of rooms for living in, usually on one floor of a building. In British English: "flat."
  - *Example: We're not allowed to have dogs in my apartment.*

- **To wonder** — To express a wish to know about something; to be curious.
  - *Example: I wonder who he belongs to.*

- **To belong to** — To be owned by someone; to be the property of.
  - *Example: I wonder who he belongs to.*

- **To train** — To teach a skill, especially to an animal. Also: to practice and prepare for a sport or activity.
  - *Example: Hey look, he's trained!*

- **Trick** — A clever action that an animal has been taught to perform on command.
  - *Example: Russell is trying to figure out which tricks the dog can perform.*

- **Master** — An owner of a pet or animal; a person who has control over something.
  - *Example: My master made me this collar. He is a good and smart master.*

- **Collar** — A strap or band worn around the neck of an animal, often used for identification or to attach a leash.
  - *Example: My master made me this collar so that I may talk.*

- **Radioactive** — A scientific term for a substance that emits harmful energy (radiation). Being exposed to radioactive things can be very dangerous or even deadly.
  - *Example: Don't touch that — it could be radioactive or something.*

- **Tracker** — Someone or something that can detect, recognize, and follow a specific scent or trail. Dogs, especially certain breeds, are natural trackers.
  - *Example: I am a great tracker.*

- **Pack** — A group of dogs or wolves that live and hunt together.
  - *Example: My pack sent me on a special mission, all by myself.*

- **Mission** — A task or job that someone is sent to do, often important or difficult.
  - *Example: My pack sent me on a special mission.*

- **Scent** — A smell, especially one left by an animal that can be followed by another animal.
  - *Example: I've been on the scent.*

- **Up close** — Very close; at a short distance. Another way of saying "from a close distance."
  - *Example: I have never seen one up close, but this is the bird.*

- **Prisoner** — Someone who has been captured and is not allowed to leave.
  - *Example: May I take your bird back to camp as my prisoner?*

- **Camp** — A place where a group sets up temporary shelter, often outdoors.
  - *Example: May I take your bird back to camp?*

- **Weird** — Strange or unusual.
  - *Example: It's just a weird trick or something.*

- **Falls** — Short for "waterfall" — a place where water flows over the edge of a cliff or rock.
  - *Example: Let's get to the falls.*

- **To figure out** — To understand something or solve a problem by thinking about it.
  - *Example: Russell is trying to figure out which tricks the dog can perform.*

- **Or something** — Added to the end of a statement to show uncertainty or vagueness.
  - *Example: It could be radioactive or something.*
  - *Example: It's just a weird trick or something.*

- **Golden retriever** — A breed of dog known for its friendly temperament and great ability to detect, track, and locate scents. Commonly used as police dogs, guide dogs, and search-and-rescue dogs. Doug is a golden retriever.
  - *Example: Doug is a golden retriever, a breed known for its great tracking abilities.*

- **Breed** — A particular type or variety of animal, especially a dog.
  - *Example: The golden retriever is a breed known for its tracking abilities.*

- **Odor** — A smell, especially an unpleasant one.
  - *Example: Golden retrievers can detect, track, and locate the source of certain odors.*

## 4. Dog & Animal Vocabulary

- **Sit** — A basic dog command telling the dog to lower its body and rest on its hindquarters.
  - *Example: Sit, boy!*

- **Shake** — A dog trick where the dog extends its paw for a handshake.
  - *Example: Shake! Uh-huh.*

- **Speak** — A dog command asking the dog to bark on command.
  - *Example: Speak! — "Hi there!"*

- **Bark** — The loud, sharp sound a dog makes to communicate with other dogs and people.
  - *Example: On the way, learn how to bark like a real dog.*

- **Howl / Howling** — A long, loud, mournful sound produced by dogs or wolves.
  - *Example: And here's howling!*

- **Squirrel** — A small animal with a bushy tail that lives in trees. Dogs are famously distracted by squirrels.
  - *Example: Squirrel!*

- **Paw** — The foot of an animal that has claws (dogs, cats, bears, etc.).
  - *Example: Shaking his paw is one of the common tricks dogs perform.*

- **Turtle** — A slow-moving reptile with a hard shell on its back.
  - *Example: That one looks like a turtle!*

## 5. Expressions & Idioms

- **Not allowed to** — Not having permission to do something.
  - *Example: We're not allowed to have dogs in my apartment.*

- **I wonder** — Expresses curiosity about something; a wish to know.
  - *Example: I wonder who he belongs to.*

- **Up close** — From a very short distance; very near.
  - *Example: I have never seen one up close.*

- **Did I mention that?** — A rhetorical question used to restate or emphasize something you already said.
  - *Example: I'm a great tracker — did I mention that?*

- **On the way** — During the journey; while traveling to a destination.
  - *Example: On the way, learn how to bark like a real dog.*

- **Can we keep him?** — A classic phrase children use when they find a stray animal and want to adopt it.
  - *Example: Can we keep him? Please, please, please!*

## 6. Pronunciation — Glottal T in American English

In American English, when a syllable or word ends in **NT**, the **T often becomes a glottal T** — the sound is cut off at the back of the throat instead of being released at the front of the mouth.

**From the movie:**
- **apartment** → /əˈpɑːrʔmənʔ/ (the T is stopped, not fully pronounced)

**More examples:**
- **mountain** → /maʊnʔən/ (sounds like "moun'ain")
- **can't** → /kænʔ/
- **assistant** → /əˈsɪstənʔ/
- **mint** → /mɪnʔ/
- **important** → /ɪmˈpɔːrʔənʔ/

**How it works:**
- Instead of placing your tongue at the front of your mouth to make the T sound, you stop the airflow at the back of your throat.
- This creates a brief pause or "catch" in the sound instead of a crisp T.

**Tip:** You don't need to force this pronunciation — just be aware of it when listening to native American English speakers. The more you hear it, the more natural it will become.

## 7. Pronunciation — Words Ending in "-sion"

Words ending in **-sion** are pronounced as **"shun"** /ʃən/:

- **mission** → /ˈmɪʃən/ ("mi-shun")
- **passion** → /ˈpæʃən/ ("pa-shun")
- **commission** → /kəˈmɪʃən/ ("kuh-mi-shun")
- **television** → /ˈtɛləvɪʒən/ (note: after a vowel, it can sound like "zhun")
- **decision** → /dɪˈsɪʒən/
- **vision** → /ˈvɪʒən/

**From the movie:**
- *"My pack sent me on a special mission."* — Doug pronounces it as "mi-shun."

**Tip:** The "-sion" ending follows the same pattern as "-tion" (which is also pronounced "shun"). Don't over-pronounce the "s-i-o-n" — just say "shun."

## 8. Grammar — Omitting "I" at the Start of Sentences

Native English speakers often **drop the pronoun "I"** at the beginning of a sentence in casual speech, even though it is grammatically incorrect.

**From the movie:**
- Carl says **"Wonder who he belongs to"** instead of **"I wonder who he belongs to."**

**More examples:**
- **"Gotta go"** instead of "I've gotta go"
- **"Don't know"** instead of "I don't know"
- **"Can't believe it"** instead of "I can't believe it"
- **"Think so"** instead of "I think so"

**Note:** This is very common in casual spoken English but should be avoided in formal writing or professional contexts.

## 9. Grammar — "Can" vs "May"

Both **"can"** and **"may"** can be used to ask for permission, but they differ in formality:

- **Can** — Informal; used in everyday conversation.
  - *Example: Can I borrow your pen?*
  - *Example: Can we keep him?*

- **May** — More formal and polite; traditionally considered the "correct" way to ask for permission.
  - *Example: May I take your bird back to camp as my prisoner?*
  - *Example: May I come in?*

**From the movie:**
- Doug uses **"may"**: *"May I take your bird back to camp as my prisoner?"*
- Russell uses **"can"**: *"Can we keep him?"*

**Tip:** In modern casual English, "can" is perfectly acceptable for asking permission. Use "may" in formal situations or when you want to sound especially polite.

## 10. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 10.1 Up — The Movie

- Up (2009) is a Disney Pixar animated film about Carl Fredricksen, a 78-year-old widower who ties thousands of balloons to his house to fly to South America and fulfill a promise he made to his late wife, Ellie.
- Along the way, he accidentally brings Russell, an eager young Wilderness Explorer scout, and they encounter talking dogs, a rare giant bird, and an adventurer-turned-villain.
- The film is famous for its deeply emotional opening sequence and its themes of adventure, loss, and finding new purpose.

---

### 10.2 Doug's Character — Talking Dogs That Are Still Dogs

- One of the funniest aspects of Up is that while the dogs can speak (thanks to special collars), they still behave exactly like real dogs.
- Doug gets distracted mid-sentence by a **squirrel** — a classic dog behavior.
- The dogs bark, howl, chase things, and have typical dog instincts, despite being able to hold conversations.
- This contrast between human-like speech and dog-like behavior is a major source of humor throughout the film.

---

### 10.3 The Pun in Doug's Name

- The character's name **"Doug"** is a clever wordplay:
  - **Doug** — A common human name.
  - **Dug** — The past tense of "dig," something dogs do all the time (digging holes in the yard).
- This pun reflects Pixar's tradition of using character names for subtle humor (similar to "Hamm" the pig in Toy Story).

---

### 10.4 Dog Tricks and Commands

- The scene where Russell tests Doug's training demonstrates common dog commands that are part of everyday English:
  - **"Sit"** — The most basic command; the dog lowers itself to sit.
  - **"Shake"** — The dog offers its paw for a handshake.
  - **"Speak"** — The dog barks on command.
- These commands are so universal that they're often used metaphorically in English:
  - *"Sit!"* can be said jokingly to a person.
  - *"Speak!"* can mean "say something" or "go ahead and talk."
- The humor in the scene comes from Doug responding to "speak" by saying "Hi there!" in full English instead of just barking.
