## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Lineup** — A synonym for agenda or schedule; the sequence of activities or events planned. Can also refer to a television or concert programming schedule, or a police identification lineup.
  - *Example: 7 AM, the usual morning lineup.*
  - *Example: Fox's morning lineup includes lots of news programs.*
  - *Example: Billie Eilish will be in Lollapalooza's lineup this year.*

- **Chores** — Routine tasks mostly related to housework, like cooking, cleaning, or taking the garbage out.
  - *Example: Start on the chores and sweep 'til the floor is all clean.*

- **Wonder** — To think about something with curiosity; to imagine or question how something could be.
  - *Example: And I'll keep wondering and wondering — when will my life begin?*

- **Room** — Physical space used for something. Can also mean the possibility of something happening or existing.
  - *Example: I'm sure there's room somewhere.*
  - *Example: This is a war — there's no room for doubt.*
  - *Example: This table takes up too much room.*

- **Pronounced** — More evident; more noticeable or obvious.
  - *Example: The grays have really started to become more pronounced.*

- **Evergreen** — Consistently relevant, regardless of how old it is; something that never goes out of style.
  - *Example: The movie's so evergreen.*

- **Grateful / Gratitude** — Feeling thankful for what you have; appreciation for something good.
  - *Example: Just how grateful I am that we got to do it together.*
  - *Example: Endless gratitude for all of the support.*

- **Immense** — Extremely large or great.
  - *Example: It was such an immense pleasure and joy.*

- **Enthusiastic** — Showing a lot of excitement and interest about something.
  - *Example: Thank you for being enthusiastic about it.*

- **Ditto** — An informal way of saying "I agree" or "the same for me." Used to express that you feel exactly the same way as the person who just spoke.
  - *Example: Ditto, ditto, ditto, ditto. All of the above.*

- **Many a time** — A somewhat formal or literary way of saying "many times" or "on many occasions."
  - *Example: Like Zach has said many a time, a bucket list opportunity.*

## 4. Daily Routine & Chores Vocabulary

- **To sweep** — To clean the floor using a brush or broom.
  - *Example: Start on the chores and sweep 'til the floor is all clean.*

- **To polish** — To clean something using a piece of cloth until it's shining.
  - *Example: Polish and wax, do laundry and mop and shine up.*

- **To wax** — To apply a wax substance to a surface to make it shiny and protected. "Wax" is also the yellow substance produced by bees, or a substance made of fat or oil used to make candles.
  - *Example: I clean and wax the floor once a week.*

- **To do the laundry** — To wash the dirty clothes.
  - *Example: Polish and wax, do laundry and mop and shine up.*

- **To mop** — To clean the floor using a mop (a long stick with strings at one end). Also used as a noun for the cleaning tool itself.
  - *Example: Polish and wax, do laundry and mop and shine up.*

- **To shine up** — To make something bright and clean by rubbing or polishing it.
  - *Example: Do laundry and mop and shine up.*

- **To brush (hair)** — To use a brush to smooth and tidy hair.
  - *Example: I'll brush and brush and brush and brush my hair.*

## 5. Free Time Activities Vocabulary

- **Reading a book** — One of the most common free time activities.
  - *Example: And so I'll read a book, or maybe two or three.*

- **Painting** — Creating art with paint on a canvas or surface.
  - *Example: I'll add a few new paintings to my gallery.*

- **Playing guitar** — Performing music on a guitar.
  - *Example: I'll play guitar and knit and cook.*

- **Knitting** — Making clothes or fabric by interlocking loops of yarn with needles.
  - *Example: I'll play guitar and knit and cook.*

- **Cooking** — A general term for preparing food using any method.
  - *Example: I'll play guitar and knit and cook.*

- **Baking** — A specific type of cooking that uses an oven.
  - *Example: Then after lunch, it's puzzles and darts and baking.*

- **Puzzles** — Games or activities where you fit pieces together or solve problems for entertainment.
  - *Example: Then after lunch, it's puzzles and darts and baking.*

- **Darts** — A game where you throw small pointed objects (darts) at a circular target on a wall.
  - *Example: Then after lunch, it's puzzles and darts and baking.*

- **Paper mache** — An art activity where you create objects by layering wet paper strips over a form and letting them harden.
  - *Example: Paper mache, a bit of ballet and chess.*

- **Ballet** — A formal style of dance with precise movements and positions.
  - *Example: Paper mache, a bit of ballet and chess.*

- **Chess** — A strategic board game played between two players with 16 pieces each.
  - *Example: Paper mache, a bit of ballet and chess.*

## 6. Idioms from Cleaning Verbs

- **Sweep something under the rug** — To hide something unpleasant; to keep something as a secret rather than dealing with it.
  - *Example: They couldn't sweep under the rug the fact that the chairman sent someone.*

- **Sweep someone off their feet** — To make someone fall completely in love with you; to shock someone in a positive way emotionally.
  - *Example: You know, you should sweep me off my feet out yonder window and down a rope onto your valiant steed.*

- **Polish the apple / Apple polisher** — To excessively and insincerely praise someone, hoping to get a favor. This idiom references the image of small children bringing their teacher an apple to make a good impression.
  - *Example: John is always polishing the apple with the boss, but everybody knows he's hoping for a promotion.*

- **The whole ball of wax** — A common American idiom meaning "the whole thing" or "everything."
  - *Example: I swear this is the whole ball of wax. I have nothing else to tell you.*

- **Wash your dirty laundry in public** — To reveal personal secrets or private problems publicly. "Dirty laundry" refers to a person's secrets.
  - *Example: Don't wash your dirty laundry in public!*

- **Mop the floor with someone** — A very informal American way of saying you completely defeated someone in a game or competition. British English uses "wipe the floor with someone" instead.
  - *Example: I mopped the floor with him this afternoon.*

## 7. Expressions & Idioms

- **The blink of an eye** — Something that happens extremely quickly; in a very short amount of time.
  - *Example: In some ways it actually feels like the blink of an eye.*

- **Keen on** — To like something; to be interested in or enthusiastic about something.
  - *Example: I'm keen on reading books.*
  - *Example: I'm keen on horror movies.*

- **Not fond of** — To not like something; to have no particular liking for something.
  - *Example: I'm not very fond of cooking.*

- **Time to spare** — Having extra free time because you finished your tasks earlier than expected.
  - *Example: She had some time to spare after finishing her chores.*

- **Bucket list** — A list of things a person wants to do or experience before they die.
  - *Example: A bucket list opportunity and experience, and one that I will never forget.*

- **All of the above** — Everything that was just mentioned; used to agree with all the previous points.
  - *Example: Ditto, ditto. All of the above.*

- **For years to come** — For many years in the future.
  - *Example: We hope you enjoy it for years to come.*

- **Teacher's pet** — A student who is considered the teacher's favorite; someone who tries to gain favor with authority figures. Related to "apple polisher" and "butt kisser."
  - *Example: You know the names for people like you? Teacher's pet, apple polisher, butt kisser.*

## 8. Connected Speech — Word Linking

When speaking naturally, native English speakers connect words together smoothly. Here are three linking patterns from the lesson:

**1. "Keen on" — Linking consonant to vowel:**
- The N in "keen" connects directly to the vowel in "on": sounds like "keen-on" /kiːnɑn/
- Practice: "I'm keen-on reading books."

**2. "But I" — American T (Flap T):**
- Americans change the T in "but" to a soft D sound before a vowel: sounds like "bud-I" /bʌɾaɪ/
- Practice: "I'm keen on reading books, bud-I'm not very fond of cooking."

**3. "Fond of" — D linking to vowel:**
- The D in "fond" connects smoothly to the O in "of": sounds like "fond-ov" /fɑndəv/
- The preposition "of" is barely audible, reduced to /əv/
- Practice: "I'm not very fond-ov cooking."

## 9. Connected Speech — Consonant Dropping & Intrusive W

**1. Consonant dropping — When two identical consonants meet:**
- When two identical consonant sounds meet across words, one of them is dropped, and you only hear one.
- **"got to"** → sounds like "go-to" /ɡɑtuː/ (only one T is heard)
  - *Example: "How grateful I am that we got to do it together."*
- **"good day"** → sounds like "goo-day" (only one D is heard)
  - *Example: "I hope you have a good day."*
- **"best time"** → sounds like "bes-time" (only one T is heard)
  - *Example: "I had the best time last night."*

**2. Intrusive W sound — When certain vowels meet:**
- When a word ending in an "oo" or "oh" sound is followed by a word starting with a vowel, an extra W sound /w/ is inserted to connect them smoothly.
- **"do it"** → sounds like "do-w-it" /duːwɪt/
  - *Example: "How grateful I am that we got to do-w-it together."*
- **"go away"** → sounds like "go-w-away" /ɡoʊwəweɪ/
- **"so excited"** → sounds like "so-w-excited"

**Tip:** These are natural features of connected speech. You don't need to force them — just be aware of them when listening, and they'll gradually become part of your natural speaking.

## 10. Grammar — "Actually" as a Discourse Marker

"Actually" is a very common word in English conversation that functions as a **discourse marker** — a word that gives the speaker more time to think, adds natural flow, or emphasizes a point. It can have different meanings depending on where it appears:

**1. At the beginning of a sentence — Changing your mind:**
- Used to correct or adjust what was said before; to introduce a contrasting thought.
- *Example: "Actually, in some ways it feels like even longer ago."* (Zach corrects his previous statement about noticing the time passing.)
- *Example: "Actually, it did make us happy for a long time."*

**2. In the middle or end of a sentence — Emphasis (synonym for "really"):**
- Used to emphasize that something is truly the case.
- *Example: "In some ways it actually feels like the blink of an eye."* (= "it really feels like the blink of an eye")
- *Example: "I'm actually going to go to a bowling alley."*

**3. Correcting a misunderstanding:**
- Used to politely correct someone or introduce surprising information.
- *Example: "I'm kidding, actually."*
- *Example: "Well, this is so exciting, actually."*

**Tip:** "Actually" is one of the most useful discourse markers in English. Native speakers use it constantly in conversation. Other common discourse markers include "well," "so," "like," "I mean," and "you know."

## 11. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 11.1 Tangled — The Movie

- Tangled (2010) is a Disney animated film that retells the classic fairy tale of Rapunzel.
- The movie follows Rapunzel, a princess with magical long golden hair who has been locked in a tower her entire life by Mother Gothel, a woman who kidnapped her as a baby to use the magic of her hair to stay young.
- When a charming thief named Flynn Rider stumbles into her tower, Rapunzel convinces him to take her to see the floating lanterns that appear every year on her birthday.
- The film was voiced by Mandy Moore (Rapunzel) and Zachary Levi (Flynn Rider).

---

### 11.2 Rapunzel's Tower and Routine

- The song "When Will My Life Begin" captures Rapunzel's daily life trapped in the tower — she has developed an extensive routine of chores and hobbies to fill her days.
- She starts with cleaning (sweeping, polishing, waxing, mopping, doing laundry) and then moves to creative activities (reading, painting, playing guitar, knitting, cooking, puzzles, darts, paper mache, ballet, chess).
- Despite having so many activities, the song reveals her deep loneliness and desire for something more — she "keeps wondering" when her real life will begin.
- The humor in the song comes from the contrast between how busy she is and how early it still is: she finishes all her chores and "by then it's like 7:15."

---

### 11.3 Cooking vs Baking

- In English, "cooking" is a general term that covers all methods of preparing food — boiling, frying, grilling, roasting, steaming, etc.
- "Baking" is a specific type of cooking that involves using an oven — making bread, cakes, cookies, pastries, and similar items.
- All baking is cooking, but not all cooking is baking.
- In the song, Rapunzel mentions both "cook" and "baking" as separate activities, highlighting that she does general cooking as well as oven-based baking.

---

### 11.4 The 10th Anniversary

- In 2020, Tangled celebrated its 10th anniversary, and Mandy Moore and Zach Levi recorded a special message for fans.
- Zach humorously notes that 10 years have passed by pointing out his gray hairs becoming "more pronounced" — using self-deprecating humor about aging.
- He then uses a contrasting pair of expressions: it feels like "longer ago" (felt like a long time) and also like "the blink of an eye" (felt very fast) — capturing the common feeling that time can seem both long and short simultaneously.
- Both actors describe the experience as a "bucket list" opportunity and express deep gratitude to fans, showing how meaningful the project was to them personally.
