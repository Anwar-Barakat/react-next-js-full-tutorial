# Learn English with Tarzan Guide

A comprehensive English lesson based on scenes from Disney's Tarzan (1999), covering informal expressions (I'll handle this, itsy-bitsy, hang out, catch up), advice and warning phrases (take it from me, stay back, I'm warning you, how dare you), phrasal verbs (get off, catch up), idioms (it serves you right, take kindly to), connected speech reductions ("gotta" from "got to," "get a" becoming "geta"), and cultural themes about Tarzan's language learning, informal size emphasis, and jungle-meets-civilization comedy.

## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **I'll handle this** — Means you will take care of a situation, especially a delicate or difficult one. It reassures others that you've got things under control.
  - *Example: I'll handle this, guys, okay?*
  - *Example: I'll handle it.*

- **Itsy-bitsy** — An informal, playful way to say "very small." Often used with other size words for emphasis: "a tiny little itsy-bitsy problem."
  - *Example: We got a tiny little itsy-bitsy problem here, okay?*

- **Aggravation** — Trouble, difficulty, or annoyance. Used when describing a situation that causes frustration.
  - *Example: Who needs this aggravation, you know?*
  - *Example: You don't need this aggravation.*

- **Tickle** — To touch someone lightly in a way that causes a slightly uncomfortable sensation and makes them want to laugh.
  - *Example: Honey, stop! That tickles!*

## 4. Warnings & Commands

- **Stay back / Don't come any closer** — Used to warn someone to keep their distance. Jane uses these commands when she feels threatened by Tarzan.
  - *Example: Stay back! Don't come in! Don't come any closer!*

- **Get off** — Used to tell someone who is invading your personal space or standing too close to move away. Can also mean to physically remove yourself or someone from something.
  - *Example: No! Get off! Get out! Get, get off!*
  - *Example: Get off me!*

- **I'm warning you** — A phrase used to tell someone that there will be consequences if they continue their behavior.
  - *Example: I'm warning you! My father won't take kindly to you!*

- **How dare you!** — Used to express shock or disapproval when someone does something offensive or audacious. It means "How did you have the courage or guts to do this?"
  - *Example: How dare!*
  - *Example: How dare you! All of you, standing around deciding my future! I am not a prize to be won!*

## 5. Expressions & Idioms

- **Hang out (with someone)** — To spend time with someone, usually doing fun or casual things together. Very commonly used among friends, regardless of location.
  - *Example: Personally, I'd love to hang out with you.*
  - *Example: The Takamoto Bowl, that's where Scoob and Shaggy hang out.*

- **Take it from me** — Used when telling someone to listen to your advice, usually because you have experience or knowledge about the subject.
  - *Example: Listen, take it from me. Go home, okay?*
  - *Example: Take it from me, boss, you are going to have one satisfied princess.*

- **Leave it to me** — Means "I'll take care of it" or "Trust me to handle this." Used when you want to reassure someone that you will manage the situation.
  - *Example: You see? Leave it to me. I told you.*

- **It serves you right** — Used when someone suffers a punishment or negative consequence that they deserve because of something they did.
  - *Example: It serves you right! Now you stay away from me like a very good wild man.*
  - *Example: Well, serves you right for spoiling people's best dreams!*

- **Take kindly to (someone/something)** — To accept or approve of a person or thing. Most often used in the negative form — "won't take kindly to" means someone will not be pleased or accepting.
  - *Example: My father won't take kindly to you!*

## 6. Connected Speech — "Gotta" & "Get a" Reductions

**1. "Gotta" — Reduction of "got to":**

- The phrase **"got to"** is commonly reduced to **"gotta"** in fast, everyday speech. When preceded by "do," the auxiliary "do" is pronounced with a flap sound, like **"duh."**
  - Full: **"What do you got to do?"**
  - Connected: **"What do ya gotta do?"**
  - *Example: You gotta go get a hair.*

**2. "Get a" — The article "a" reduces to "uh":**

- The article **"a"** after the verb **"get"** has the same sound as **"uh,"** so "get a" becomes **"geta"** (one smooth unit).
  - Full: **"You gotta go get a hair."**
  - Connected: **"You gotta go geta hair."**

**3. "Gonna" — Reduction of "going to":**

- As in many previous lessons, **"going to"** is reduced to **"gonna"** in casual speech.
  - *Example: I'm probably gonna leave soon myself.*

**Tip:** Understanding how natives reduce, cut, and connect words is crucial for comprehension. These reductions — "gotta," "gonna," and linking articles like "a" to the preceding verb — are among the most common patterns in spoken English.

## 7. The Phrasal Verb "Catch Up" — Two Meanings

The phrasal verb **"catch up"** has two common meanings:

- **Meaning 1 — To follow and reach someone:** "You go first, and I'll follow you soon." Used when someone is behind and will join the group later.
  - *Example: You go, I'll catch up, alright?*

- **Meaning 2 — To get together and exchange news:** To meet with someone you haven't talked to in a while in order to hear each other's news and updates.
  - *Example: We'll, uh, we'll catch up, okay? — Sure, I'm so proud of you.*

**Tip:** Context always tells you which meaning is intended. If someone is physically leaving and says "I'll catch up," they mean they'll follow. If old friends say "Let's catch up," they mean they want to talk and share what's been happening in their lives.

## 8. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 8.1 Tarzan — The Movie

- Tarzan (1999) is a Disney animated film based on the novel "Tarzan of the Apes" by Edgar Rice Burroughs. It tells the story of a human baby raised by gorillas in the African jungle after his parents are killed by a leopard.
- Tarzan grows up struggling to fit in with the gorillas, feeling different but not understanding why. When a group of English explorers — including Professor Porter and his daughter Jane — arrives in the jungle, Tarzan encounters humans for the first time and discovers his true origins.
- The film explores themes of identity, belonging, and the tension between two worlds. Tarzan must ultimately choose between the jungle family that raised him and the human world he was born into.
- The soundtrack by Phil Collins, including the Oscar-winning song "You'll Be in My Heart," became one of the best-selling Disney soundtracks of all time.

---

### 8.2 Tarzan Learns to Speak — The Name Scene

- The opening scene of the lesson shows one of the most iconic moments in the film: Tarzan and Jane's first attempt to communicate. Tarzan can only say his own name — "Tarzan" — while Jane tries to teach him hers.
- The humor comes from the back-and-forth repetition: Tarzan keeps saying "Tarzan" while Jane keeps correcting him with "Jane," until he finally understands.
- This scene illustrates one of the film's central themes: the challenge of communication across different worlds. Tarzan has grown up speaking only with gorillas and must learn human language from scratch.
- For English learners, this scene is a charming reminder that learning a new language always starts with the basics — names, simple words, patience, and repetition.

---

### 8.3 "Itsy-Bitsy" — Emphasizing Smallness

- Terk's phrase **"a tiny little itsy-bitsy problem"** is a great example of how English speakers stack multiple words together for emphasis.
- Each word individually means "small": **tiny**, **little**, and **itsy-bitsy**. Using all three together is deliberately exaggerated — Terk is trying to make the problem sound as insignificant as possible (even though it isn't).
- **"Itsy-bitsy"** (also spelled "itty-bitty") is an informal, playful word most often associated with children's language. It comes from the famous nursery rhyme "The Itsy Bitsy Spider."
- This technique of stacking synonyms for emphasis is common in informal English:
  - "A big huge enormous problem" (emphasizing largeness)
  - "A tiny little itsy-bitsy problem" (emphasizing smallness)
  - "A long, boring, never-ending meeting" (emphasizing duration)

---

### 8.4 "It Serves You Right" — Deserved Consequences

- Jane uses the expression **"It serves you right!"** after kicking Tarzan in the face. She means that Tarzan got what he deserved for invading her personal space.
- This expression is used when someone suffers a consequence that feels fair or justified based on their actions. It implies "you brought this on yourself."
- The expression comes from the idea of "serving" justice — as if the universe is delivering a punishment that fits the offense.
- Common usage patterns:
  - **"It serves you right for..."** + gerund: *"It serves you right for not listening."*
  - **"Serves you right!"** (shortened, more casual): *"Serves you right for spoiling people's best dreams!"*
- The humor in the Tarzan scene comes from the contrast: Jane is a proper, well-mannered Englishwoman using polite expressions while simultaneously kicking a wild man in the face — the clash between her refined language and the physical comedy is classic Disney humor.
