## 5. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Can't take it anymore** — Cannot deal with or accept something anymore, usually due to pain, irritation, or frustration.
  - *Example: You gotta make it stop! I can't take it anymore!*

- **Can't stand** — A similar expression meaning to strongly dislike or be unable to tolerate something.
  - *Example: I can't stand waiting in long lines.*

- **To complain** — To express dissatisfaction or annoyance about something.
  - *Example: I've seen things that didn't complain this much.*

- **Squirm** — To uncomfortably move from side to side in an awkward way, often due to nervousness, embarrassment, or pain.
  - *Example: Nobody spoke for at least five minutes, and Rachel squirmed in her chair with embarrassment.*

- **To lick** — To pass the tongue over the surface of something.
  - *Example: Someone should lick it, just in case.*

- **Thingy** — An informal word used to refer to something when you do not know or cannot remember its name.
  - *Example: He's wearing one of those baby thingies.*
  - *Example: Or I will mess with this gravity thingy.*

- **Diaper** — A piece of absorbent material wrapped around a baby's bottom to absorb waste. What Manny calls "one of those baby thingies."
  - *Example: The baby needs a diaper change.*

- **To poop** — An informal word meaning to defecate; to pass waste from the body.
  - *Example: So if he poops, where does it go?*

- **Disgusting** — Extremely unpleasant; causing a strong feeling of dislike or revulsion.
  - *Example: Humans are disgusting.*

- **Runt** — Literally: the smallest and weakest animal of a group born at the same time to the same mother. Used informally to refer to someone small and weak.
  - *Example: Returning the runt was your idea.*

- **Insignificant** — Too small or unimportant to be worth consideration.
  - *Example: Because you're small and insignificant.*

- **To pummel** — To hit someone or something repeatedly, especially with your fists.
  - *Example: I'll pummel you if you don't.*

- **To slip** — To slide accidentally, losing your balance.
  - *Example: I'm gonna slip!*

- **Towards** — Facing in the direction of; moving in the direction of.
  - *Example: She stood up and walked towards him.*
  - *Example: Turn him towards me.*

- **Peekaboo** — A game played with babies where you hide your face and suddenly reveal it, saying "peekaboo!" Also known as "where's the baby?"
  - *Example: Diego plays peekaboo with the baby: "Where's the baby? There he is!"*

- **To bet** — Literally: to risk money on a result. Informally: to be confident or certain about something.
  - *Example: I bet he's hungry.*

- **I bet** — An expression meaning "I believe" or "I expect," especially when you're confident or excited.
  - *Example: I bet she's the nicest, gentlest, warmest person ever.*

- **To lactate** — (Of a woman or female animal) to produce milk to feed a baby.
  - *Example: Well, I ain't exactly lactating right now, pal.*

- **Pal** — An informal word for friend, similar to "buddy" or "mate."
  - *Example: I ain't exactly lactating right now, pal.*

- **Food chain** — In biology: the series of living things connected because each group eats the group below it. Figuratively: a hierarchy or ranking system.
  - *Example: You're a little low on the food chain to be mouthing off to me.*

- **To mouth off** — To talk in an unpleasantly loud, boastful, or opinionated way; to talk back disrespectfully.
  - *Example: You're a little low on the food chain to be mouthing off to me.*

- **Junk food** — Food that is unhealthy because it contains a lot of fat, sugar, or artificial substances.
  - *Example: I don't eat junk food.*

- **Spit that out** — A command telling someone to remove something from their mouth.
  - *Example: Manny, when Diego — spit that out! You don't know where it's been!*

- **You don't know where it's been** — A common warning phrase, usually said to children, meaning an object could be dirty or contaminated.
  - *Example: Spit that out! You don't know where it's been!*

- **To wave** — To hold something up and move it rapidly from side to side.
  - *Example: You want to stop waving that thing around?*

- **Gotta** — Informal contraction of "got to" or "have got to," meaning must or need to.
  - *Example: You gotta make it stop!*

- **Gonna** — Informal contraction of "going to."
  - *Example: I'm gonna slip!*

## 6. Expressions & Idioms

- **Can't take it anymore** — Unable to endure or tolerate a situation any longer.
  - *Example: I can't take it anymore — make it stop!*

- **Just in case** — Done as a precaution; as a way of being safe from something that might happen, especially to avoid negative consequences.
  - *Example: Someone should lick it, just in case.*
  - *Example: I also packed your paintings of our best time together, just in case you get lonely.*

- **Cut it out** — An informal expression asking someone to stop doing something annoying or offensive.
  - *Example: Will you cut it out?*

- **Look out / Watch out** — A warning expression telling someone to be careful, especially when they're about to have an accident.
  - *Example: All right, look out! Look out!*

- **Coming through** — A phrase said or shouted when trying to pass through a crowded area, asking others to make way.
  - *Example: Look out! Coming through!*

- **How about** — Used to make a suggestion or offer.
  - *Example: How about some milk?*

- **Low on the food chain** — Figuratively: being of lower rank or importance in a hierarchy.
  - *Example: You're a little low on the food chain to be mouthing off to me.*

## 7. Interjections & Exclamations

- **My goodness** — An expression used to convey surprise. It's a softened alternative to "my God," used to avoid religious connotations.
  - *Example: I mean, my goodness!*

- **Ew / Yuck** — Sounds made by native English speakers to express strong distaste or disgust.
  - *Example: Ew! Humans are disgusting.*

## 8. Pronunciation — Word Linking & Sound Changes

In natural, fast American English speech, words are often linked together, causing sounds to change.

**1. Linking "where" + "does" — Soft D Sound:**
- When **"where"** and **"does"** are connected, the result is a softer D sound.
- **where does** → sounds like /wɛrdəz/

**2. Tap T Sound (Flap T):**
- When a T falls between two vowel sounds, it becomes a soft D-like tap.
- **does it** in this context also shows a related pattern
- Other examples: **water** → /wɑɾər/, **butter** → /bʌɾər/

**3. S-to-Z Sound Change:**
- When **"does"** and **"it"** are linked, the S sound in "does" moves into a **Z sound**.
- **does it** → /dʌzɪt/

**Full natural pronunciation of Manny's line:**
- "So if he poops, where does it go?" → sounds like "whereduzit go"

**Tip:** These sound changes happen naturally in native speech. Focus on listening for them rather than memorizing rules — the more you listen, the more natural they'll become.

## 9. Grammar — "Else" with Question Words

The word **"else"** means "additional" or "other." It is commonly paired with question words to ask about additional things:

- **What else?** — What other thing?
  - *Example: What else do you want?*
- **Who else?** — What other person?
  - *Example: Who else is coming to the party?*
- **Where else?** — What other place?
  - *Example: Where else have you looked?*
- **How else?** — In what other way?
  - *Example: How else can we solve this problem?*
- **Why else?** — For what other reason?
  - *Example: Why else would he do that?*

*Example from the movie: Sid wants to know what else (what other reason) he should check the baby's diaper.*

## 10. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 10.1 Ice Age — The Movie

- Ice Age (2002) is an animated film set during the Paleolithic ice age, following three prehistoric animals — Manny (a mammoth), Sid (a sloth), and Diego (a saber-toothed tiger) — who find a lost human baby and try to return him to his family.
- The film is known for its humor, much of which comes from the contrast between the tough prehistoric animals and their unexpected tenderness with the baby.
- The movie spawned multiple sequels and became one of the most popular animated franchises.

---

### 10.2 Informal Language in Ice Age

- Ice Age is packed with informal, everyday English, making it an excellent resource for learning how natives actually speak.
- Characters frequently use contractions like **"ain't"** (am not / is not / are not), **"gotta"** (got to), and **"gonna"** (going to).
- The word **"thingy"** is a perfect example of how natives handle forgotten words — instead of pausing, they substitute with a placeholder word.
- Be careful: while these informal words are very common in spoken English, using them in formal contexts (job interviews, academic writing) may make you sound uneducated.

---

### 10.3 Softening Religious Words

- Native English speakers often soften words related to religion in exclamations.
- Instead of saying **"my God"** (which some consider taking God's name in vain), they use alternatives like:
  - **My goodness**
  - **Oh my gosh**
  - **Oh my word**
  - **Goodness gracious**
- These softened versions are considered more polite and appropriate in most social settings.

---

### 10.4 Wet Noses in Animals vs Humans

- In the movie, Sid checks the baby's nose and worries because it's dry.
- For animals like dogs, cats, and other canines and felines, a **wet nose** indicates good health. A dry nose can be a sign of illness.
- However, for **humans**, a dry nose is perfectly normal and healthy.
- The humor comes from the animals applying their own health standards to a human baby — they don't understand that human babies are supposed to have dry noses.
