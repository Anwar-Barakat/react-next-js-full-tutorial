## 1. Scene 1 — Remy Teaches About Food

- **Remy:** I brought you something to— no, no, no, no, no! Spit that out right now! I have got to teach you about food.
- **Remy:** Close your eyes. Now, take a bite of this. No, no, no — don't just fork it down!
- **Remy:** Chew it slowly. Only think about the taste. Creamy, salty, sweet — and oaky nuttiness. You detect that?
- **Emile:** Oh, I'm detecting nuttiness.
- **Remy:** Close your eyes. Now taste this. A whole different thing, right? Sweet, crisp, slight tang on the finish. Okay — now try them together.
- **Emile:** I think I'm getting a little something there. It might be the nuttiness.
- **Remy:** See? Could be the tang. That's it! Now, imagine every great taste in the world being combined into infinite combinations. Tastes that no one has tried yet — discoveries to be made!
- **Emile:** I think— uh-huh. You lost me again.

## 2. Scene 2 — Ego's Review

- **Colette:** It's a peasant dish. Are you sure you want to serve this to Ego?
- **Linguini:** What? I am making ratatouille.
- **Ego:** They must be joking.
- **Ego:** I can't remember the last time I asked a waiter to give my compliments to the chef. And now I find myself in the extraordinary position of having my waiter be the chef.
- **Linguini:** Thanks, but I'm just your waiter tonight.
- **Ego:** Then who do I thank for the meal? Uh, excuse me a minute.
- **Ego:** You must be the chef. If you wish to meet the chef, you'll have to wait until all the other customers have gone.
- **Ego:** So be it.
- **Ego (review):** Last night, I experienced something new — an extraordinary meal from a singularly unexpected source. To say that both the meal and its maker have challenged my preconceptions about fine cooking is a gross understatement. They have rocked me to my core. In the past, I have made no secret of my disdain for Chef Gusteau's famous motto: "Anyone can cook." Not everyone can become a great artist, but a great artist can come from anywhere. It is difficult to imagine more humble origins than those of the genius now cooking at Gusteau's, who is, in this critic's opinion, nothing less than the finest chef in France. I will be returning to Gusteau's soon, hungry for more.

## 4. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **To spit (something) out** — To forcefully push something out of your mouth.
  - *Example: Spit that out right now!*

- **To fork (something) down** — To eat something very quickly without savoring it.
  - *Example: Don't just fork it down!*

- **To chew** — To bite food several times in your mouth before swallowing.
  - *Example: Chew it slowly. Only think about the taste.*
  - *Example: This meat's so tough I can hardly chew it.*

- **To swallow** — To make food or drink go from your mouth down your throat to your stomach.
  - *Example: Remy is saying he should savor the flavors, not just quickly swallow them.*

- **To savor** — To enjoy food or drink slowly, appreciating the taste fully.
  - *Example: He should savor the flavors of the food.*

- **To detect** — To discover or notice something, especially something that is not easy to see, hear, or taste.
  - *Example: Oaky nuttiness — you detect that?*

- **To try** — In the context of food: to put a piece of food in your mouth and taste it to see if you like it.
  - *Example: Okay, now try them together.*

- **Compliment** — A remark that shows you admire someone or something. Can also be used as a verb.
  - *Example: I asked a waiter to give my compliments to the chef.*

- **To serve** — To give someone food or drink, especially as part of a meal or in a restaurant.
  - *Example: Are you sure you want to serve this to Ego?*

- **Waiter** — A person whose job is to serve food and drinks at the tables in a restaurant.
  - *Example: I'm just your waiter tonight.*

- **Chef** — A professional cook, especially the head cook in a restaurant.
  - *Example: You must be the chef.*

- **Credibility** — The quality of being trusted and believed in.
  - *Example: Ego lost his job and his credibility.*

- **Investor** — A person who puts money into something (a business, project, etc.) hoping to make a profit.
  - *Example: He's doing very well as a small business investor.*

- **Predictable / Unpredictability** — Predictable: able to be expected or foreseen. Unpredictability: the quality of being impossible to predict.
  - *Example: The only thing predictable about life is its unpredictability.*

## 5. Food & Cooking Vocabulary

- **Take a bite (of something)** — To take a small piece of food into your mouth. When asking someone, you can also say "have a bite."
  - *Example: Now, take a bite of this.*
  - *Example: Can I have a bite of that?*

- **Taste (noun)** — The flavor of food or drink.
  - *Example: Only think about the taste.*

- **Taste (verb)** — The action of experiencing the flavor of food.
  - *Example: Now taste this.*

- **Flavor** — The particular taste of a food or drink.
  - *Example: Each flavor was totally unique.*

- **Creamy** — Having a smooth, rich texture like cream.
  - *Example: Creamy, salty, sweet.*

- **Salty** — Having the taste of salt.
  - *Example: Creamy, salty, sweet.*

- **Sweet** — Having the taste of sugar or honey.
  - *Example: Sweet, crisp, slight tang on the finish.*

- **Crisp / Crispy** — Having a firm, crunchy texture that breaks easily.
  - *Example: Sweet, crisp, slight tang on the finish.*

- **Tang / Tangy** — A taste or smell that is pleasantly strong or sharp, often from citrus or vinegar.
  - *Example: Slight tang on the finish.*
  - *Example: This sauce is so tangy and delicious.*

- **Nutty / Nuttiness** — Having a flavor similar to nuts. "Nuttiness" is the noun form.
  - *Example: Oaky nuttiness — you detect that?*

- **Oaky** — Having a flavor associated with oak wood, often used to describe wine or aged food.
  - *Example: Oaky nuttiness.*

- **Fruity** — Having a taste or flavor like fruit.
  - *Example: It's a bold flavor — fruity, a little earthy.*

- **Earthy** — Having a flavor that tastes like earth or soil; natural and robust.
  - *Example: Fruity, a little earthy.*

- **Dish** — A type of food that is cooked in a particular way and served as part of a meal. Also: a container for serving food.
  - *Example: It's a peasant dish.*
  - *Example: Spaghetti al pomodoro is a traditional Italian dish.*

- **Cuisine** — A style or method of cooking, especially associated with a particular country or region.
  - *Example: Other terms for fine cooking are gourmet food and high-class cuisine.*

- **Gourmet** — Relating to high-quality, refined food and cooking.
  - *Example: Gourmet food is the opposite of peasant food.*

## 6. Formal & Sophisticated Vocabulary

From Ego's review — examples of elevated, formal English:

- **Extraordinary** — Very unusual or remarkable; exceptionally good.
  - *Example: An extraordinary meal from a singularly unexpected source.*

- **Singularly** — In a way that is very noticeable or unusual; remarkably.
  - *Example: A singularly unexpected source.*
  - *Example: He is singularly qualified for the job.*

- **Preconceptions** — Ideas or opinions formed before having enough knowledge or experience.
  - *Example: Both the meal and its maker have challenged my preconceptions about fine cooking.*

- **Understatement** — A statement that is not strong enough to express how good, bad, or impressive something really is. "Gross understatement" intensifies this.
  - *Example: To say they have challenged my preconceptions is a gross understatement.*
  - *Example: To say the movie was bad is an understatement.*

- **Disdain** — A formal synonym for strong dislike or contempt.
  - *Example: I have made no secret of my disdain for Chef Gusteau's famous motto.*

- **Motto** — A short sentence or phrase that expresses a belief or gives a rule on how to behave.
  - *Example: Chef Gusteau's famous motto: "Anyone can cook."*
  - *Example: The school's motto was "work hard and play hard."*

- **Humble origins** — Coming from a simple, modest, or low social background.
  - *Example: It is difficult to imagine more humble origins.*

- **Critic** — A person who judges or evaluates something, especially food, art, movies, or books, professionally.
  - *Example: In this critic's opinion, nothing less than the finest chef in France.*

- **Peasant** — A word historically used to refer to poor farming families. Used to describe food that is simple and unsophisticated.
  - *Example: It's a peasant dish.*

## 7. Expressions & Idioms

- **You lost me** — I don't understand what you mean anymore; I can't follow your explanation.
  - *Example: You lost me again.*

- **So be it** — An expression of acceptance or resignation; "I accept this, even if I don't like it."
  - *Example: If you wish to wait, so be it.*

- **Give my compliments to (someone)** — To express admiration or praise to someone, usually through another person. Also: "pay someone a compliment."
  - *Example: I asked a waiter to give my compliments to the chef.*

- **Nothing less than** — Used to emphasize how great something is; at least as important as.
  - *Example: Nothing less than the finest chef in France.*

- **Nothing short of** — Similar to "nothing less than"; describes something extreme (good or bad).
  - *Example: The party was nothing short of a disaster.*

- **Rocked me to my core** — Affected me in a very profound way; provided a big revelation or surprise.
  - *Example: They have rocked me to my core.*

- **Made no secret of** — Always said publicly; never hidden one's opinion.
  - *Example: I have made no secret of my disdain.*

- **To let (someone) loose** — To set someone free; to release someone who was restrained.
  - *Example: We had to let Skinner and the health inspector loose.*

- **To rat (someone) out** — To tell someone in authority about something wrong that another person has done; to betray or inform on someone.
  - *Example: They ratted us out.*

- **It got out** — A secret became known by everyone.
  - *Example: Once it got out there were rats in the kitchen, the restaurant was closed.*

- **Must be joking / They must be joking** — An expression of disbelief or surprise about what someone is doing.
  - *Example: They must be joking.*

## 8. Grammar — Adding "-y" and "-ness" to Describe Food

When describing food, it's very common to turn **nouns into adjectives** by adding **-y** and then turn those adjectives into **nouns** by adding **-ness**:

**Noun → Adjective (-y):**
- **cream** → **creamy** (having a smooth, rich texture)
- **salt** → **salty** (tasting of salt)
- **nut** → **nutty** (tasting of nuts)
- **oak** → **oaky** (tasting of oak)
- **fruit** → **fruity** (tasting of fruit)
- **earth** → **earthy** (tasting like earth)

**Adjective → Noun (-ness):**
- **creamy** → **creaminess**
- **salty** → **saltiness**
- **nutty** → **nuttiness**

*Example: We might describe cheese by talking about the creaminess and saltiness.*

**Tip:** This word modification pattern is extremely useful for describing food, wine, coffee, and cooking in English.

## 9. Grammar — "Must" for Strong Certainty

The modal verb **"must"** has two main uses:

**1. Obligation (stronger than "have to"):**
- *All passengers must wear seat belts.*

**2. Strong certainty (you're very sure about something, especially because of evidence):**
- *You must be the chef.* (I'm certain you are the chef.)
- *They must be joking.* (I'm certain they're joking.)
- *You must be tired.* (I'm sure you are tired.)
- *You must be the dragon warrior.*

**From the movie:** Ego says *"You must be the chef"* — he is confident because of the evidence (the extraordinary meal).

---

## 10. Grammar — Emphasis with Uncontracted Forms

Native speakers often **contract words** in casual speech:
- "have got" → "gotta"
- "cannot" → "can't"

However, when expressing **strong emotion** (surprise, anger, emphasis), speakers deliberately use the **full, uncontracted form** to make their point stronger:

- **Casual:** *"You can't use your phone in class."*
- **Emphatic:** *"You cannot use your phone in class."*

- **Casual:** *"I gotta go now."*
- **Emphatic:** *"I have got to go now."*

**From the movie:** Remy says *"I have got to teach you about food"* — using the full form to emphasize how urgently he needs to do this.

## 11. Pronunciation — "Great" and Weakening of "Of"

**1. The G in "great" sounds like K:**
- In natural speech, the G in **"great"** often sounds more like a K sound.
- *"It was a great night"* → the G sounds almost like /kreɪt/

**2. Weakening of "of":**
- The word **"of"** is often reduced to just **"uh"** /ə/ in native speech and connects to the preceding word.
- **"happiest of my life"** → sounds like "happiest-uh my life"

**Tip:** These are subtle details of native speech. You don't need to force them — just pay attention when listening to natives and you'll start noticing them naturally.

## 12. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 12.1 Ratatouille — The Movie

- Ratatouille (2007) is a Disney Pixar animated film about Remy, a rat with an extraordinary sense of taste and smell who dreams of becoming a chef in Paris.
- Remy teams up with Linguini, a clumsy garbage boy at the famous restaurant Gusteau's, to cook incredible meals — with Remy hiding under Linguini's chef hat and controlling his movements.
- The film's central theme is Chef Gusteau's motto: "Anyone can cook" — meaning talent can come from the most unexpected places.
- The movie culminates with the fearsome food critic Anton Ego being won over by a simple peasant dish: ratatouille.

---

### 12.2 Ratatouille — The Dish

- Ratatouille is a traditional French vegetable dish from Provence, typically made with tomatoes, zucchini, eggplant, bell peppers, onions, and herbs.
- The movie's title is a clever wordplay combining the name of this French recipe with "rat" — since the main character is a rat who cooks.
- In the film, Colette calls it "a peasant dish," meaning it's simple home cooking, not sophisticated haute cuisine — which makes serving it to the most feared food critic in Paris a bold and risky choice.

---

### 12.3 Fine Dining vs Peasant Food

- The movie explores the tension between **fine dining** (gourmet, high-class cuisine) and **peasant food** (simple, humble dishes).
- Ego's review reveals the film's message: great food doesn't have to be expensive or sophisticated — it can come from humble origins and still be extraordinary.
- Terms for upscale food: **gourmet**, **fine dining**, **haute cuisine**, **high-class cuisine**
- Terms for simple food: **peasant dish**, **home cooking**, **comfort food**

---

### 12.4 The "Nuttiness" Pun

- When Remy says *"Oaky nuttiness — you detect that?"*, Emile responds: *"Oh, I'm detecting nuttiness."*
- This is a play on words (double entendre):
  - **Literal meaning:** Emile can taste the nutty flavor in the food.
  - **Figurative meaning:** "Nutty" and "nuts" are slang for "crazy" — so Emile is also joking that Remy seems crazy for being so obsessed with food flavors.
- This type of humor — where a word has both a literal and slang meaning — is very common in English comedy.
