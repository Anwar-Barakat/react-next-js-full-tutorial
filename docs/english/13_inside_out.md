## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Emotions** — Feelings such as joy, sadness, anger, disgust, and fear. In the movie, each emotion is represented by a character inside a person's mind.
  - *Example: We're Riley's emotions.*

- **Memory / Memories** — Things remembered from the past. In the movie, memories are represented as glowing spheres stored in Riley's mind.
  - *Example: These are Riley's memories. They're mostly happy.*

- **Not to brag** — A phrase used before saying something you're proud of, to appear modest.
  - *Example: They're mostly happy — you'll notice. Not to brag.*

- **Curse word** — A rude or offensive word; a swear word.
  - *Example: Can I say that curse word now?*

- **To pick up on (something)** — To detect or notice something that is not very obvious; to become aware of it.
  - *Example: Did you guys pick up on that? Something's wrong.*
  - *Example: He didn't pick up on her lack of interest in the subject.*

- **Support** — Help or assistance.
  - *Example: We'll need support. Signal the husband.*

- **To signal** — To make a specific gesture or sound in order to send someone a particular message they can interpret.
  - *Example: Signal the husband.*

- **Signal (noun)** — The gesture, sound, or sign used to communicate a message.
  - *Example: We'll need support — signal the husband.*

- **Garbage night / Garbage day** — The evening or day when people place their garbage outside their homes for collection by the waste service.
  - *Example: Is it garbage night?*

- **Toilet seat** — The part of the toilet you sit on. "Leaving the toilet seat up" (after using it standing) is considered rude or disrespectful, especially by women sharing the same bathroom.
  - *Example: We left the toilet seat up.*

- **To roll your eyes** — To move your eyes around in a circle, typically to express frustration, annoyance, or contempt about something someone said or did.
  - *Example: She just rolled her eyes at us.*

- **Deal** — In the phrase "what's your deal" or "what is her deal," it means "what is the problem" or "why are you acting this way." Similar to "what's the matter with you."
  - *Example: What is her deal?*

- **Attitude** — A way of feeling or acting in response to a person, thing, or situation. Also used to describe a forceful or aggressive style of behavior.
  - *Example: I do not like this new attitude.*

- **To leave someone alone** — To stop bothering or annoying someone. It does not necessarily mean leaving the room.
  - *Example: What is your problem? Just leave me alone!*

- **Sass / Sassy** — Sass is the act of being disrespectful in a lively, confident way. A sassy person talks back boldly and rudely.
  - *Example: Sir, reporting high levels of sass.*

- **Disrespectful** — Showing a lack of respect or courtesy.
  - *Example: I don't know where this disrespectful attitude came from.*

- **Young lady** — A way parents commonly address their daughter when disciplining her or when they are upset or angry.
  - *Example: Listen, young lady, I don't know where this disrespectful attitude came from.*

- **Pops** — An informal way to call your father.
  - *Example: You wanna piece of this, Pops?*

- **Disaster** — A sudden event causing great damage or difficulty; something that goes very wrong.
  - *Example: That could have been a disaster. — Well, that was a disaster.*

- **Insights** — A deep understanding of something; the ability to see the true nature of a situation.
  - *Example: Inside Out gives us some insights into human emotions and psychology.*

- **To interact** — To communicate or work together; to act upon each other.
  - *Example: We see how all these emotions interact and control each person's actions.*

- **To escalate** — To increase in intensity, seriousness, or severity.
  - *Example: As the situation escalates, they prepare to put the foot down.*

- **To discipline** — To train someone to follow rules or a code of behavior, especially by punishing them for breaking rules.
  - *Example: Parents call their daughter "young lady" when trying to discipline her.*

- **Tantrum** — An outburst of anger or frustration, especially by a child.
  - *Example: Enjoy your little tantrum, diaper baby.*

- **To provoke** — To deliberately make someone angry or to challenge them.
  - *Example: "You wanna piece of this?" is said to provoke somebody into a fight.*

## 4. Expressions & Idioms

- **Pick up on (something)** — To detect or notice something that is not obvious.
  - *Example: Did you guys pick up on that?*
  - *Example: The enemy submarine was picked up on the radar.*

- **Put your foot down** — To use your authority to make somebody do or stop something; to take a firm stand.
  - *Example: I don't want to have to put the foot down.*

- **Show of force** — Literally: a military act of displaying weapons or power to intimidate an opponent. Figuratively: displaying authority or power without necessarily using it.
  - *Example: All right, make a show of force.*

- **What is your deal? / What's your deal?** — Asking why someone is acting in a certain way; what is their problem. Similar to "what's the matter with you?"
  - *Example: What is her deal?*

- **Leave me alone** — Stop bothering or annoying me.
  - *Example: Just leave me alone!*

- **You wanna piece of this?** — An informal, confrontational phrase used to provoke someone into a fight.
  - *Example: You wanna piece of this, Pops?*

- **Not to brag** — Said before mentioning something you're proud of, to appear humble.
  - *Example: They're mostly happy — not to brag.*

- **I guess** — Used to express uncertainty, reluctance, or a lack of enthusiasm.
  - *Example: It was fine, I guess.*

## 5. Military & War Parody Vocabulary

The father's emotions are portrayed like soldiers in a military war room, creating a humorous parody. These military terms appear throughout the scene:

- **Sir** — The formal way soldiers address superior officers in the military.
  - *Example: Sir, she just rolled her eyes at us.*

- **DEFCON** — Stands for "Defense Readiness Condition." One of five levels of US military defense status, ranked from 5 (lowest threat) to 1 (highest threat / nuclear war). Used in the movie humorously to describe how seriously the father's emotions take Riley's sass.
  - *Example: Take it to DEFCON 2.*

- **Show of force** — A military display of power or weapons to warn or intimidate an opponent.
  - *Example: Make a show of force.*

- **On your command** — A military phrase meaning "ready to execute when you give the order."
  - *Example: Ready. Wait — on your command, sir.*

- **Fire** — The command to launch an attack, shoot weapons, or in this case, deliver the punishment.
  - *Example: Fire! — "That's it! Go to your room!"*

- **Reporting** — Formally communicating information to a superior, as soldiers do.
  - *Example: Sir, reporting high levels of sass.*

- **Gentlemen** — A formal address used for a group of men, commonly heard in military contexts.
  - *Example: Good job, gentlemen.*

- **Launch position / Launch sequence** — The process of preparing and launching a missile. In the movie, it parodies the preparation of "putting the foot down."
  - *Example: Keys to launch position. Ready.*

## 6. Connected Speech — Linking & Sound Changes

In the line **"Sir, she just rolled her eyes at us"**, several connected speech patterns occur when spoken at native speed:

**1. S-to-Z Sound Change:**
- The **S sound** in **"eyes"** becomes a **Z sound** when linking to "at."
- **eyes at** → /aɪzæt/

**2. T-to-Soft-D Sound Change (Flap T):**
- The **T sound** in **"at"** becomes a **soft D sound** when linking to "us."
- **at us** → /æɾəs/ (sounds like "ad-us")

**Full natural pronunciation:**
- "Sir, she just rolled her eyes at us" → native speakers connect all four final words smoothly: "eyezadus"

**Why this matters:**
- Connected speech is the natural tendency of linking, cutting, and reducing words in spoken English.
- If you're not familiar with these patterns, fast native speech can sound impossible to understand.
- Practice listening at half speed first, then gradually increase to full speed.

**More examples of the Flap T:**
- **water** → /wɑɾər/ (sounds like "wadder")
- **butter** → /bʌɾər/ (sounds like "budder")
- **got it** → /ɡɑɾɪt/ (sounds like "god-it")

## 7. Grammar — "You Guys" as Plural "You"

In American English, **"you guys"** is commonly used as a plural form of "you" to address a group of people — **even if the group includes women**.

- The word **"guy"** on its own informally refers to a man.
- But **"you guys"** is gender-neutral when addressing a group.

**From the movie:**
- All of the mother's emotions are female characters, yet Joy says: *"Did you guys pick up on that?"*

**More examples:**
- *You guys are the best!*
- *Are you guys coming to the party?*
- *What do you guys think?*

**Note:** In some regions, alternatives like **"y'all"** (Southern US) or **"youse"** (New York/Philadelphia) serve the same purpose.

## 8. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 8.1 Inside Out — The Movie

- Inside Out (2015) is a Disney Pixar animated film that takes place inside the mind of an 11-year-old girl named Riley.
- Every person has five emotions represented by individual characters living inside their head: **Joy**, **Sadness**, **Anger**, **Disgust**, and **Fear**.
- These emotions control the person's reactions, memories, and personality through a "control room" inside the mind.
- The movie explores how emotions work together (and sometimes conflict) to shape our experiences and responses to life events.

---

### 8.2 The War Room Parody

- The dinner argument scene is a brilliant parody of war and action movies.
- The father's emotions operate like soldiers in a military **war room**, using terms like "sir," "DEFCON," "show of force," "launch position," and "fire."
- The humor comes from the contrast between the mundane situation (a family dinner argument) and the over-the-top military language and procedures.
- "Putting the foot down" (a parenting expression) is treated like launching a missile, complete with a full launch sequence and countdown.

---

### 8.3 Parent-Child Discipline Language

- The scene showcases common language parents use when disciplining children:
  - **"Young lady"** — used to address a daughter seriously when upset
  - **"Go to your room"** — a classic punishment for children
  - **"I do not like this new attitude"** — expressing disapproval of behavior
  - **"Put the foot down"** — using parental authority firmly
- Riley's responses also demonstrate typical pre-teen reactions:
  - **Rolling her eyes** — showing frustration nonverbally
  - **"Leave me alone"** — wanting to be left unbothered
  - **"What is your problem?"** — expressing defiance
  - **"You wanna piece of this?"** — escalating the confrontation

---

### 8.4 The Mother's Mind vs The Father's Mind

- Pixar put incredible detail into the design of each character's mind:
  - The **mother's emotions** are all female and operate in a calm, organized manner, trying to understand what's wrong with Riley.
  - The **father's emotions** are all male and operate like a military unit, led by Anger, responding to situations with a war-like approach.
- The father's emotions are easily distracted (thinking about garbage night or the toilet seat) while the mother's emotions are focused on Riley.
- These contrasting control rooms reveal personality differences between the parents and add humor through stereotypical portrayals of how men and women process situations differently.
