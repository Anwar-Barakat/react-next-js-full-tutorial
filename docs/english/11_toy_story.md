## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Pull my string** — An expression invented for the movie. Woody has a string on his back that makes him talk when pulled. He uses it like "pinch me" — an expression of surprise or disbelief.
  - *Example: Pull my string — the birthday party's today!*

- **Pinch me** — An expression used when you find something hard to believe. Comes from the full phrase "pinch me, I must be dreaming."
  - *Example: I just got promoted? Pinch me!*

- **Coast is clear** — Used to say there is no danger of being observed or caught; it's safe to proceed.
  - *Example: Okay everybody, coast is clear.*

- **Ages three and up** — A label on toy packaging indicating the minimum age for safe play.
  - *Example: It's on my box — ages three and up.*

- **Babysit / Babysitter** — To take care of a child, usually when the parents are out. A babysitter is the person who does this.
  - *Example: I'm not supposed to be babysitting princess drool.*

- **Drool** — Saliva that drops from the mouth uncontrollably, common in babies. Can be used as both a noun and a verb.
  - *Example: The baby drooled all over his new shirt.*

- **Spoiled** — (Of a child) treated too well and allowed to have everything they want, resulting in bad behavior.
  - *Example: The girl can sometimes be referred to as a princess if she is spoiled.*

- **Uncultured** — Lacking culture, education, or refinement; ignorant.
  - *Example: You uncultured swine!*

- **Swine** — A formal or literary word for pig. Used as an insult to call someone dirty or unpleasant.
  - *Example: Calling someone an uncultured swine means calling them ignorant.*

- **Ya** — A common informal shortening of the pronoun "you."
  - *Example: What are you looking at, ya hockey puck?*

- **Gather up** — A phrasal verb meaning to get everyone to come together in one place.
  - *Example: Just gather everyone up for a staff meeting.*

- **Staff** — The group of people who work in an organization or team.
  - *Example: The staff meeting is at 3 PM today.*

- **Staff meeting** — A meeting of all the members of a team or group; a reunion of the whole team.
  - *Example: Gather everyone up for a staff meeting.*

- **Podium** — A raised platform or area from where a person speaks to a large number of people.
  - *Example: The speaker walked up to the podium and began her speech.*

- **Duty** — The things that someone is responsible for doing; an obligation.
  - *Example: Snake, Robot — podium duty.*

- **Draw** — Most commonly means to create a picture. Also means to pull out a weapon (especially a gun) quickly.
  - *Example: The cowboy was the fastest to draw his gun.*

- **Knobs** — Small round handles or dials used to control something. On an Etch A Sketch toy, knobs control the drawing.
  - *Example: Turn the knob to change the volume.*

- **Doodle pad** — A pad used for drawing casual pictures or scribbles. Refers to the Etch A Sketch toy in the movie.
  - *Example: Hey, who moved my doodle pad way over here?*

- **Fearsome** — Frightening, especially in appearance; inspiring fear.
  - *Example: Going for fearsome here but I just don't feel it.*

- **Coming off as** — Appearing to others as; giving the impression of being something.
  - *Example: I think I'm just coming off as annoying.*

- **Flock** — A group of sheep, goats, or birds.
  - *Example: I wanted to thank you, Woody, for saving my flock.*

- **Shepherdess** — A woman who guards and guides sheep.
  - *Example: Bo Peep is a shepherdess — a woman who guards and guides sheep.*

- **Block** — A unit of distance in towns or cities, measured from one street intersection to the next.
  - *Example: The store is just a couple of blocks away.*

- **Shelf** — A flat horizontal surface attached to a wall or part of a piece of furniture, used for putting things on.
  - *Example: Everybody hear me? Up on the shelf — can you hear me?*

- **Item** — One thing in a group or list. In meetings, each topic on the agenda is called an item.
  - *Example: First item today — has everyone picked a moving buddy?*

- **Buddy** — An informal word for a close friend.
  - *Example: Has everyone picked a moving buddy?*

- **Left behind** — Remaining in a place after others have gone; abandoned or forgotten.
  - *Example: I don't want any toys left behind.*

- **Corrosion** — The process by which metal is gradually destroyed by chemical reactions (rust, oxidation). Used humorously for plastic toys.
  - *Example: Tuesday night's plastic corrosion awareness meeting was a big success.*

- **Minor** — Small and not very important or serious.
  - *Example: One minor note here — Andy's birthday party has been moved to today.*

- **Kindergarten** — A school or class for young children, usually ages 4–6, before they start formal education.
  - *Example: He's been Andy's favorite since kindergarten.*

- **Darn it** — A mild exclamation of frustration or emphasis. A polite substitute for "damn it."
  - *Example: If Woody says it's all right, then well darn it, it's good enough for me.*

- **To steer** — To control the direction of movement (a car, horse, boat, or figuratively a person/group).
  - *Example: Woody has never steered us wrong before.*

- **Steering wheel** — The wheel in a car or vehicle used to control its direction.
  - *Example: Keep both hands on the steering wheel while driving.*

- **To steer someone wrong** — To lead someone in the wrong direction or give bad advice.
  - *Example: He has never steered us wrong before.*

- **Rejection** — The act of being pushed aside, refused, or not accepted.
  - *Example: I just don't think I can take that kind of rejection.*

- **Replaced** — Substituted by something or someone new that takes your position.
  - *Example: No one's getting replaced. This is Andy we're talking about.*

- **To go through (something)** — To experience something, especially something difficult or unpleasant.
  - *Example: Every Christmas and birthday we go through this.*

- **Pun** — A joke that exploits different meanings of a word or words that sound alike but have different meanings.
  - *Example: Ham's name is a pun because ham is a product made from pigs.*

- **Parody** — A humorous imitation of something serious, especially a movie, book, or style.
  - *Example: The scene works as a parody of old western-style movies.*

- **Sincere** — Genuine and honest; meaning what you say.
  - *Example: We can hear by his tone he is not really being sincere.*

- **Tease** — To make fun of someone in a playful, friendly way.
  - *Example: Woody teases him in a friendly manner with a western movie reference.*

- **Piggy bank** — A small container, often shaped like a pig, where children save coins.
  - *Example: Hamm is a piggy bank — a toy where kids can save coins.*

## 4. Puns & Wordplay

- **"Ya hockey puck"** — Works as a double meaning. "Hockey puck" is slang for calling someone foolish. But Mr. Potato Head is literally talking to a hockey puck (the disc used in ice hockey), so it's both an insult and a factual statement.
  - *Example: What are you looking at, ya hockey puck?*

- **"Fastest knobs in the West"** — A parody of the old western movie phrase "fastest gun in the West." The Etch A Sketch toy is controlled by knobs (small dials), and he "draws" a gun (both meanings: sketches a picture and pulls out a weapon). So Woody teases him by combining both meanings.
  - *Example: You've been working on that, drawers — fastest knobs in the West!*

- **"Princess drool"** — Mr. Potato Head's nickname for baby Molly. "Princess" because she's well looked after, and "drool" because babies drool a lot.
  - *Example: I'm not supposed to be babysitting princess drool.*

- **"Uncultured swine"** — A double pun. The phrase means "an ignorant person," but Ham is literally a pig (swine), making the insult both figurative and literal.
  - *Example: You uncultured swine!*

- **"A couple of blocks away"** — "Blocks" is a unit of distance in cities, but Bo Peep says this while walking next to toy blocks, creating a visual pun.
  - *Example: Remember, I'm just a couple of blocks away.*

- **Hamm's name** — The character is a piggy bank (shaped like a pig), and his name is "Hamm" — ham is a food product made from pigs.

## 5. Military Expressions

- **Sarge** — An informal abbreviation of "Sergeant," a middle-rank officer in the armed forces. Woody calls the army man toy by this nickname.
  - *Example: Hey Sarge, have you seen Slinky?*

- **Sir, no sir / Sir, yes sir** — A formal way soldiers respond to a superior officer in the military. "Sir" is used before and after the response.
  - *Example: Have you seen Slinky? — Sir, no sir.*

- **At ease** — A military command that allows a soldier to adopt a relaxed standing position. Informally, it can be used to tell someone to relax.
  - *Example: Okay, thank you. At ease.*

## 6. Expressions & Idioms

- **Pull my string** — (Invented for the movie) Woody's version of "pinch me" — expressing surprise or disbelief. Based on his pull-string voice mechanism.
  - *Example: Pull my string — the birthday party's today!*

- **Coast is clear** — It is safe to proceed; there is no danger of being seen or caught.
  - *Example: Okay everybody, coast is clear.*

- **Losing her marbles** — Going crazy; acting irrationally. An informal expression.
  - *Example: This is Mom losing her marbles — obviously she wanted to have the party before the move.*

- **Good enough for me** — An expression indicating that you accept something as satisfactory.
  - *Example: If Woody says it's all right, then darn it, it's good enough for me.*

- **What do you say** — An informal way to suggest or propose something.
  - *Example: What do you say I get someone else to watch the sheep tonight?*

- **I was close to being (something)** — Almost reaching a state but not quite.
  - *Example: I was close to being scared that time.*

- **Putting that on** — Organizing or hosting an event.
  - *Example: We want to thank Mr. Spell for putting that on for us.*

## 7. Connected Speech — American T (Flap T)

When a **T sound** falls between two vowel sounds (even across separate words), it morphs into a soft **D sound** (called a flap T or tap T). This is very common in American English.

**From the movie:**
- **got it** → sounds like /ɡɑɾɪt/ ("god-it")

**More examples:**
- **butter** → /bʌɾər/ (sounds like "budder")
- **chatter** → /tʃæɾər/ (sounds like "chadder")
- **go to** → /ɡoʊɾə/ (sounds like "go-da")
- **water** → /wɑɾər/ (sounds like "wadder")

**Tip:** This happens naturally in native speech. Don't try to force it — just be aware of it when listening, and it will start to feel natural the more you practice.

## 8. Connected Speech — Phrasal Verb Stress

Phrasal verbs consist of a **verb** (content word) and a **particle** (function word). Natives **stress the verb** and **unstress the particle**, connecting them as one smooth sound.

**From the movie:**
- **come on** → stress "COME," unstress "on" → sounds like /kʌmɑn/ ("cuh-mon")

**Why this matters:** Over-stressing function words (like particles) makes your speech sound unnatural. Connecting the verb and particle into one smooth sound makes you sound more fluent.

**More examples:**
- **give up** → stress "GIVE," unstress "up"
- **pick up** → stress "PICK," unstress "up"
- **work on** → stress "WORK," unstress "on"
- **go back** → stress "GO," unstress "back"

## 9. Grammar — "Ain't" in Informal Speech

**"Ain't"** is considered grammatically incorrect in formal English, but it is a very common part of informal native speech. You will hear it constantly in movies, TV shows, and everyday conversation.

**"Ain't" can replace:**
- **am not** — *I ain't going to help you.* (= I'm not going to help you.)
- **is not** — *He ain't worried.* (= He's not worried.)
- **are not** — *They ain't ready.* (= They're not ready.)
- **have not** — *I ain't got time for this.* (= I haven't got time for this.)
- **has not** — *She ain't seen it yet.* (= She hasn't seen it yet.)

*Example from the movie: "He ain't worried." (= He's not worried.)*

## 10. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 10.1 Toy Story — The First Computer-Animated Film

- Toy Story (1995) was the first entirely computer-animated feature film and the first ever produced by Pixar Animation Studios.
- It was a groundbreaking achievement in filmmaking, proving that full-length animated films could be made entirely with computer graphics.
- The film launched Pixar as one of the most important animation studios in history.

---

### 10.2 The Universe Where Toys Are Alive

- Toy Story is set in a world where toys are living things with feelings, thoughts, and fears.
- However, toys must **pretend to be lifeless** whenever humans are present — this is a fundamental rule of their world.
- This is why the phrase **"coast is clear"** is so important — toys must constantly check if it's safe to move and talk.
- The toys belong to a boy named Andy, and their primary purpose is to be there for him when he needs them.

---

### 10.3 Toy Names as Puns

- Many character names in Toy Story are clever puns or wordplay:
  - **Hamm** — A piggy bank (pig-shaped coin container). "Ham" is a food product made from pigs.
  - **Mr. Potato Head** — A toy where you attach facial features to a plastic potato body.
  - **Slinky (Slinky Dog)** — Named after the Slinky toy (a spring that walks down stairs), combined with a dog shape.
  - **Rex** — A dinosaur toy. "Rex" comes from Tyrannosaurus Rex, the most famous dinosaur.
  - **Bo Peep** — Named after the nursery rhyme character "Little Bo Peep," who is a shepherdess.
- The movie constantly uses these character traits for humor — Ham is called "uncultured swine" (he's literally a pig), Bo Peep talks about her "flock" (she's a shepherdess figure), etc.

---

### 10.4 The Fear of Being Replaced

- The central emotional theme of Toy Story is the **fear of being replaced** by a newer, better toy.
- Every birthday and Christmas, the toys worry that Andy will receive a new toy that will take their place as his favorite.
- Rex worries about another dinosaur replacing him; Mr. Potato Head is cynical about new arrivals.
- Woody, as the leader and Andy's longtime favorite since kindergarten, reassures everyone that what matters is being there for Andy when he needs them.
- This theme mirrors real-life feelings of insecurity — the fear of being replaced in friendships, relationships, or at work.
