## 4. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Fairy** — An imaginary creature with magic powers, usually represented as a very small person with wings. In some fantasy stories, fairies can also be powerful witches who help other characters.
  - *Example: The fairy godmothers help the princess in Sleeping Beauty.*

- **Fae** — Another way to say "fairy" (has many different possible spellings: fay, fey, fae). In the Maleficent universe, Dark Fae are a type of fairy perceived as evil but who also represent forces of nature.
  - *Example: Maleficent is a Dark Fae.*

- **Maleficent** — As a word (not a character name): extremely evil, causing harm or destruction. This word is not commonly used in everyday English.
  - *Example: The villain's maleficent plans threatened the entire kingdom.*

- **Evil** — The opposite of good. We usually think of villains as evil, wrong, amoral, or bad.
  - *Example: We usually think of villains as evil and heroes as good.*

- **Mistress** — A title given to a woman who has power or authority over other people or a place.
  - *Example: Maleficent is the Mistress of the Moors.*

- **To inhabit** — To live in a place or region. If a place is "inhabited," people or animals live there.
  - *Example: These remote islands are inhabited only by birds.*

- **Mother Nature** — A figurative way to refer to nature as a force that controls the weather and all living things.
  - *Example: In a way, they represent Mother Nature.*

- **Stunning** — Shockingly beautiful, attractive, or impressive.
  - *Example: The house has a stunning view of the lake.*

- **Glorious** — Having great beauty, splendor, or magnificence; deserving great admiration.
  - *Example: Mother Nature can be absolutely stunning and glorious.*

- **To get on with (something)** — To start or continue doing something, especially work. As a command: "On with it!" means "Go ahead and say/do it!"
  - *Example: Stop talking and get on with it!*

- **Of (no/little) consequence** — If something is "of consequence," it is important or valuable. "Of no consequence" means not important.
  - *Example: The sanity of the plan is of no consequence.*

- **To overreact** — To respond to something in an overly excited, angry, or fearful way; showing more emotion than is necessary or appropriate.
  - *Example: It's certainly no reason to overreact.*

- **To ruin** — To spoil or destroy something completely.
  - *Example: He was going to ask for your hand — you ruined the surprise!*

- **To disappear** — To cease to be visible; to vanish or go missing.
  - *Example: It's just that Prince Phillip has disappeared.*

- **Purpose** — The reason for which something exists or is done; a goal or intention.
  - *Example: Anything in nature has a purpose.*

- **To destroy** — To completely damage or put an end to something so that it no longer exists or functions.
  - *Example: Mother Nature can absolutely destroy.*

- **To feed** — To give food to a person, animal, or plant; to nourish.
  - *Example: Mother Nature can feed and shade and protect.*

- **To shade** — To provide shelter from direct sunlight; to block the light.
  - *Example: The trees shade the path from the hot sun.*

- **To protect** — To keep someone or something safe from harm or danger.
  - *Example: Mother Nature can feed, shade, and protect.*

- **To guess** — To try to give an answer or opinion when you don't have all the facts.
  - *Example: Maleficent tries to guess the rest of the sentence.*

- **Unpleasant** — Not pleasant; causing discomfort or unhappiness.
  - *Example: Phillip asking Aurora to marry him would be very unpleasant news for Maleficent.*

- **Impatiently** — In a way that shows you are not willing to wait; restlessly.
  - *Example: Maleficent impatiently asked Diaval to share the news.*

- **Remote** — Far away from places where other people live; isolated.
  - *Example: These remote islands are inhabited only by birds.*

- **Snowstorm** — A heavy fall of snow with strong winds.
  - *Example: Mother Nature shows herself in earthquakes and snowstorms.*

- **Earthquake** — A sudden and violent shaking of the ground caused by Earth's tectonic plate movements.
  - *Example: The earthquake destroyed several buildings in the city.*

- **Figuratively** — In a metaphorical or symbolic way, not literally.
  - *Example: Mother Nature is considered figuratively as a force that controls the weather.*

- **Yellow fever** — A viral disease transmitted by infected mosquitoes, common in certain regions of South America and Africa.
  - *Example: Maleficent sarcastically guesses that Phillip has yellow fever.*

- **Leprosy** — Also known as Hansen's disease; a serious infectious disease that affects skin and the nervous system.
  - *Example: Maleficent guesses leprosy as another unpleasant possibility.*

- **Sunrise** — The time in the morning when the sun appears above the horizon.
  - *Example: Some fairies are related to the sunrise.*

## 5. Nature & Fantasy Vocabulary

- **Moss** — A very small green or yellow plant that grows on wet surfaces such as rocks, walls, and trees.
  - *Example: The old stone wall was covered in moss.*

- **Tree bark / Bark** — The hard outer layer of a tree that covers the trunk.
  - *Example: The bark of the oak tree was rough and thick.*

- **Biome** — A region of the Earth's surface with a particular combination of climate, plants, and animals (e.g., forests, desert, the sea).
  - *Example: The Amazon rainforest is one of the most biodiverse biomes on Earth.*

- **Trunk** — The thick main stem of a tree from which the branches grow.
  - *Example: The trunk of the tree was so wide we couldn't wrap our arms around it.*

- **Wings** — The parts of a bird, insect, or fairy used for flying.
  - *Example: Fairies are usually represented as small people with wings.*

- **Godmother** — A woman who promises to help raise a child in a particular faith; in fairy tales, a magical protector.
  - *Example: The fairy godmothers protect Princess Aurora in Sleeping Beauty.*

- **The Moors** — In the Maleficent movies, the magical land and forests ruled by Maleficent.
  - *Example: Maleficent rules the Moors and all its creatures.*

## 6. Connected Speech — Blending Prepositions and Articles

Native speakers commonly reduce and blend prepositions with articles so they sound like one word:

**"in" + "a" → sounds like "in-a" /ɪnə/**

**From the interview:**
- "**In a** way, they represent Mother Nature" → sounds like "in-a way"

**Examples from Friends:**
- "It's somewhere **in a** ball" → /ɪnə bɔːl/
- "Why did I have to speak **in a** British accent?" → /ɪnə ˈbrɪtɪʃ/

**Other common blends:**
- "**at a**" → /æɾə/
- "**on a**" → /ɑnə/
- "**for a**" → /fɔrə/

**Tip:** Pay attention to how natives blend these small words together. They almost never pause between a preposition and an article.

## 7. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 7.1 The Maleficent Universe — Dark Fae

- In Disney's Maleficent films, the "Dark Fae" are powerful fairy creatures associated with different biomes across the world.
- Unlike traditional "evil" fairies, the Dark Fae have a purpose in nature — they represent the powerful, sometimes destructive forces of the natural world.
- Angelina Jolie explains that they "inhabit all lands" and travel "across the world, across the oceans."

---

### 7.2 Mother Nature as a Metaphor

- Angelina uses "Mother Nature" as a metaphor to explain the Dark Fae's dual nature.
- Mother Nature can be "stunning and glorious" — feeding, shading, and protecting life.
- But it can also "absolutely destroy" — through earthquakes, snowstorms, and other powerful forces.
- The Dark Fae embody this duality: they are part of both the beautiful and destructive aspects of nature.

---

### 7.3 Character Dynamics — Maleficent and Prince Phillip

- The movie clip shows Maleficent's relationship with Diaval (her servant/companion) and her feelings about Prince Phillip.
- Maleficent clearly does not care for Prince Phillip — when Diaval hesitates to share news, she sarcastically guesses terrible things (yellow fever, leprosy).
- The actual news — that Phillip has proposed to Aurora — is even worse for Maleficent, prompting her dry response: "Ruin my morning."
- This scene demonstrates British-style dry humor and sarcasm.

---

### 7.4 Historical Diseases as Humor

- Maleficent's guesses of "yellow fever" and "leprosy" are used for comedic effect.
- These are real historical diseases that were once feared and widespread.
- The humor comes from Maleficent hoping for these terrible outcomes for Prince Phillip rather than the actual news (a marriage proposal).
- This type of dark humor relies on the audience understanding that Maleficent would prefer the prince to be seriously ill rather than marry Aurora.
