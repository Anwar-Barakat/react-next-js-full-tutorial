## 1. Scene 1 — Miguel's Family Story

- **Miguel:** Sometimes I think I'm cursed because of something that happened before I was even born. See, a long time ago there was this family. The papa — he was a musician. He and his family would sing and dance and count their blessings.
- **Miguel:** But he also had a dream — to play for the world. And one day, he left with his guitar and never returned.
- **Miguel:** And the mama — she didn't have time to cry over that walk-away musician. She rolled up her sleeves and she learned to make shoes.
- **Miguel:** She could have made candy, or fireworks, or sparkly underwear for wrestlers. But no — she chose shoes.

## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Cursed** — Having a curse placed on you; feeling like bad luck follows you because of something from the past.
  - *Example: Sometimes I think I'm cursed because of something that happened before I was even born.*

- **Musician** — A person who plays a musical instrument or sings, especially as a profession.
  - *Example: The papa — he was a musician.*

- **To return** — To come back to a place.
  - *Example: One day he left with his guitar and never returned.*

- **Audience** — The group of people who gather in one place to watch someone perform. Note: this word refers to a group, not an individual person.
  - *Example: I'll be your first audience!*

- **Shine / Shoe shine** — The act of polishing shoes to make them shiny and clean. A shoe shiner is a person who polishes shoes for others.
  - *Example: I was just getting a shine.*

- **Tricks** — Actions intended to deceive someone; a clever way of fooling someone.
  - *Example: I know your tricks, mariachi!*

- **Mariachi** — A traditional Mexican musician who wears distinctive clothes and plays traditional music, typically in a group with guitars, violins, and trumpets.
  - *Example: I know your tricks, mariachi!*

- **Guitar** — A stringed musical instrument played by strumming or plucking the strings.
  - *Example: He was just showing me his guitar.*

- **Sparkly** — Shining with small flashes of light; glittery.
  - *Example: She could have made sparkly underwear for wrestlers.*

- **Wrestler** — A person who takes part in wrestling, a sport or entertainment where two people fight by grappling and trying to throw each other down.
  - *Example: Sparkly underwear for wrestlers.*

- **To fool** — To trick or deceive someone.
  - *Example: Who am I fooling?*

- **Miracle** — A very lucky, surprising, and unexpected event.
  - *Example: It's going to take a miracle to get me into the army.*

- **Nodding** — Moving your head up and down several times to show approval or agreement.
  - *Example: Both nodding and yesing express agreement.*

- **Yesing** — A slang word meaning to agree with someone repeatedly (by saying "yes" over and over).
  - *Example: She was just nodding and yesing the whole time.*

- **Blessed / Blessing** — Feeling lucky or fortunate to have something good, such as health or love. A blessing is something you feel grateful for.
  - *Example: He and his family would sing and dance and count their blessings.*

- **To polish** — To rub something (shoes, metal, furniture) to make it smooth and shiny.
  - *Example: Miguel polishes shoes for the musician.*

- **Catchphrase** — A well-known phrase or sentence associated with a particular person or character.
  - *Example: "Seize your moment" was the catchphrase of Ernesto de la Cruz.*

## 4. Phrasal Verbs

- **Set up** — To prepare something; to arrange or organize things so they are ready for use.
  - *Example: They're setting up for tonight — the music competition.*
  - *Example: Some hot-shot race car is setting up his racing headquarters here.*

- **Sign up** — To enroll yourself in an activity or event in order to participate.
  - *Example: You should sign up!*
  - *Example: I thought I might sign up for the talent show.*

- **Freak out** — To panic or have a strong emotional reaction to something (can be positive or negative).
  - *Example: My family would freak!*
  - *Example: Just don't freak out.*

- **Keep away from** — To avoid someone or something because they seem dangerous, unpleasant, or likely to cause problems.
  - *Example: You keep away from him!*
  - *Example: Keep the baby away from the kitchen.*

- **Roll up (your sleeves)** — Literally: to fold your sleeves up your arm. Figuratively: to prepare to work hard.
  - *Example: She rolled up her sleeves and she learned to make shoes.*

## 5. Expressions & Idioms

- **Seize your moment** — To take advantage of an opportunity; to live life to its full potential and enjoy it while you can.
  - *Example: What did De la Cruz always say? Seize your moment!*

- **Show me what you got** — An expression used to ask someone to demonstrate their talents or abilities. Similar to "show me what you're made of."
  - *Example: Show me what you got, muchacho!*

- **Shame on you** — A phrase used to tell someone off; to say they should feel ashamed of their actions.
  - *Example: Shame on you for lying to a child!*

- **Count your blessings** — To be grateful for the good things you have in life.
  - *Example: He and his family would sing and dance and count their blessings.*

- **You've got it** — An expression meaning you are good at something; you have talent.
  - *Example: Miguel, you've got it — you're a really good musician.*

- **Take liberties** — To feel very comfortable in a situation and act in a way that could be considered too free or inappropriate.
  - *Example: I took some liberties when writing the essay and didn't follow the professor's rules.*

- **Take the liberty (to do something)** — To do something without asking for permission first.
  - *Example: I took the liberty of ordering a drink for you while you were in the bathroom.*

## 6. Connected Speech — T+Y Becomes CH

When a word ending in **T** is followed by a word starting with **Y** (or a "you" sound), the two sounds merge into a **CH sound** /tʃ/.

**From the movie — "what you":**
- **"what you got"** → sounds like **"whatchu got"**
- The musician says: *"Show me what you got"* → sounds like *"Show me whatchu got"*

**More examples:**
- **don't you** → "don'tchu"
- **won't you** → "won'tchu"
- **can't you** → "can'tchu"
- **got you** → "gotcha"
- **let you** → "letchu"

**Tip:** This is extremely common in American English and happens naturally in fast speech. Once you start listening for it, you'll hear it everywhere.

---

## 7. Connected Speech — Glottal T

When a word ends in **T** (especially after a vowel), the T sound is sometimes **cut off at the back of the throat** instead of being released at the front of the mouth. This is called a **glottal T** or **glottal stop**.

**From the movie:**
- **"got"** → the final T is stopped: /ɡɑʔ/ (sounds like "go'")
- *"Show me what you got"* — the final "got" has a glottal T

**More examples:**
- **apartment** → /əˈpɑːrʔmənʔ/
- **mountain** → /maʊnʔən/
- **important** → /ɪmˈpɔːrʔənʔ/
- **can't** → /kænʔ/

---

## 8. Connected Speech — American T (Flap T)

When a **T sound** falls between two vowel sounds (even across separate words), it morphs into a **soft D sound** in American English. This is called the flap T or American T.

**From the movie:**
- **"that I"** → sounds like /ðæɾaɪ/ ("thad-I")
- The T in "that" is between the vowel "a" in "that" and the vowel "I," so it becomes a soft D.

**More examples:**
- **better** → /bɛɾər/ ("bedder")
- **water** → /wɑɾər/ ("wadder")
- **got it** → /ɡɑɾɪt/ ("god-it")
- **cat and** → /kæɾænd/ ("cad-and")

**Important:** This doesn't have to occur within the same word — as long as the T is surrounded by vowel sounds, even across words, the sound change happens.

## 9. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 9.1 Coco — The Movie

- Coco (2017) is a Disney Pixar animated film about Miguel, a 12-year-old boy from a Mexican family of shoemakers who dreams of becoming a musician.
- His family has banned music for generations after Miguel's great-great-grandfather abandoned his family to pursue a music career.
- On Dia de Muertos (Day of the Dead), Miguel accidentally enters the Land of the Dead and must find his way back with the help of his deceased relatives.
- The film celebrates Mexican culture, family bonds, and the importance of remembering loved ones who have passed away.

---

### 9.2 Dia de Muertos — Day of the Dead

- Dia de Muertos is a Mexican holiday (November 1-2) that honors and remembers deceased family members and friends.
- Families create altars (ofrendas) decorated with flowers, candles, photos, and the favorite foods of the deceased.
- It is not a sad occasion — it's a celebration of life and the continuation of the bond between the living and the dead.
- Mexican culture is very present in the United States due to geographical proximity and immigration, making this tradition increasingly familiar to English speakers.

---

### 9.3 Mariachi Music

- Mariachi is a form of traditional Mexican music typically performed by a group of musicians wearing distinctive charro outfits.
- Instruments commonly include guitars, violins, trumpets, and the vihuela (a small guitar-like instrument).
- In the movie, Abuelita uses "mariachi" not as the musician's name but as a label — referring to him by his profession, showing her distrust of all musicians.
- Ernesto de la Cruz, Miguel's hero, is a famous fictional mariachi singer whose catchphrase is "Seize your moment."

---

### 9.4 Family Honor and Blessings

- The concept of family is central to Coco — Miguel's family has strict rules about music because of the pain caused by his great-great-grandfather leaving.
- The word "blessing" appears multiple times: Miguel's family "counts their blessings," and later in the movie, Miguel needs his family's blessing to return to the living world.
- "Rolling up her sleeves" — when the great-great-grandmother was abandoned, she didn't cry; she worked hard and built a shoemaking business. This phrase represents resilience and determination.
- The conflict between following your dreams and respecting family traditions is the emotional heart of the film.
