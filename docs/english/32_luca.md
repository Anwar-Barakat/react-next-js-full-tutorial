# Learn English with Luca Guide

A comprehensive English lesson based on scenes from Disney Pixar's Luca (2021), covering the difference between "make" and "do," vocabulary related to intensity and emotions (stinking, pretty, bloodthirsty, lunatic, on edge, horrifying, gross), connected speech reductions (gonna, wanna, gotta, kinda), the word "right" for immediacy, emphatic "do" in affirmative sentences, and cultural themes about the Italian Riviera, Vespa culture, and sea creature mythology.

## 1. Scene 1 — Luca and Alberto Discover the Vespa

- **Alberto:** What's that? Oh, it's just the greatest thing that humans ever made. The Vespa.
- **Luca:** Whoa.
- **Alberto:** You just sit on it and it takes you anywhere you want to go. In the whole stinking world.
- **Alberto:** Vespa is freedom.
- **Alberto:** Pretty cool, right?
- **Luca:** Huh. Are you going to make one? I think you have all the parts.
- **Alberto:** I do have the parts. I am going to make one. You want to help?
- **Luca:** Me? Yeah! Wait. No, I can't. I've got to go home.
- **Alberto:** Right, this second?
- **Luca:** Yeah. If my parents found out I was up here... it would be bad. So, thank you, but... goodbye. Forever.
- **Luca:** Okay, but now I really do have to go.
- **Alberto:** Okay, bye.
- **Luca:** Seriously, I have to go like now, like right now.
- **Alberto:** Okay, see ya.
- **Luca:** It's even better than the picture.
- **Alberto:** Yeah, it is.
- **Luca:** Ugh. I've got to run. See you tomorrow.

## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Stinking / Stinky** — Used as an intensifier to emphasize something excessive or extreme. As an adjective, it means something that smells very bad, like rotten eggs or raw fish.
  - *Example: In the whole stinking world.* (= the entire world)
  - *Example: My neighbour owns two Ferraris and a Porsche. He's stinking rich.*
  - *Example: I can't afford that fancy stuff. All I need is one stinkin' neck joint.*

- **Pretty** — When used before an adjective, it means "fairly" or "quite." It adds emphasis to the quality being described.
  - *Example: Pretty cool, right?*
  - *Example: This is a pretty expensive dress.*
  - *Example: I'm pretty confident I'll do well on the test.*

- **Even** — Used to emphasize how shocking or surprising something is, or to add more detail to something you have said. "Even better" points out that something is more exciting than another option.
  - *Example: It's even better than the picture.*
  - *Example: I couldn't afford a flight to Hawaii, but I found a cheap one to Cancun. That's even better.*

- **Bloodthirsty** — Eager to use violence or demonstrating strong interest in violent things. Also used to refer to very violent situations. Synonyms: brutal, cruel, savage.
  - *Example: How could my mother tell him about this town of bloodthirsty lunatics!*
  - *Example: Not wind, nor rain, nor a thousand bloodthirsty savages shall stand in our way.*

- **Lunatic** — A person who behaves in a crazy, dangerous, or very stupid way. Sometimes used humorously.
  - *Example: This town of bloodthirsty lunatics!*
  - *Example: Everyone knows her father's a lunatic.*

- **On edge** — Nervous and unable to relax; tense or anxious.
  - *Example: I'm sorry, I'm just a little on edge.*
  - *Example: Nobody moves. I got a dragon here and I'm not afraid to use it. I'm a donkey on edge!*

- **Horrifying** — Something that gives you a sensation of horror or shock; extremely frightening.
  - *Example: Wow. We look horrifying.*
  - *Example: How do I look, Minion? Do I look bad? Disgustingly horrifying, sir.*

- **Gross** — Very unpleasant; disgusting. Used as an informal reaction to something that repulses you.
  - *Example: Ugh, gross.*
  - *Example: Ew, what's that? A grub. Ew, gross.*

- **Scales** — The small, flat pieces of hard skin that cover a fish or reptile's body. Used here as a pun (see section 9.4).
  - *Example: You scared the scales off me!*

- **Geez** — An interjection used to express surprise, anger, or annoyance. An informal exclamation.
  - *Example: Geez, you're strong!*
  - *Example: Other ways to express surprise: Dang! No way! That's crazy!*

## 4. "Make" vs "Do"

"Make" and "do" are two verbs that express similar ideas but are used differently:

- **Make** — Used for creating something, and for actions that you choose to do.
  - *Example: Are you going to make one?* (creating a Vespa)
  - *Example: Oh, it's just the greatest thing that humans ever made.*
  - Common collocations: make a salad, make money, make friends, make a decision, make a mistake
  - Also commonly used for cooking or preparing food:
    - *Example: Oh, there you are. I made your favorite.*

- **Do** — Used with work, jobs, and tasks. It focuses on the idea of performing an action, a repetitive task, or some sort of obligation.
  - *Example: So we did our homework, tested the results.*
  - Common collocations: do yoga, do the dishes, do homework, do the laundry, do a favor

**Tip:** A helpful rule of thumb: "make" often implies producing or creating a result, while "do" implies performing an activity or task.

## 5. Expressions & Idioms

- **Got to run** — A way to say goodbye when you are leaving a place, with a sense of urgency. It means you are in a hurry or late to be somewhere else.
  - *Example: Ugh. I've got to run. See you tomorrow.*

- **Find out** — A phrasal verb that means the same as "discover something," especially information that was previously unknown.
  - *Example: If my parents found out I was up here... it would be bad.*
  - *Example: I just found out about that movie on the streaming platform. I loved it.*

- **Keep your guard up** — Usually used as a command or imperative to warn someone to be careful and alert. Alternatively, you can say "don't let your guard down."
  - *Example: Just keep your guard up. There's gonna be land monsters everywhere.*
  - *Example: Come on, Kristoff. Let down your guard.*

- **Not today** — Used when you are not in the mood to deal with something, or when you're not going to accept a situation. A firm refusal.
  - *Example: Stop! Not today! It's me!*
  - *Example: Not today, Zurg.*

- **Scared the [something] out of someone** — Used when someone frightens or shocks you very suddenly or severely. People commonly say "scare the crap out of someone" or "scare the bejesus out of someone." In this movie, "scales" replaces the usual word as a pun on the characters being sea creatures.
  - *Example: You scared the scales off me!*

- **See ya** — An informal way of saying "goodbye." Short for "see you (later)."
  - *Example: Okay, see ya.*

## 6. Connected Speech — Reductions

In natural spoken English, fluent speakers change or cut some sounds when they say words in a sentence. These are called **reductions**, and they are the reason why you sometimes feel like native speakers talk too fast.

**Common reductions with "to":**

- **Gonna** = going to
  - *Example: I am gonna make one.* (= I am going to make one)
  - *Example: There's gonna be land monsters everywhere.*

- **Wanna** = want to
  - *Example: Wanna help?* (= Want to help?)

- **Gotta** = got to / have got to
  - *Example: I gotta go home.* (= I've got to go home)

**Other common reductions:**

- **Kinda** = kind of
  - *Example: I kinda like him. I think he's funny.*

- **Didja** = did you
  - *Example: Where didja go last night?* (= Where did you go last night?)

- **'em** = them
  - *Example: I hate 'em.* (= I hate them)

- **Wentout** = went out
  - *Example: I went out for drinks with people from work.*

**What all these reductions have in common:** Most involve verbs followed by the preposition "to" (gonna, wanna, gotta), where the "to" gets reduced and attached to the verb.

**Tip:** You don't need to use reductions in your own speech to sound natural, but recognizing them is essential for understanding native English speakers in movies, TV shows, and real conversations.

## 7. Grammar — "Right" for Immediacy

"Right" is a versatile word in English that can mean different things depending on context. In this lesson, it is used to **emphasize the precise time** of a question or statement, meaning "immediately" or "at this exact moment."

- *Example: Right, this second?* (= Do you need to leave at this very second?)
- *Example: Seriously, I have to go like now, like right now.*

**Common combinations with "right" that mean "immediately":**

- **Right now** — At this exact moment.
  - *Example: I have to go right now.*

- **Right away** — Immediately, without delay.
  - *Example: Yes, yes. Right away.*

- **Right off** — Immediately, from the very start.
  - *Example: She knew right off that something was wrong.*

- **Right this second** — At this very instant; used for extra urgency.
  - *Example: Right, this second? Yeah.*

## 8. Grammar — Emphatic "Do"

You've probably learned to use the auxiliary verb "do/does/did" in negative and interrogative sentences. But you can also use it in **affirmative sentences to emphasize** what you're saying. This is called **emphatic "do"** and is used in speaking rather than writing.

**From the movie:**

- *"Okay, but now I really **do** have to go."*
  - Luca uses "do" to stress that he truly must leave — it's not a casual statement.

- *"I **do** have the parts."*
  - Alberto emphasizes that he genuinely has all the parts needed.

**How it works:**

- Present tense: **do** + base form of verb
  - *I love that movie.* → *I **do** love that movie.*
- Third person: **does** + base form of verb
  - *He cooks delicious dishes.* → *He **does** cook delicious dishes.*
- Past tense: **did** + base form of verb
  - *I loved that movie you recommended.* → *I **did** love that movie you recommended.*

**Important rule:** Whenever you use the auxiliary "do" (whether in past, present, or third person), the verb you're emphasizing comes back to the **base form**.

**More examples from Disney movies:**

- *I **do** believe that's our cue.*
- *I **did** do that. That's true.* (Sisu from Raya and the Last Dragon)

## 9. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 9.1 Luca — The Movie

- Luca (2021) is a Disney Pixar animated film set in a beautiful seaside town on the Italian Riviera.
- The story follows Luca Paguro, a curious and enthusiastic young sea monster who, like any other child, is eager to explore the world above the surface.
- When Luca gets the courage to go on land, he meets Alberto Scorfano, another sea creature who has experience living among humans. Their friendship grows as they search for adventures and, most importantly, a real Vespa.
- The film explores themes of friendship, self-discovery, the desire to belong, and the courage to be yourself in a world that may not accept you for who you really are.

---

### 9.2 The Vespa & Italian Culture

- **Vespa** is a luxury brand of motor scooters manufactured by the Italian company Piaggio. The name comes from the Italian word for "wasp," due to the vehicle's resemblance to the insect.
- The Vespa is an icon of Italian culture, symbolizing freedom, style, and youthful adventure — especially among young people.
- In the film, Alberto shows Luca a poster of a Vespa and declares "Vespa is freedom." This mirrors the real cultural significance of the scooter in post-war Italy, where it represented affordable mobility and independence.
- The boys' dream of building and riding their own Vespa drives much of the plot and represents their desire for freedom and exploration.

---

### 9.3 Sea Creatures & Shapeshifting

- Luca and Alberto are marine creatures (sea monsters) that shapeshift into human form when they are on dry land and revert to their sea creature form when wet.
- Luca spent his early childhood beneath the sea, where his parents — Daniela and Lorenzo — warned him about the dangers of the surface world, calling humans "land monsters."
- When Luca's parents discover he has been going to the surface, they plan to send him to live in the deep sea with his uncle. Luca and Alberto run away to Portorosso, a fishing town, and find a real Vespa.
- Luca's parents have no choice but to go to the surface for the first time in their lives to bring him back — leading to the comedic scene where they are terrified of their own human appearance.

---

### 9.4 The "Scales" Pun & Wordplay

- When Lorenzo is startled by Daniela, he says: "You scared the scales off me!" This is a **pun** (play on words) based on the common English expressions "scare the crap out of someone" or "scare the bejesus out of someone."
- Since the characters are sea creatures with scales instead of skin, the word "scales" replaces the usual word in the expression, creating a joke that fits perfectly with the marine context.
- This type of wordplay — where a familiar expression is modified to fit a character or situation — is very common in animated movies and is a great example of how English humor works through unexpected substitutions.
- Other examples of this type of pun in movies include characters replacing words with references to their own world (e.g., "What in the world?" becoming "What in the reef?" in an ocean-themed story).
