## 1. The Story of WALL-E

- It's the year 2805, and there are no human beings left on Earth. Some seven centuries ago, a corporation called Buy-N-Large assumed control of the planet's entire economy and government, and humanity got into a vicious cycle of buying more things than needed and producing mountains of trash that covered the entire planet.
- WALL-E is a cute little solitary robot in charge of cleaning all the garbage off the surface of planet Earth. In the year 2105, all humans took off to space because Earth was full of waste. At that time, there were millions of other similar robots assigned with the same task, but when the plan to restore the planet failed, all robots but this one were turned off.
- So WALL-E has been cleaning up Earth for 700 years. He feels quite lonely and nostalgic.
- Then he meets EVE, another more sophisticated robot, and they get entangled in a little romantic story. Later they go on a mission to bring humanity back to Earth.

## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Mankind / Humankind** — Terms that refer to all humans considered as a group.
  - *Example: The discovery of fire was one of the most important events in the history of mankind.*
  - *Example: What if mankind evacuated Earth and forgot to turn off the last remaining robot?*

- **Cruise** — A holiday on a large ship. Can also refer to a journey on a spaceship.
  - *Example: This company sent everybody on an intergalactic five-year cruise.*

- **Toxic** — Containing poisonous substances. Can also be used figuratively to describe harmful relationships or environments.
  - *Example: Earth was considered too toxic to support life again.*

- **Cockroach** — A common insect known for being extremely resilient and hard to kill. In the movie, WALL-E befriends a cockroach named Hal.
  - *Example: He finds a good friend in that cockroach.*

- **Consumerism** — The belief that it is good to buy and use a lot of goods and services. Often used critically to describe excessive consumption.
  - *Example: The first theme I noticed was consumerism.*

- **Doomed** — Probably going to experience an unfortunate or terrible future. Used to describe something that seems destined to fail or end badly.
  - *Example: Are we doomed to that bleak future?*
  - *Example: If I don't study more, I'm doomed to fail this subject.*

- **Nostalgia** — A sentimental longing or wistful affection for a period in the past.
  - *Example: It also emphasized this feeling of nostalgia.*

- **Reminisce** — To talk or think about pleasant events in your past.
  - *Example: Something so simple as a seedling made him reminisce about the time when there used to be so much life.*
  - *Example: When I see my ex-classmates, we always reminisce about the college days.*

- **Colorful** — Literally: full of color. Used in contrast to the bleak, mainly grey-colored landscape we see in the movie.
  - *Example: The world was so colorful.*

## 4. Waste & Environment Vocabulary

- **Waste** — Unwanted materials or substances that are left after you have used something.
  - *Example: The city is trying to recycle a third of the waste it produces.*

- **Wasteland** — An unattractive area, often with old ruined buildings, factories, or debris. In the movie, cities have turned into wastelands.
  - *Example: The entire planet became a wasteland.*

- **Trash** — (US English) Waste material such as paper, empty containers, or food that you throw away.
  - *Example: Producing mountains of trash that covered the entire planet.*

- **Garbage** — (US English) Another word for trash; waste material.
  - *Example: WALL-E is in charge of cleaning all the garbage off the surface of planet Earth.*

- **Rubbish** — (UK English) The British equivalent of "trash" or "garbage."
  - *Example: The waste and rubbish started to pile up.*

- **Dirt** — Unwanted filthy or messy substances on a surface.
  - *Example: He has to literally pick up all the dirt and useless objects.*

- **Filth** — Dirt; dirty, disgusting stuff. A stronger word than "dirt."
  - *Example: He goes about the land cleaning all the filth.*

- **Pile** — A group of several things of the same type that are put on top of each other. Can also be used as a verb: "to pile up."
  - *Example: These many robots could clean up all of the piles of waste.*
  - *Example: The waste started to pile up so much that they had to evacuate.*

- **Seedling** — A young plant or tree grown from a seed.
  - *Example: A really happy moment was when he found a seedling that brought hope.*

- **Bleak** — Unhappy, unpleasant, having no hope. Often collocated with "future."
  - *Example: Are we doomed to that bleak future?*
  - *Example: The company has a bleak future.*

- **Lonely** — Unhappy because you have no one to talk to or spend time with.
  - *Example: WALL-E is quite lonely because there's no one he can play with.*

- **Support life** — To have the conditions necessary for living things to exist. If a planet can't "support life," nothing can survive there.
  - *Example: Earth was considered too toxic to support life again.*
  - *Example: Mercury can't support life because it is too close to the sun.*

- **Aftermath** — The period of time after something bad (such as a war, storm, or accident) when people are still dealing with the results.
  - *Example: The movie shows us what it looks like in the aftermath of an environmental catastrophe.*
  - *Example: One aspect of the aftermath of COVID was that a lot of people lost their jobs.*

## 5. Collocations & Word Pairs

- **Come to fruition** — When a plan or idea begins to happen, exist, or be successful. Also collocates with "bring": "bring something to fruition."
  - *Example: When that plan didn't come to fruition, they turned off all the robots.*
  - *Example: The entrepreneur's idea never came to fruition.*
  - *Example: She worked very hard to bring her dreams to fruition.*

- **Vicious cycle** — A negative cycle where one problem leads to another, which makes the original problem worse, and so on.
  - *Example: Humanity got into a vicious cycle of consuming and consuming.*

- **Bleak future** — A future with no hope or positive prospects. The opposite is a **bright future**.
  - *Example: Are we doomed to that bleak future?*
  - *Example: The company has a bright future.* (opposite)

- **Take out the trash / Take out the rubbish** — A common collocation describing the everyday action of removing waste from your home.
  - *Example: WALL-E's job is essentially to take out the trash for the entire planet.*

## 6. Expressions & Idioms

- **Doomed to (something)** — Destined to experience an unfortunate future; fated to fail.
  - *Example: Are we doomed to that bleak future?*
  - *Example: If I don't study more, I'm doomed to fail this subject.*

- **Pile up** — To accumulate; to keep increasing in amount until there is a large quantity.
  - *Example: The waste and rubbish started to pile up so much that they ended up having to evacuate.*

- **End up (doing something)** — To finally be in a particular situation or place, especially one you didn't plan.
  - *Example: They ended up having to evacuate the whole planet.*

- **Go about** — To do something as you normally would; to continue with your regular activities.
  - *Example: The song he plays when he goes about the land cleaning all the filth.*

- **Clean up** — To make a place clean and tidy by removing dirt, waste, or mess.
  - *Example: These robots could clean up all of the piles of waste and trash left on Earth.*

## 7. UK vs US English — Waste Words

English has different words for the same concept depending on whether you're using **British English** or **American English**:

- **UK English:** rubbish
- **US English:** trash, garbage

**All three words** refer to waste material — paper, empty containers, food scraps, and other things you throw away.

**Common collocations:**
- **UK:** "Take out the rubbish," "rubbish bin"
- **US:** "Take out the trash," "trash can," "garbage can," "garbage truck"

**The word "waste"** is more neutral and formal, used in both UK and US English, and can refer to any unwanted materials or substances (not just household trash).

**Tip:** "Rubbish" in British English can also mean "nonsense" or "something of poor quality" — *"That movie was rubbish!"* This usage doesn't apply to "trash" or "garbage" in the same way (though "trash" can informally mean "of low quality" in American English).

## 8. Connected Speech — H-Dropping & Word Linking

**1. H-dropping — "Remind him of":**
- In natural speech, the H in **"him"** is often dropped, and the word connects to the previous word.
- **"remind him"** → sounds like **"remind-im"** /rɪmaɪndɪm/
- This then links further with **"of"**: "remind-im-of"
- Full phrase: **"remind him of how the world used to be"** → flows as one connected unit.

**2. Word linking — "Turned off all the robots":**
- Instead of pronouncing each word separately, the words link together in natural speech.
- **"turned off"** → the D links to the vowel in "off": "turn-doff"
- **"off all"** → the F links to the vowel in "all": "of-fall"
- **"all the"** → flows smoothly: "all-the"
- Full phrase sounds smooth and connected rather than choppy.

**Practice:**
- Say this phrase smoothly: "remind him of how the world used to be in the past"
- Try linking the words together rather than pausing between each one.

**Tip:** Connected speech is one of the main reasons native English can sound "fast" to learners. The words aren't actually spoken faster — they're linked together so there are fewer pauses between them.

## 9. Pronunciation — "Cruise" and Linked Phrases

**The word "cruise":**
- The vowel is a **long U sound** /uː/
- The final consonant is a **Z sound** /z/, not an S sound
- Pronunciation: /kruːz/
- While we traditionally associate "cruise" with ships, the movie uses it for a spaceship cruise.

**Linked phrases to practice:**
- **"piles of waste and trash"** — "piles-of" links together, "waste-and" links together
- **"too toxic to support life"** — "too toxic" has two T sounds; "to support" reduces "to" to /tə/
- **"the world used to be"** — "used to" reduces to "use-tuh" /juːstə/

## 10. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 10.1 WALL-E — The Movie

- WALL-E (2008) is a Disney Pixar animated film set primarily on a future Earth that has been abandoned by humanity due to overwhelming pollution and waste.
- WALL-E (Waste Allocation Load Lifter Earth-class) is a small, solitary robot who has been compacting trash on Earth for 700 years after all humans left on a massive spaceship cruise operated by the Buy-N-Large corporation.
- When a sleek probe robot named EVE arrives on Earth searching for signs of plant life, WALL-E falls in love and follows her into space, triggering a chain of events that will determine whether humanity can ever return home.
- The film is notable for having almost no dialogue in its first half, telling the story primarily through visual storytelling and the expressive sounds of the robots.

---

### 10.2 Consumerism and the Vicious Cycle

- One of the movie's strongest themes is **consumerism** — the idea that buying more and more goods leads to a destructive cycle.
- In the film, humanity got into a **vicious cycle**: buying things made them want to buy even more, which produced more waste, which ruined the planet, which forced them to leave.
- The term "vicious cycle" describes a situation where one problem creates another problem, which then makes the first problem worse — and so on, endlessly.
- The Buy-N-Large corporation controlled not just shopping but the entire government and economy — a satirical exaggeration of corporate power and consumer culture.
- The movie asks a provocative question: if we continue consuming at our current rate, are we **doomed** to this **bleak future**?

---

### 10.3 Environmental Catastrophe

- The movie shows Earth in the **aftermath** of an environmental catastrophe — the planet has become a **wasteland** covered in mountains of trash.
- The atmosphere is so **toxic** that Earth can no longer **support life** — nothing can grow, and no living creatures can survive (except, humorously, a cockroach).
- The WALL-E robots were supposed to burn the accumulated waste, but the toxic emissions from this process made Earth even more unsuitable for life.
- The film serves as a cautionary tale: if humanity doesn't change its relationship with consumption and waste, the consequences could be catastrophic.

---

### 10.4 Nostalgia and the Seedling

- Despite being a robot, WALL-E displays deep **nostalgia** — he collects objects from the past (a Rubik's cube, a light bulb, a match lighter) that remind him of how the world used to be.
- He plays an old song as he works, adding to the melancholy atmosphere and his longing for a world he never experienced but seems to miss.
- The most hopeful moment in the story is when WALL-E discovers a **seedling** — a tiny green plant growing among the trash. This small sign of life represents hope that Earth might recover.
- The seedling made WALL-E **reminisce** about a time when the world was **colorful** and full of life — in contrast to the grey, bleak landscape he inhabits.
- The contrast between the bleak present and the colorful past is central to the movie's emotional impact: even something as simple as a seedling can bring hope for a **bright future**.
