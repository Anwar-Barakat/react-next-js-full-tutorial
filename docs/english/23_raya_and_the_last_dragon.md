## 7. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Speech** — The act of exposing personal ideas and opinions in front of a group of people. Often used by leaders to inspire or persuade.
  - *Example: Nice speech, Chief Benja, but why'd you really bring us here?*
  - *Example: Didn't my speech inspire anyone to rise up and prove themselves worthy?*

- **Mighty** — Really powerful or strong. The superlative form is "mightiest."
  - *Example: Oh, it's easy to pontificate on Kumandra when you hold the mightiest weapon in all the lands.*

- **Pontificate** — To speak in an important way as if your opinion is the only correct one; to lecture others pompously.
  - *Example: It's easy to pontificate on Kumandra when you hold the mightiest weapon in all the lands.*

- **Weapon** — A general word for any object used in a war or a fight, such as a bomb, gun, knife, or sword.
  - *Example: Drop the weapon!*
  - *Example: The gem's not a weapon.*

- **Relic** — An object from the past that has value and importance in history. When described as "sacred," it has religious or spiritual significance.
  - *Example: The gem's not a weapon. It's a sacred relic.*

- **Joke** — When used informally to describe a person, it means they are a liar, useless, absurd, or not to be taken seriously.
  - *Example: You're a joke!*

- **Fairy tale** — A story often told to children about magical and imaginary beings and lands. Also used to describe something made up or an invented story that isn't real.
  - *Example: Kumandra is a fairy tale.*

- **Pointless** — A waste of time; having no useful purpose.
  - *Example: But here, I'm just another irrelevant, pointless nobody.*

- **Glow** — Literally, to shine with a soft, warm light. Also used as a compliment meaning someone looks extremely gorgeous, usually when well-dressed or visibly happy.
  - *Example: Whoa, uh, you're glowing.*

- **Aloe** — A plant (like aloe vera) widely used in health and beauty products due to its healing properties.
  - *Example: I use aloe and river slime to maintain my—*

- **Slime** — A sticky, unpleasant liquid. "River slime" would be the thick, slimy substance found on the surface of a river.
  - *Example: I use aloe and river slime to maintain my—*

- **Unique** — Different from everything else; one of a kind; exclusive.
  - *Example: Every dragon has a unique magic.*

- **Strong** — In this context, not about physical strength but about great ability or competence at something.
  - *Example: I'm a really strong swimmer.*

- **Reassemble** — To put something back together after it has been taken apart. The prefix "re-" means "again."
  - *Example: If we can get all the other gem pieces, I can reassemble it.*

## 8. Leadership & Conflict Vocabulary

- **Chief** — The leader of a people, tribe, or clan. Also used for the highest-ranking person in an organization (e.g., "chief of police," "chief executive").
  - *Example: Okay, first, I'm not a princess, I'm the daughter of the chief.*

- **Chef** — A skilled and trained cook; the most important cook in a restaurant. Note: "chief" and "chef" sound similar but have completely different meanings.
  - *Example: The chef prepared an extraordinary meal.*

- **Boss** — The person in charge of an organization or project who tells others what to do.
  - *Example: And that is how the minions found their new boss.*

- **Sham** — Something that is not what it appears to be; a fraud or deception.
  - *Example: Now you're nothing but a fake, a sham, a con.*

- **Con** — Short for "con artist" — a person who deceives others; also refers to the act of deception itself.
  - *Example: Now you're nothing but a fake, a sham, a con.*

- **Fake** — Something or someone not genuine; pretending to be something they're not.
  - *Example: Now you're nothing but a fake, a sham, a con.*

- **Irrelevant** — Not important; not connected to what is being discussed or done.
  - *Example: I'm just another irrelevant, pointless nobody.*

- **Worthy** — Deserving of respect, attention, or effort.
  - *Example: Didn't my speech inspire anyone to rise up and prove themselves worthy?*

## 9. Phrasal Verbs

- **See eye to eye** — To agree with someone; to share the same opinion or perspective.
  - *Example: Our people never see eye to eye.*

- **Come together** — To unite; to join as a group for a common purpose.
  - *Example: I believe our people can come together again.*

- **Rise up** — To take action; to stand up against something, often in a courageous way.
  - *Example: Didn't my speech inspire anyone to rise up and prove themselves worthy?*

- **Pitch in** — To contribute with ideas, effort, or help to a group or project.
  - *Example: There's like that one kid who didn't pitch in as much but still ended up with the same grade.*

- **End up** — To refer to the consequence or final result of a sequence of previous activities; to arrive at a situation you didn't necessarily plan.
  - *Example: That one kid who didn't pitch in as much but still ended up with the same grade.*
  - *Example: After several interviews, John ended up changing his job.*

- **Turn in** — To deliver or submit the final product; to hand something over.
  - *Example: I wasn't the one who actually made the gem. I just turned it in.*
  - *Example: You didn't turn in your paperwork last night!*

- **Be done (trying)** — To have finished with something; to have given up and stopped making an effort.
  - *Example: No, Cece, I am done trying.*

## 10. Expressions & Idioms

- **To be real with someone** — To be genuine and honest with that person; to speak frankly without hiding the truth.
  - *Example: I'm gonna be real with you, all right? I'm not like the best dragon, you know.*

- **For real** — Used to emphasize that what you're saying or doing is serious or genuine. Can also be used as an alternative to "really."
  - *Example: We're going to be fine. For real, for real.*

- **Same difference** — An informal expression meaning "it's essentially the same thing" even though there may be a technical difference.
  - *Example: I'm not a princess, I'm the daughter of the chief. — Same difference, no?*

- **Take the first step** — To be the one who initiates action; to begin a process that others are reluctant to start.
  - *Example: Someone has to take the first step.*

- **Pretty much** — An informal way of saying "almost" or "basically."
  - *Example: Back home, I've got pretty much everything.*

- **Somebody / Nobody** — "Somebody" means an important or significant person. "Nobody" means an insignificant or unimportant person.
  - *Example: To me you were a somebody. — I'm just another pointless nobody.*

- **Let this be it** — An expression of hope that what you're looking for has finally been found.
  - *Example: Please let this be it.*

## 11. Connected Speech — Reductions & Linking

**1. "Why'd you really bring us here?" — Contraction of "did":**
- **"why did you"** reduces to **"why'd you"** — "did" is reduced to just "d" attached to "why."
- **"you"** connects with the R from **"really"**: sounds like "you-really"
- **"bring us"** links together: the G connects to "us" → "bring-us"
- Full phrase sounds like: "why'd you really bring-us here?"

**2. "Are you gonna rob us?" — "Going to" becomes "gonna":**
- **"going to"** contracts to **"gonna"** in fast speech.
- Similar reductions: "want to" → "wanna," "got to" → "gotta," "kind of" → "kinda"
- Practice: "Are you gonna rob us?"

**3. "Why would he need to rob us?" — Emphasis and reduction:**
- **"need"** is emphasized as the key content word.
- **"to"** is reduced to "tuh" /tə/
- **"rob us"** links together: "rob-us" /rɑbəs/

**4. "I have something to say" — V becomes F:**
- **"have"** is reduced, and because the next word ("something") starts with an unvoiced S sound, the voiced V sound in "have" changes to an unvoiced **F sound**.
- Sounds like: "I haf something to say"

**5. "What's yours?" — S becomes Z:**
- **"is"** contracts to **"'s"** attached to "what."
- The S sound changes to a **Z sound** /z/: "what'z yours?"

**6. "You know" — Reduction of "you":**
- In American English, **"you"** is often reduced to **"ya"** /jə/ in casual speech.
- *Example: "Ya know, I'm not like the best dragon."*

## 12. Grammar — Emphatic "Do"

The auxiliary verb **"do," "does,"** or **"did"** can be used in **affirmative sentences** to add emphasis and stress what is being said. This is more common in speaking than in writing.

**From the movie:**
- **Sisu:** *"I did do that, that's true."*
  - Normal: "I did that." (simple past, states a fact)
  - Emphatic: "I **did** do that." (stresses that it really happened)

**How it works:**
- Add "do/does/did" before the **base form** of the verb.
- The emphasized verb always returns to its base form (infinitive without "to").

**More examples:**
- Normal: *"I love that movie you recommended."*
- Emphatic: *"I **did** love that movie you recommended."*
- Normal: *"She works hard."*
- Emphatic: *"She **does** work hard."*
- Normal: *"We enjoy coming here."*
- Emphatic: *"We **do** enjoy coming here."*

**Tip:** The stress falls on "do/does/did" when speaking. This construction is often used to confirm something that was doubted, to correct a misunderstanding, or to express strong agreement.

## 13. Grammar — Discourse Markers

**Discourse markers** are words or short phrases used in conversation to give the speaker more time to think, add natural flow, check understanding, or signal a change in topic. They don't add literal meaning to the sentence but are essential for natural-sounding English.

**Common discourse markers from the movie:**

- **"All right"** — Used to check if the listener is following or to signal you're about to say something.
  - *Example: "I'm gonna be real with you, all right?"*

- **"You know"** — Used to check understanding or fill a pause.
  - *Example: "I'm not like the best dragon, you know."*

- **"Okay"** — Used to signal a transition or change of topic.
  - *Example: "Okay, what's yours?"*

- **"Like"** — Used as a filler or to introduce an example (very common in informal American English).
  - *Example: "Have you ever done like a group project, but there's like that one kid..."*

- **"So"** — Used to introduce a conclusion or a new topic.

- **"Actually"** — Used to correct, emphasize, or introduce surprising information.
  - *Example: "I wasn't the one who actually made the gem."*

- **"Anyway"** — Used to return to the main topic after a digression.

**Tip:** Discourse markers are one of the biggest differences between textbook English and real conversational English. Using them naturally makes your speech sound much more fluent.

## 14. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 14.1 Raya and the Last Dragon — The Movie

- Raya and the Last Dragon (2021) is a Disney animated film set in the fictional land of Kumandra, inspired by Southeast Asian cultures.
- Long ago, humans and dragons lived together in harmony. When the Druun — evil spirits that turn people to stone — threatened the world, the dragons sacrificed themselves to save humanity.
- 500 years later, the land has split into five warring tribes (Heart, Fang, Talon, Spine, and Tail), and the dragon gem that protects them has been shattered.
- Raya, a warrior from the land of Heart, sets out to find Sisu, the last dragon, and reassemble the gem pieces to defeat the Druun and reunite the lands.

---

### 14.2 Trust and Unity

- The central theme of the movie is trust — Chief Benja believes the divided peoples of Kumandra can "come together again," but "someone has to take the first step."
- The expression "see eye to eye" perfectly captures the conflict: the tribes can never agree because they don't trust each other.
- Raya's journey is as much about learning to trust others as it is about finding the gem pieces — she must overcome her own suspicion and fear of betrayal.
- The movie's message is that unity requires vulnerability: you have to trust first, even when it's risky, because peace cannot exist without it.

---

### 14.3 Sisu's Humor and Humility

- Sisu is a dragon who doesn't fit the typical "mighty dragon" stereotype — she's awkward, funny, and self-deprecating.
- She compares her role in saving the world to "that one kid in a group project who didn't pitch in as much but still ended up with the same grade" — a relatable analogy that makes her immediately likable.
- When Raya says "you're glowing," Sisu misinterprets it as a compliment about her appearance ("I use aloe and river slime"), creating humor through the literal vs. figurative misunderstanding.
- Her "unique magic" being that she's "a really strong swimmer" is an anticlimactic punchline that subverts the expectation of a grand, impressive power.

---

### 14.4 Chief vs Chef vs Boss

- These three words describe people in charge but in very different contexts:
  - **Chief** — The leader of a tribe, clan, or organization. Used for traditional or formal leadership roles (e.g., "chief of police," "commander-in-chief").
  - **Chef** — The head cook in a restaurant. From the French word for "chief," but in English it only refers to cooking. Pronounced /ʃɛf/ (with a "sh" sound), not /tʃiːf/.
  - **Boss** — The person who tells you what to do at work. More informal and used in everyday contexts.
- In the movie, Raya corrects someone who calls her a "princess" by saying she's "the daughter of the chief" — "Same difference, no?" is the humorous response, suggesting the distinction doesn't really matter.
