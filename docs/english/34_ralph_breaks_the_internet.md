# Learn English with Ralph Breaks the Internet Guide

A comprehensive English lesson based on scenes from Disney's Ralph Breaks the Internet (2018), featuring the iconic meeting between Vanellope and the Disney Princesses. Covers the schwa sound and connected speech reductions, fairy tale vocabulary (poisoned, cursed, kidnapped, enslaved), informal expressions (barf, daddy issues, million dollar question), agreement with "so" and "neither," and cultural themes about Disney Princess stereotypes and humor.

## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Poisoned** — Having eaten or drunk something with a dangerous chemical or harmful substance. Different from "food poisoning," which means the person ate something that was spoiled or contaminated and got an upset stomach (not literally poisoned).
  - *Example: Were you poisoned?*
  - *Example: A special sort of death for one so fair... a poisoned apple!*

- **Cursed** — Under the influence of a magical spell intended to bring bad luck or misfortune. In real life, "to curse" means to use impolite words to show anger (in the UK, "swear" is used more commonly).
  - *Example: Cursed? No.*
  - *Example: I cursed so loudly when I stubbed my little toe.*

- **Kidnapped** — Illegally taken or abducted by force and against someone's will.
  - *Example: Kidnapped? Enslaved? No.*
  - *Example: He was kidnapped!*

- **Enslaved** — Forced to work without any compensation, or to have someone completely take control of your life.
  - *Example: Kidnapped? Enslaved?*

- **Assume** — To accept something as the truth without questioning or proof.
  - *Example: I have to assume you made a deal with an underwater sea witch.*
  - *Example: Am I to assume you are the expert?*

- **Barf** — American slang for vomiting. Used as an interjection to express extreme disgust.
  - *Example: Ew, barf!*
  - *Example: Can you imagine if having a family healed some of my darkest drama? Barf!*

- **Spotlight** — A round circle of light produced by a lamp. "To be in the spotlight" means to be getting a lot of public attention.
  - *Example: There was like music and a spotlight.*
  - *Example: She's been in the spotlight ever since her debut album.*

- **Gown** — A long dress worn on formal occasions such as proms, balls, or weddings.
  - *Example: I guess it's back to the gowns, girls.*

- **Comfy** — Short for "comfortable." Informal and widely used in everyday English.
  - *Example: All hail Princess Vanellope, the queen of comfy!*

- **Thingamabob** — A very informal word used when you don't remember or don't know the name of an object.
  - *Example: I've got whosits and whatsits galore. See, one thingamabob? I got twenty.*

## 4. Fairy Tale & Disney Vocabulary

- **Witch / Sea Witch** — A woman believed to have magical powers, who uses them to help or harm other people. In Disney stories, evil witches often ask for something in exchange for granting a wish. Ariel's reference is to Ursula, the villain in The Little Mermaid.
  - *Example: I have to assume you made a deal with an underwater sea witch.*

- **In exchange for** — In order to obtain what you want, you have to give something in return.
  - *Example: She took your voice in exchange for a pair of human legs.*

- **True love's kiss** — In Disney stories, when two characters who are truly in love share a kiss, it is believed to cure whatever curse has fallen over one of the characters.
  - *Example: Have you ever had true love's kiss?*

- **Sleeping death** — A magical state of unconsciousness, often induced by a spell or poison. In Snow White's case, this was caused by the poisoned apple.
  - *Example: A poisoned apple... sleeping death.*

- **Spell** — A set of magic words used to make something happen. Related: "to cast a spell" means to perform magic.

## 5. Expressions & Idioms

- **What kind of (a)...** — Used to ask about the type or nature of something, often implying surprise or doubt.
  - *Example: What kind of a princess are you?*

- **Daddy issues** — A psychological complex referring to a person's problems with their father or father figure, often leading to poor choices in relationships. The word "issue" can also be used as a synonym for "problems" in general.
  - *Example: Do you have daddy issues?*
  - *Example: Wow, you really got some trust issues.*

- **Million dollar question** — A figurative way of saying something is really important or difficult to answer.
  - *Example: And now for the million dollar question: do people assume all your problems got solved because a big strong man showed up?*
  - *Example: Jay asked Susan to marry him. The million dollar question is, is she ready for this?*

- **What is up with that?** — An informal way of expressing confusion or frustration about something that doesn't make sense.
  - *Example: What is up with that?*

- **All hail** — An old-fashioned way of greeting or welcoming an important person. Commonly used to salute or introduce members of royalty such as kings, queens, princes, or princesses.
  - *Example: All hail Princess Vanellope, the queen of comfy!*
  - *Example: All hail the rightful ruler of Sugar Rush, Princess Vanellope!*

- **What's it called again?** — A phrase used when you can't remember the name of something. Very useful for language learners.
  - *Example: I never thought I'd get to wear a real — what's it called again? Oh yeah, shirt.*

- **Best of luck** — A common phrase used to say you hope the other person succeeds in what they are doing.
  - *Example: It was lovely to meet you, Vanellope, and best of luck finding your song.*
  - *Example: Best of luck controlling them without an alpha.*

- **Ew** — An interjection used to express disgust or displeasure. Other common ones include: yuck, ugh, ick.
  - *Example: Ew, barf!*

## 6. Connected Speech — The Schwa Sound

The **schwa** is the most common vowel sound in English. It is represented by the symbol **"ə"** or written informally as **"uh."** It is a short, relaxed vowel sound — something between an "uh" and an "air."

**Why does this matter?**

In connected speech (the natural way of linking words in English), most **auxiliary verbs, pronouns, articles, linkers, and prepositions** are not stressed. They are reduced to a schwa sound.

**Example from the movie:**

- Full form: **"What kind of a princess are you?"** (7 words)
- In connected speech: **"What kind-ə-ə princess are you?"** — the words "of" and "a" are reduced to schwa sounds, making it sound like fewer words.

**How it works:**

- The word **"of"** → reduced to /əv/ or just /ə/
- The word **"a"** → reduced to /ə/
- The word **"are"** → reduced to /ər/
- The word **"to"** → reduced to /tə/

**More practice:**

- **"Princess Vanellope Von Schweetz"** — The word "of" in "of the Sugar Rush" would be reduced to a schwa.
- **"Has a nice ring to it, don't you think?"** — "a" and "to" are reduced.

**Which words typically get reduced?**

- Articles: a, an, the
- Prepositions: of, to, for, from, at
- Pronouns: you, he, she, her, him
- Auxiliary verbs: do, does, can, have, was, were
- Linkers: and, but, or

**Tip:** Understanding the schwa sound is one of the biggest keys to improving your listening comprehension. Native speakers aren't "swallowing" words — they're simply reducing unstressed words to schwa sounds, which is perfectly natural English.

## 7. Grammar — Agreement with "So" & "Neither"

You can use **"so"** or **"neither"** as short replies to show you have something in common with the other person. Both are followed by an **auxiliary verb** (do, did, can, have, etc.) and a **subject pronoun** (I, we, they, etc.).

**"So" — Agreeing with something positive:**

- Structure: **So + auxiliary verb + subject**
- *Example: "I love this series." — "So do I."* (= I love it too)
- *Example: "She can speak French." — "So can I."*

**"Neither" — Agreeing with something negative:**

- Structure: **Neither + auxiliary verb + subject**
- *Example: "I don't even have a mom." — "Neither do we."*
- *Example: "I've never been to France." — "Neither have I."*
- *Example: "Didn't know playing hooky could be so much fun." — "Yeah, neither did I."*

**Important rules:**

- Use **"so"** for positive statements (agreeing with something good or affirmative).
- Use **"neither"** for negative statements (agreeing with something negative — containing "not," "never," "don't," etc.).
- The auxiliary verb must match the tense and form of the original statement:
  - Present simple: do/does
  - Past simple: did
  - Present perfect: have/has
  - Modal verbs: can, will, would, etc.

**Common mistake:** Using "so" with a negative statement. Never say ~~"So don't I"~~ — always use "Neither do I."

## 8. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 8.1 Ralph Breaks the Internet — The Movie

- Ralph Breaks the Internet (2018) is the sequel to Wreck-It Ralph (2012). It follows Ralph and Vanellope as they travel into the internet to find a replacement part for Vanellope's arcade game, Sugar Rush.
- During their journey, Vanellope stumbles into the "Oh My Disney" website, where she meets all the Disney Princesses in a backstage dressing room.
- The scene is one of the most celebrated moments in the film because it brings together all the Disney Princesses for the first time and makes fun of classic Disney Princess tropes and stereotypes.
- Vanellope (voiced by Sarah Silverman) is the princess of Sugar Rush, a candy-themed racing game. She is a very different kind of princess — no gowns, no songs, no true love's kiss.

---

### 8.2 The Disney Princess Checklist

- The princesses quiz Vanellope with a hilarious checklist of classic Disney Princess experiences:
  - **Magic hair?** — Rapunzel (Tangled) has magical healing hair
  - **Magic hands?** — Elsa (Frozen) has ice powers
  - **Animals talk to you?** — Pocahontas, Snow White, Cinderella, and others communicate with animals
  - **Poisoned?** — Snow White ate a poisoned apple
  - **Cursed?** — Aurora (Sleeping Beauty) was cursed by Maleficent
  - **Kidnapped?** — Rapunzel was kidnapped as a baby by Mother Gothel
  - **Enslaved?** — Belle (Beauty and the Beast) was held captive; Rapunzel was forced to stay in the tower
  - **Deal with a sea witch?** — Ariel (The Little Mermaid) traded her voice to Ursula
  - **True love's kiss?** — Nearly every classic Disney Princess story
  - **Daddy issues?** — Many princesses have complicated relationships with their fathers
  - **No mom?** — Most Disney Princesses are notably motherless
- This scene is Disney poking fun at its own storytelling patterns, acknowledging the recurring themes across decades of princess movies.

---

### 8.3 Merida & "The Other Studio"

- At the end of the scene, Merida (from Brave) speaks, but the other princesses say they can't understand her because "she's from the other studio."
- This is a meta joke with two layers:
  - **Language joke:** Merida speaks with a thick Scottish accent (or Scots language), which is genuinely difficult for other English speakers to understand.
  - **Studio joke:** Brave (2012) was produced by **Pixar Animation Studios**, while all the other Disney Princess movies were made by **Walt Disney Animation Studios**. So Merida is literally "from the other studio."
- This is an example of Disney's self-aware humor, breaking the fourth wall to make a joke about its own corporate structure.

---

### 8.4 True Love's Kiss & Princess Stereotypes

- The "million dollar question" — whether all of Vanellope's problems were solved because a big strong man showed up — is the ultimate Disney Princess stereotype that the movie is deliberately deconstructing.
- In classic Disney films (Snow White, Sleeping Beauty, Cinderella), the princess is typically rescued by a prince, and "true love's kiss" breaks whatever curse she's under. The princesses' enthusiastic "Yeah! Yes! What is up with that?" shows even they recognize this pattern is problematic.
- Vanellope's disgusted reaction to true love's kiss ("Ew, barf!") represents a more modern, kid-friendly perspective that rejects the idea that romance is the only path to happiness.
- The scene where the princesses change into comfortable clothes (T-shirts, sweatpants, pajamas) and crown Vanellope "the queen of comfy" symbolizes their liberation from the constraints of traditional princess expectations — fancy gowns, perfect posture, and fairy tale romance.
- This is part of a broader trend in modern Disney films (Frozen, Moana, Brave) where the princess saves herself rather than waiting for a prince.
