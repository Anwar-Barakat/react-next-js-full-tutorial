## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **The coast is clear** — An expression meaning it is safe to do something or go somewhere without being seen or noticed.
  - *Example: Come on, the coast is clear — let's go!*

- **To get rid of (something/someone)** — To throw away or remove something you don't want. For people: to make someone go away because they are causing problems.
  - *Example: We're pretending to move to Yemen — it's the only way I can get rid of her.*

- **Card key** — A type of card (similar to a credit card) that works as a key, scanned electronically to open doors.
  - *Example: I need to get the card key for the door.*

- **Men's room** — A restroom for men in a public building (hotel, restaurant, office). Women's equivalent: **women's room** or **ladies' room**.
  - *Example: He ran into the men's room to hide.*

- **Weird** — Strange or odd. **Weirdest** = superlative (the most weird).
  - *Example: That is the weirdest thing you have ever said.*

- **To turn (into/adjective)** — To change state, causing something to become something else or look different.
  - *Example: The weather has suddenly turned cold.*
  - *Example: Did she turn invisible?*

- **To disappear** — To cease to be visible; to vanish.
  - *Example: Did she disappear? Did she turn invisible?*

- **Gotcha** — An exclamation meaning "I have got you." Used to say you understood someone, or when catching/surprising someone.
  - *Example: "He's in the director's chair." — "Gotcha."*
  - *Example: Sully catches Boo by surprise: "Gotcha!"*

- **Boo** — An exclamation said to scare someone (what ghosts say). Also Boo's name in the movie — because she often says it.
  - *Example: Once you name it, you start getting attached to it.*

- **Blossom** — As a noun: another word for flower. As a verb: to produce many flowers.
  - *Example: The cherry trees are blossoming this spring.*

- **Tender** — Soft or gentle. If someone is tender, they are fragile, sensitive, or easily bruised. **Tenderly** = in an attentive or loving way.
  - *Example: He tenderly kissed her on the cheek.*

- **Oozing** — Normally: flowing slowly out of something through a small opening. Used here as a poetic adjective (in monster culture, gross things are pleasant).
  - *Example: The cut was oozing blood.*

- **Fabulous** — Wonderful, excellent, or very beautiful (common informal use in fashion).
  - *Example: You're looking fabulous today!*

- **Lift and tuck** — Referring to a facelift surgery — plastic surgery to remove wrinkles or sagging skin.
  - *Example: You've had a lift. You've had a tuck. Something has been inserted in your skin.*

- **Favor** — Something you ask someone to do for you as a kindness.
  - *Example: Listen, I need a favor.*

- **Scare floor** — In the movie: the floor of the factory where monsters go through doors to scare children.
  - *Example: Randall was working late last night out on the scare floor.*

- **To harvest** — To collect or gather (literally: crops; figuratively: screams for energy in the movie).
  - *Example: Monsters harvest children's screams into energy.*

- **Toxic** — Very harmful or poisonous to health.
  - *Example: Human children are believed to be toxic to monsters.*

- **Portal** — A doorway or entrance, especially one that leads to another world or dimension.
  - *Example: Doors activate portals to children's bedroom closets.*

- **Attached (to something/someone)** — Having a feeling of affection or fondness for something.
  - *Example: Once you name it, you start getting attached to it.*

- **Wrinkles** — Lines or creases in the skin, typically associated with aging.
  - *Example: A facelift removes wrinkles and sagging skin.*

- **Sagging** — Drooping or hanging down loosely.
  - *Example: Plastic surgery to remove sagging skin typical of aging.*

- **Sincere** — Genuine and honest; not pretending.
  - *Example: Mike is not being sincere — he's flattering Roz to get a favor.*

- **To flatter** — To give excessive compliments, often insincerely, to gain favor.
  - *Example: Mike flatters Roz by calling her his "tender, oozing blossom."*

- **Scarer** — In the movie: a monster whose job is to scare children and collect their screams.
  - *Example: Skilled monsters working as scarers go into the human world.*

- **Recruits** — New members of an organization, often still in training.
  - *Example: We've hired some new scare recruits, and they're inexperienced.*

- **Inexperienced** — Lacking experience or knowledge in a particular area.
  - *Example: The new recruits are inexperienced.*

## 4. Expressions & Idioms

- **The coast is clear** — It is safe to proceed without being seen.
  - *Example: The perimeter looks to be all clear, Captain.*

- **Get rid of** — To remove or eliminate something/someone unwanted.
  - *Example: All we have to do is get rid of that thing.*

- **Loves it here / Like it here** — Enjoying the current location. Note: "I like here" (without "it") is grammatically incorrect. Always say: **"I like it here"** or **"I love it here."**
  - *Example: Look, it loves it here!*
  - *Example: You're gonna love it there, Princess.*

- **That's got to be** — Informal for "that must be" — expressing certainty.
  - *Example: That's got to be a new haircut!*

- **I have no idea** — I don't know at all.
  - *Example: Where did she go? I just have no idea.*

## 5. Pronunciation — The Three "-ed" Sounds

The past simple and past participle of regular verbs end in "-ed," but there are three different ways to pronounce it:

**1. Full /ɪd/ sound** — When the verb ends in a T or D sound:
- want → want**ed** /wɑntɪd/
- trade → trad**ed** /treɪdɪd/
- paint → paint**ed** /peɪntɪd/

**2. Unvoiced /t/ sound** — When the verb ends in these sounds (p, k, f, s, sh, ch):
- hope → hop**ed** /hoʊpt/
- laugh → laugh**ed** /læft/
- wash → wash**ed** /wɑʃt/
- watch → watch**ed** /wɑtʃt/
- like → lik**ed** /laɪkt/
- fax → fax**ed** /fækst/

**3. Voiced /d/ sound** — When the verb ends in all other sounds:
- tag → tagg**ed** /tæɡd/
- use → us**ed** /juːzd/
- call → call**ed** /kɔːld/
- clean → clean**ed** /kliːnd/
- offer → offer**ed** /ɑfɚd/
- claim → claim**ed** /kleɪmd/
- rub → rubb**ed** /rʌbd/
- play → play**ed** /pleɪd/
- scare → scar**ed** /skɛrd/

**Tip:** Don't try to memorize the lists — this is something you'll naturally get used to the more you listen and practice.

## 6. Grammar — Superlatives Review

Superlatives express the highest degree of a quality:

**Short adjectives:** adjective + **-est**
- cool → cool**est**
- fast → fast**est**
- smart → smart**est**
- weird → weird**est**
- nice → nic**est**
- gentle → gentl**est**
- warm → warm**est**

**Long adjectives:** **most** + adjective
- intelligent → **most** intelligent
- creative → **most** creative
- fascinating → **most** fascinating
- beautiful → **most** beautiful

*Example: "That is the weirdest thing you have ever said."*
*Example: "Who is the most beautiful monster in all of Monstropolis?"*

## 7. Grammar — "It" as a Pronoun for Non-Humans

- The pronoun **"it"** is neutral and normally used for objects, animals (not pets), and anything that is not a human.
- In Monsters Inc., Mike refers to Boo as **"it"** instead of **"she"** because monsters see humans like animals.
- **Important:** Referring to a person as "it" is generally considered offensive in real life.
- The humor comes from the contrast — the audience sees Boo as a cute child, but Mike treats her as a strange creature.

*Example: "Look, it loves it here." / "Get rid of that thing."*
*Contrast: Sully starts calling her "she" — showing he's becoming attached.*

## 8. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 8.1 The Monsters Inc. Universe

- The city of **Monstropolis** is powered by energy from the screams of human children.
- At the Monsters Inc. factory, skilled monsters called **scarers** go through special doors (portals) into human children's bedrooms to scare them and harvest their screams into energy.
- Human children are believed to be **toxic** (dangerous to monsters' health).
- In the movie, a child named **Boo** accidentally enters the monster world, and Mike and Sully must return her safely.

---

### 8.2 Monster Humor — Reversed Standards

- In the monster world, things humans find gross are considered pleasant.
- Monster deodorant scents include: "Smelly Garbage," "Old Dumpster," "Low Tide," and "Wet Dog."
- Mike calls Roz his "tender, **oozing** blossom" — "oozing" would be unpleasant to humans but is a compliment in monster culture.
- This reversal of standards is a recurring source of humor throughout the film.

---

### 8.3 Flattery and Favors

- Mike's conversation with Roz is a perfect example of **flattery** — giving excessive, insincere compliments to get something you want.
- He calls her his "tender, oozing blossom," compliments her appearance, and suggests she's had cosmetic surgery — all to butter her up before asking for a favor (the card key).
- Roz's dry response — "This office is now closed" — shows that the flattery failed completely.

---

### 8.4 "Once You Name It, You Start Getting Attached"

- When Mike names the child "Boo," Sully warns him: "You're not supposed to name it. Once you name it, you start getting attached to it."
- This reflects a real-world truth — naming something (a pet, a stray animal) creates an emotional bond.
- This line foreshadows the emotional connection that develops between Sully and Boo throughout the film.
- It also shows the difference between Mike (who sees Boo as a problem) and Sully (who begins to care for her).
