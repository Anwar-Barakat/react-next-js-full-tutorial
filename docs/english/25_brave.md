## 1. Scene 1 — The Argument

- **Queen Elinor:** Mighty me! I've just about had enough of you, lass!
- **Merida:** You're the one that— you've embarrassed me! I followed them! You don't know what you've done!
- **Queen Elinor:** It'll be fire and sword if it's not set right!
- **Merida:** This whole marriage is what you want! Do you ever bother to ask what I want? No! You walk around telling me what to do, what not to do, trying to make me be like you! Well, I'm not going to be like you!
- **Queen Elinor:** Oh, you're acting like a child! You're a beast, that's what you are!
- **Merida:** I'd rather die than be like you!
- **Queen Elinor:** You are a princess! I expect you to act like one!

## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Selfish** — Only concerned about oneself; not having consideration for other people's feelings. Synonyms include self-centered, egoistic, and narcissistic.
  - *Example: I've been selfish.*

- **Rift** — Literally: a crack or split in something, like a break in the ground. Figuratively: a break in a friendly relationship; people not getting along anymore. Synonyms include estrangement, quarrel, and fallout.
  - *Example: I tore a great rift in our kingdom.*

- **Amend** — To make something better; to improve it. Often used when referring to laws or formal corrections.
  - *Example: I need to amend my mistake.*
  - *Example: The law was amended to allow women to vote.*

- **Mend** — To repair something that is broken or damaged.
  - *Example: I need to mend our bond.*

- **Bond** — A connection between people because they share similar feelings, experiences, or interests. Can also be used as a verb meaning to develop a close connection.
  - *Example: I need to mend our bond.*
  - *Example: Let's go bond with daddy.*

- **Matter** — Can mean different things depending on context: (1) a situation that needs to be dealt with, (2) used to ask if someone is unhappy or if there's a problem ("What's the matter?"), (3) "doesn't matter" means it's not important.
  - *Example: There is the matter of my betrothal.*
  - *Example: What's the matter? You don't look so well.*
  - *Example: It doesn't matter. It's in the past.*

- **Betrothal** — An old-fashioned word for "engagement" — a mutual promise of marriage. In the past and in some cultures today, this is commonly arranged by the parents of the future couple.
  - *Example: There is the matter of my betrothal.*

- **Unfair** — Not right because not everyone had an equal opportunity or was treated justly.
  - *Example: This is so unfair!*
  - *Example: It's unfair to punish everybody because of one person's mistake.*

- **Beast** — Literally: a wild animal. When used to describe a person (old-fashioned), it means they are unpleasant, annoying, or behaving badly.
  - *Example: You're a beast, that's what you are!*

- **Embarrass** — To make someone feel ashamed, uncomfortable, or self-conscious in front of others.
  - *Example: You've embarrassed me!*

## 4. Scottish English Vocabulary

- **Mighty me** — A Scottish and Irish exclamation of surprise or disappointment. Old-fashioned even in Scotland. Roughly equivalent to "oh dear," "amazing," or "what have you done."
  - *Example: Mighty me! I've just about had enough of you, lass!*

- **Lass** — A Scottish and Northern English word used to refer to a young woman, similar to "girl."
  - *Example: I've just about had enough of you, lass!*

- **Lassie** — A fond, diminutive form of "lass," used to refer to little girls.
  - *Example: Come here, lassie.*

- **Clan** — A group of people descending from a common ancestor. Clans are a significant part of Scottish Highlands culture, with household names like MacDonald, Mackenzie, and Mackintosh still in existence today.
  - *Example: The oldest sons of three different clans arrive to dispute Merida's hand in marriage.*

## 5. Conflict & Relationships Vocabulary

- **To blame** — To point to a person as guilty or responsible for something bad. Can be used with different prepositions: "blame someone for something," "put the blame on someone," "take the blame."
  - *Example: There's no one to blame but me.*

- **To bother** — Two different meanings: (1) "Don't bother doing something" means you simply don't do it — you don't make the effort. (2) "Something bothers you" means it makes you annoyed or irritated.
  - *Example: Do you ever bother to ask what I want?*
  - *Example: Doesn't that bother you?*

- **To expect** — (1) To require or consider something obligatory. (2) To believe something will happen.
  - *Example: I expect you to act like a princess!*
  - *Example: Hope for the best but expect the worst.*

- **To act** — To behave in a certain manner. "Acting like a child" means being immature.
  - *Example: You're acting like a child!*
  - *Example: I expect you to act like a princess.*

- **Suitor** — A man who wants to marry a particular woman; a potential husband.
  - *Example: Finding the best suitor for Merida to marry.*

## 6. Expressions & Idioms

- **I've had enough of (this/you)** — Used when you are tired or angry about a situation and want it to stop.
  - *Example: I've just about had enough of you, lass!*

- **I've had it up to here** — A stronger version of "I've had enough." Often accompanied by a hand gesture at the forehead or above the head, showing you've reached your limit.
  - *Example: That's it! I've had it up to here!*

- **I'm sick and tired of** — Another way to express frustration with a recurring situation.
  - *Example: I'm sick and tired of making minuscule profits.*

- **Fire and sword** — An expression meaning there will be violent fights and even death. Used to describe the most extreme consequences of a conflict.
  - *Example: It'll be fire and sword if it's not set right!*

- **To set something right** — To fix something and make it return to its previous position or status; to correct a wrong.
  - *Example: It'll be fire and sword if it's not set right!*
  - *Example: We'll set it right now. Tell me everything.*

- **To be there for someone** — To be available to provide support by listening, comforting, or helping when the person needs it.
  - *Example: You're never there for me!*

- **To make amends** — To correct a mistake or to do something good for a person you wronged, in order to make up for it.
  - *Example: Why not make amends before you leave? It's the least you can do.*

- **How could you?** — A phrase expressing surprise or strong disapproval of another person's behavior.
  - *Example: How could you? (after the stepmother destroys the dress in Cinderella)*

## 7. Grammar — "I'd Rather" for Preferences

The expression **"I'd rather"** (contraction of "I would rather") is used to talk about your preference between two things. It is followed by the **base form** of a verb.

**From the movie:**
- *"I'd rather die than be like you!"* — Merida expresses an extreme preference to emphasize how strongly she feels.

**Structure:**
- **I'd rather** + verb + **than** + verb
- *I'd rather watch a movie at home than go to the cinema.*
- *I'd rather stay home than go out tonight.*

**Alternative — "I'd prefer":**
- "I'd prefer" can be used instead, but the grammar changes slightly:
- **I'd prefer** + verb-ing + **to** + verb-ing
- *I'd prefer watching a movie at home to going to the cinema.*

**Tip:** "I'd rather" is more common in everyday speech. "I'd prefer" is slightly more formal.

## 8. Grammar — "But" Meaning "Except"

The word **"but"** is commonly used to express contrast between two ideas:
- *I love traveling, but I hate packing.*

However, "but" can also mean **"except"** or **"apart from"**:

**From the movie:**
- *"There's no one to blame but me."* — This means "there's no one to blame **except** me." Merida is saying she is the only one guilty.

**More examples:**
- *Everyone was invited but John.* (= Everyone except John)
- *I have nothing but respect for her.* (= I have only respect)
- *Who but a fool would do that?* (= Who except a fool)

**Tip:** When "but" means "except," it usually appears after words like "no one," "nothing," "everyone," "anything," or "who."

## 9. Pronunciation — Scottish English

Brave is set in Scotland, and the characters speak with Scottish accents. Here are the main features to listen for:

**1. Rhotic R — Rolling and fully pronouncing the R:**
- One of the most recognizable features of Scottish English is the strong, rolled R sound.
- While many English accents (like standard British RP) drop the R at the end of words or before consonants, Scottish speakers **fully pronounce** every R.
- *Example: "warriors," "scarred," "northern"* — the R is clearly rolled and audible.

**2. Vowel sounds — Different from standard English:**
- Scottish vowels can sound different from standard American or British pronunciation.
- The word **"you"** in standard English has a long U sound /juː/. In Scottish English, the intonation often goes up and the final sound is elongated, sounding more like "yoo-uh."
- *Example: "Trying to make me be like you!"* — Listen to how Merida's "you" sounds different.

**3. Musical rhythm — Different stress patterns:**
- Scottish English has a distinctive musical rhythm because the stress on certain syllables is placed differently than in standard English.
- This gives the accent a unique melody that is immediately recognizable.

**Tip:** You don't need to imitate the Scottish accent, but being able to recognize it helps you understand a wider range of English speakers. Scotland is part of the UK, and like the rest of Britain, it has many different regional accents.

## 10. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 10.1 Brave — The Movie

- Brave (2012) is a Disney Pixar animated film set in 10th-century Scotland.
- The story follows Merida, an intrepid princess who defies the tradition of arranged marriage by competing in an archery competition for her own hand — and winning.
- Her actions cause a rift between the clans and infuriate her mother, Queen Elinor. After a bitter argument, Merida seeks out a witch and asks for a spell to "change her mother," which accidentally transforms Elinor into a bear.
- Mother and daughter must work together to break the spell, learning to understand and accept each other in the process.
- Brave was the first Pixar film with a female protagonist and the first set in a real historical period and place.

---

### 10.2 Scottish Clans and Betrothal

- **Clans** are extended family groups that have been central to Scottish Highlands culture for centuries. Each clan has its own tartan (plaid pattern), territory, and traditions.
- Famous clan names include MacDonald, Mackenzie, Mackintosh, and MacGregor — the "Mac" or "Mc" prefix means "son of."
- In the movie, the oldest sons of three clans arrive to compete for Merida's hand in marriage — this reflects the historical practice of **betrothal** (arranged engagement) to form alliances between powerful families.
- Merida's rebellion against this tradition drives the plot: by competing for her own hand and winning, she defies centuries of custom and triggers a political crisis among the clans.

---

### 10.3 Mother-Daughter Conflict

- The argument between Merida and Queen Elinor is rich with expressions of frustration: "I've had enough of you," "this is so unfair," "you're never there for me," "do you ever bother to ask what I want?"
- Both characters use extreme language: Elinor calls Merida a "beast" and says she's "acting like a child," while Merida says "I'd rather die than be like you."
- By the second scene, Merida has matured. She admits she's been "selfish," takes full responsibility ("there's no one to blame but me"), and commits to "amend my mistake and mend our bond."
- The resolution is not that Merida submits to tradition, but that both mother and daughter learn to respect each other: Merida acknowledges her selfishness, and Elinor supports her daughter's right to "follow her heart and find love in her own time."

---

### 10.4 Mend vs Amend vs Make Amends

- These three words look similar but have distinct meanings:
  - **Mend** — To physically repair something that is broken or damaged. *"I need to mend our bond."* (Figuratively: to repair a relationship.)
  - **Amend** — To make something better or to improve it, especially formal documents or laws. *"The law was amended to allow women to vote."*
  - **Make amends** — To do something good for a person you wronged, in order to correct a past mistake and restore the relationship. *"Why not make amends before you leave?"*
- Merida uses both "amend" and "mend" in the same sentence: *"I need to amend my mistake and mend our bond"* — she wants to correct what she did wrong (amend) and repair the broken relationship (mend).
