## 1. Scene 1 — Judy Arrives at the Police Station

- **Wolf Officer:** Come on, he bared his teeth first!
- **Judy:** Excuse me... down here!
- **Clawhauser:** Hi! Oh my goodness!
- **Clawhauser:** They really did hire a bunny! What?!
- **Clawhauser:** I gotta tell you, you are even cuter than I thought you'd be!
- **Judy:** Uh, you probably didn't know, but a bunny can call another bunny cute, but when other animals do it... it's a little...
- **Clawhauser:** Oh! I am so sorry! Me, Benjamin Clawhauser — the guy everyone thinks is just a flabby, donut-loving cop stereotyping you!
- **Judy:** Oh, it's okay. Oh, you've actually... you've actually got... there's a... in your neck, the fold... the...
- **Clawhauser:** Oh! Who, you went... you little dickens!
- **Judy:** I should get to roll call. So, which way do I—
- **Clawhauser:** Oh! Bullpen's over there to the left.
- **Judy:** Great, thank you!
- **Clawhauser:** Oh, that poor little bunny's gonna get eaten alive.

## 2. Scene 2 — Roll Call / Briefing Room

- **Judy's Neighbor:** Hey, Officer Hopps! You ready to make the world a better place?
- **Officer:** All right, all right, everybody sit!
- **Chief Bogo:** I've got three items on the docket.
- **Chief Bogo:** First, we need to acknowledge the elephant in the room. Francine — happy birthday!
- **Chief Bogo:** Number two: there are some new recruits with us I should introduce, but I'm not going to — because I don't care.
- **Chief Bogo:** Finally, we have 14 missing mammal cases. All predators — from a giant polar bear to a teensy little otter. And City Hall is right up my tail to find them. This is priority number one.
- **Chief Bogo:** Assignments! Officers Grizzoli, Fang, Delgato — your teams take missing mammals from the Rainforest District. Officers McHorn, Rhinowitz, Wolford — your teams take Sahara Square. Officers Higgins, Snarlov, Trunkaby — Tundratown.
- **Chief Bogo:** And finally, our first bunny — Officer Hopps... parking duty. Dismissed!

## 3. Scene 3 — Judy Confronts the Chief

- **Judy:** Chief! Chief Bogo! Sir, you said there were 14 missing mammal cases. So, I can handle one. You probably forgot, but I was top of my class at the Academy.
- **Chief Bogo:** Didn't forget. Just don't care.
- **Judy:** Sir, I'm not just some token bunny.
- **Chief Bogo:** Well then, writing 100 tickets a day should be easy.
- **Judy:** 100 tickets? I'm not gonna write 100 tickets. I'm gonna write 200 tickets — before noon!

## 4. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **To bare your teeth** — To show hostility or a threatening reaction (from animal behavior).
  - *Example: The dog bared its teeth at the stranger.*

- **OMG / Oh my goodness** — Expression of surprise or excitement; "goodness" softens the religious reference.
  - *Example: Oh my goodness, you scared me!*

- **To hire** — To employ someone.
  - *Example: I was hired by the first company I applied to.*

- **Gotta (got to / have to)** — Informal contraction meaning "have to" or "need to".
  - *Example: I gotta tell you something important.*

- **Flabby** — Soft and fat (describing a body part).
  - *Example: Exercise helps flatten a flabby stomach.*

- **Stereotype** — A preconceived, often prejudiced notion about a group.
  - *Example: That's a harmful stereotype about teenagers.*

- **Fold** — A part that covers or bends over another part.
  - *Example: She folded the napkin neatly on the table.*

- **You little dickens** — Playful expression of surprise or mild annoyance (usually toward children).
  - *Example: You little dickens, give me back my wallet!*

- **Roll call** — Reading names aloud from a list to check attendance.
  - *Example: The teacher does roll call every morning.*

- **Bullpen** — An open-floor-plan room (police/newsroom); literally, an enclosure for bulls.
  - *Example: The detectives all work together in the bullpen.*

- **Eaten alive** — To be completely overwhelmed or defeated by someone stronger.
  - *Example: He was afraid he'd be eaten alive in that meeting.*

- **To hop** — To make a small jump (associated with bunnies).
  - *Example: The rabbit hopped across the garden.*

- **Docket** — A list of things to be discussed or acted upon.
  - *Example: I've got three items on today's docket.*

- **Acknowledge** — To accept, admit, or recognize something.
  - *Example: He barely even acknowledges me at work.*

- **The elephant in the room** — An obvious problem or issue that people avoid discussing.
  - *Example: We need to address the elephant in the room.*

- **Recruit** — A new member of an organization (often military/police).
  - *Example: The fraternity has 50 new recruits this semester.*

- **Mammal** — An animal whose females feed young with milk from their own body.
  - *Example: Humans, dogs, and dolphins are all mammals.*

- **Missing** — Lost; location unknown.
  - *Example: My phone is missing — have you seen it?*

- **Predator** — An animal that hunts, kills, and eats other animals.
  - *Example: Lions, wolves, and foxes are all predators.*

- **Teensy** — Very small (same as tiny / teeny).
  - *Example: The movie was a teensy bit boring.*

- **Assignment** — A piece of work given to someone.
  - *Example: The teacher gave us a homework assignment.*

- **Parking duty** — Responsibility for managing parking enforcement.
  - *Example: Judy was assigned parking duty on her first day.*

- **Token** — Someone included only to give the appearance of diversity.
  - *Example: I'm not just some token hire.*

- **Priority** — Something regarded as more important than other things.
  - *Example: Finding the missing animals is priority number one.*

- **Dismissed** — A command (military/police) meaning "you may leave now; the meeting is over."
  - *Example: The sergeant said "Dismissed!" and the officers left the room.*

- **To handle** — To deal with or manage a situation or task.
  - *Example: I can handle one missing mammal case.*

- **Top of one's class** — Ranked first or among the best in a group of students.
  - *Example: She graduated top of her class at the Academy.*

- **Academy** — A specialized training school, often for police or military.
  - *Example: He spent six months at the police academy before becoming an officer.*

- **Gonna (going to)** — Informal contraction of "going to," used in casual speech.
  - *Example: I'm gonna write 200 tickets before noon!*

- **Bunny** — An informal, affectionate word for a rabbit.
  - *Example: They really did hire a bunny!*

- **Cop** — An informal word for a police officer.
  - *Example: The guy everyone thinks is just a flabby, donut-loving cop.*

- **To confront** — To face someone directly, especially to challenge them about a problem.
  - *Example: Judy confronts the chief about the missing mammal cases.*

- **Ticket** (parking ticket) — A written notice of a fine given for a parking violation or traffic offense.
  - *Example: Writing 100 tickets a day should be easy.*

- **Noon** — 12:00 PM; midday.
  - *Example: I'm gonna write 200 tickets — before noon!*

- **Giant** — Extremely large in size.
  - *Example: From a giant polar bear to a teensy little otter.*

- **Otter** — A small aquatic mammal with thick fur that lives near rivers and seas.
  - *Example: From a giant polar bear to a teensy little otter.*

- **Polar bear** — A large white bear that lives in Arctic regions.
  - *Example: All predators — from a giant polar bear to a teensy little otter.*

- **Sir** — A formal or respectful way to address a man, especially someone in authority.
  - *Example: Chief! Chief Bogo! Sir, you said there were 14 missing mammal cases.*

## 5. Connected Speech — The American T

When a **T** sound appears between two vowel sounds, it morphs into a **soft D** sound. This is called the **American T** (or flap T).

**Examples:**

- **butter** → budder
- **getting** → gedding
- **better** → bedder
- **gotta** → godda

**In the movie:** "I gotta tell ya" — the T in "gotta" sounds like a soft D.

---

## 6. Dropping the T in NT Clusters

When the letters **NT** appear together, native speakers often **drop the T** sound entirely.

**Examples:**

- **internet** → innernet
- **international** → innernational
- **I can't eat that** → I can' eat that

**In the movie:** Chief Bogo says "elephant in" but it sounds like "elephan-in" (no T).

---

## 7. Dropping Auxiliary Verbs in Informal Questions

In casual speech, natives often remove auxiliary verbs (are, do, have) at the start of questions.

**Examples:**

- **Are you ready?** → You ready?
- **Do you think?** → You think?
- **Is he coming?** → He coming?
- **Have you seen it?** → You've seen it?

## 8. Attend-hut! (Military Term)

- An American military command meaning **"Attention!"**
- Used in police/military settings to call a room to order.
- Shortened from "Attention" over time.

## 9. Double Meanings & Puns in Zootopia

Zootopia uses wordplay brilliantly by applying human expressions to an animal world, creating both literal and figurative meanings simultaneously.

---

### 9.1 "He bared his teeth first!"

- **Figurative:** To show hostility toward someone.
- **Literal (in Zootopia):** A wolf actually showing his teeth. The double meaning makes it humorous.

---

### 9.2 "The elephant in the room"

- **Figurative:** An obvious issue everyone is avoiding.
- **Literal (in Zootopia):** There is literally an elephant in the room — it's Francine, and it's her birthday!

---

### 9.3 "Eaten alive"

- **Figurative:** To be completely overwhelmed or defeated.
- **Literal (in Zootopia):** In an animal world, someone could literally be eaten alive.

---

### 9.4 "City Hall is right up my tail"

- **Original expression:** "Up my butt" — meaning someone is pressuring you intensely.
- **In Zootopia:** Changed to "tail" because Chief Bogo is a buffalo. Also keeps it kid-friendly.

---

### 9.5 "Bullpen"

- **Standard meaning:** An open-plan office room (common in police stations).
- **Literal (in Zootopia):** An actual enclosure for bulls — fitting in a city of animals!

---

### 9.6 Judy Hopps (Character Name)

- **The pun:** Judy is a bunny, and bunnies hop. Her last name "Hopps" is a play on the word "hop" (a small jump).

---

### 9.7 "Cute" as an In-Group Word

- Judy explains that a bunny can call another bunny "cute," but when other animals say it, it's offensive.
- This is a clever, kid-friendly reference to how certain words can be acceptable within a group but discriminatory when used by outsiders.
- Touches on themes of prejudice and empowerment.

## 10. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 10.1 Discrimination & Prejudice

- Despite being a kids' movie, Zootopia addresses complex social issues like discrimination, stereotyping, and prejudice.
- The "cute" scene mirrors real-world dynamics where words carry different weight depending on who says them.

---

### 10.2 Stereotyping

- Clawhauser recognizes that he's stereotyping Judy (as a small, helpless bunny) the same way people stereotype him (as a fat, donut-loving cop).
- This self-awareness is a key teaching moment.

---

### 10.3 Token Representation

- Judy uses the phrase "token bunny," highlighting that she doesn't want to be hired just for appearances — she wants to prove her worth.
- This reflects real-world discussions about diversity and meaningful inclusion.

---

### 10.4 Softening Religious Words

- English speakers often soften expressions involving religious words.
- "Oh my God" becomes "Oh my goodness."
- "Little devil" becomes "little dickens."
- This is common in polite or kid-friendly speech.
