# Learn English with The Emperor's New Groove Guide

A comprehensive English lesson based on scenes from Disney's The Emperor's New Groove (2000), covering colloquial phrases and informal vocabulary (thingy, pal, sharp, getaway), idioms (pull the wool over someone's eyes, give the word, everything goes your way), connected speech (dropping final D, reducing "to"), and cultural themes about the Inca Empire, the city of Cusco, and the film's message about humility.

## 1. Scene 1 — Kuzco Announces His Plan

- **Kuzco:** Welcome to Kuzcotopia, my ultimate summer getaway, complete with waterslide!
- **Kuzco:** What? Isn't it great? It's my birthday gift to me. I'm so happy.
- **Pacha:** I don't understand how this could happen.
- **Kuzco:** Well, let me clear it up for you. At my birthday celebration tomorrow, I give the word and your town will be destroyed to make way for this.
- **Kuzco:** So if I were you, I'd pick up some change of address forms on the way home.
- **Pacha:** But... but, um, where will we live?
- **Kuzco:** Hmm, don't know, don't care. How's that?
- **Pacha:** Oh, but wait, you can't—
- **Kuzco:** When I give the word, your little town thingy will be bye-bye.
- **Kuzco:** Oh yeah, everything was going my way.

## 2. Scene 2 — Kuzco Decides Not to Destroy the Village

- **Kuzco:** No, no. I'm sharp. I'm on it.
- **Kuzco:** Looks like you and your family are stuck on the tuneless hilltop forever, pal.

## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Ultimate** — Used to say that something is the best or most important. It can also be used negatively to mean the worst or least important.
  - *Example: My ultimate summer getaway, complete with waterslide!*
  - *Example: Things are working out according to my ultimate design.*

- **Getaway** — A place where you go on vacation; a holiday destination.
  - *Example: My family has a summer getaway at the beach.*

- **Waterslide** — A sloping structure for sliding down that ends in a swimming pool or area of water.
  - *Example: My ultimate summer getaway, complete with waterslide!*

- **Ridge** — A long, narrow raised part of a surface, especially a high edge along a mountain.
  - *Example: You said when the sun hits this ridge just right, these hills sing.*

- **Drag** — To move something by pulling it along a surface, usually the ground.
  - *Example: Well, pal, I was dragged all over those hills.*
  - *Example: They captured him, dragged him off.*

- **Sharp** — When describing a person: very smart and able to easily understand what is going on.
  - *Example: No, no. I'm sharp. I'm on it.*

## 4. Informal & Colloquial Vocabulary

- **Thingy** — An informal word used when you don't remember (or don't care about) someone or something's name. Kuzco uses it arrogantly to show that Pacha's village is unimportant to him.
  - *Example: Your little town thingy will be bye-bye.*
  - *Example: Resume your, uh... space guy thingy.*

- **Pal** — An informal word for "friend" or "buddy." Usually used among men and very common in American English.
  - *Example: Well, pal, I was dragged all over those hills.*
  - *Example: Looks like you and your family are stuck on the tuneless hilltop forever, pal.*

- **Bye-bye** — An informal, childish way of saying "goodbye." When Kuzco says the village will be "bye-bye," he means it will be gone or destroyed — said dismissively.
  - *Example: Your little town thingy will be bye-bye.*

## 5. Expressions & Idioms

- **Clear something up** — To clarify or explain something in a way that makes it easier for another person to understand. Also means to cause the end of a problem by talking about it or dealing with it.
  - *Example: Well, let me clear it up for you.*
  - *Example: I might be able to clear up this little misunderstanding.*

- **Give the word / Say the word** — To give an order. Kuzco means that simply by giving the order, Pacha's village would be destroyed immediately. "Say the word" is an alternative with the same meaning.
  - *Example: I give the word and your town will be destroyed.*
  - *Example: Just say the word and you're a prince again.*

- **Everything goes your way** — Everything happens according to what you want; the situation is favorable to you.
  - *Example: Oh yeah, everything was going my way.*
  - *Example: It seems that things are going his way at last.*

- **Pull the wool over someone's eyes** — An idiom that means to trick or deceive someone; to hide the truth from someone.
  - *Example: Couldn't pull the wool over your eyes, huh?*
  - *Example: He was too clever to let them pull the wool over his eyes.*

- **I'm on it** — In this context, Kuzco means he knows what Pacha was trying to do (trick him) and he was smarter than that. More generally, "I'm on it" means "I'm aware" or "I'm taking care of it."
  - *Example: No, no. I'm sharp. I'm on it.*

- **Change of address form (COA)** — A request to the postal service to re-route your mail to a new address when you move. This is humorously anachronistic in the film since the story takes place in the ancient Inca Empire, long before postal services existed.
  - *Example: So if I were you, I'd pick up some change of address forms on the way home.*

## 6. Connected Speech — Dropping Sounds & Reductions

**1. "Isn't it great" — Dropping final T and linking:**

- The T in **"isn't"** and **"it"** is dropped, and the N from "isn't" joins with the vowel I in "it."
- The phrase is spoken fast and continuously.
  - Full: **"Isn't it great?"**
  - Connected: **"Isn-it great?"** (one smooth unit)

**2. "So you lied to me" — Dropping final D and reducing "to":**

- The D in **"lied"** is not pronounced.
- The word **"to"** gets reduced to just a D sound.
  - Full: **"So you lied to me"**
  - Connected: **"So you lie-d'me"** (sounds almost like "you lie da me")

**These patterns in summary:**

- **Final T dropping** — Very common, especially before consonants or in fast speech.
- **Final D dropping** — Happens frequently with past tense verbs ending in "-ed."
- **"To" reduction** — The word "to" is commonly reduced to a D or schwa sound in connected speech.

**Tip:** These reductions happen naturally in fast speech. You don't need to copy them perfectly, but recognizing them will dramatically improve your ability to understand native speakers in movies and conversations.

## 7. Music & Sound Vocabulary

- **Tune** — A song or melody.
  - *Example: "Ziggy Stardust" is a tune by David Bowie.*

- **In tune** — When someone sings well or when music sounds pleasant and harmonious.
  - *Example: She always sings in tune.*

- **Tuneless** — Having no tune; sounding unpleasant or lacking melody. Kuzco calls Pacha's village "tuneless" to mock the idea that the hills "sing."
  - *Example: Looks like you and your family are stuck on the tuneless hilltop forever, pal.*

## 8. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 8.1 The Emperor's New Groove — The Movie

- The Emperor's New Groove (2000) is a Disney animated comedy set in the Inca Empire. It follows Emperor Kuzco, a selfish and egotistical young ruler who plans to destroy a local village to build an extravagant summer home for himself.
- Kuzco's advisor, Yzma, tries to poison him to take over the throne, but her assistant Kronk accidentally mixes the poison with a potion that turns Kuzco into a llama.
- Lost and helpless as a llama, Kuzco ends up in Pacha's village — the very place he planned to destroy. Pacha agrees to help Kuzco return to the palace, and through their adventures together, Kuzco learns the importance of thinking about others and being humble.
- The film is known for its rapid-fire humor, breaking the fourth wall (Kuzco narrates his own story), and being one of Disney's most quotable comedies.

---

### 8.2 Cusco & the Inca Empire

- The main character's name, **Kuzco**, comes from **Cusco** (also spelled Cuzco), a city in southeastern Peru.
- Cusco was the **capital of the Inca Empire** from the 13th century to the 16th century, when it was conquered by Spanish colonizers.
- The Inca Empire (Tawantinsuyu) was the largest empire in pre-Columbian America, stretching along the western coast of South America.
- Today, Cusco is a major tourist destination with approximately 2 million visitors per year, known for its historical architecture and as the gateway to Machu Picchu.
- The film takes creative liberties with Incan history and culture for comedic purposes, blending ancient settings with modern American humor.

---

### 8.3 Kuzco's Arrogance & Character Growth

- Kuzco begins the story as extremely **arrogant and self-centered** — he doesn't even bother to learn the name of Pacha's village, dismissing it as "your little town thingy."
- His language throughout the first scene reflects this: "don't know, don't care," "it's my birthday gift to me," and casually telling Pacha to pick up "change of address forms."
- By the end of the movie, Kuzco has learned humility through his adventures with Pacha. However, the final scene shows that even as a reformed person, he still has traces of his old personality — he frames not destroying the village as a favor he's doing for Pacha, rather than admitting he's changed.
- This subtle humor — where Kuzco is genuinely kind but can't quite admit it — makes his character arc both funny and relatable.

---

### 8.4 Humor Through Anachronism

- One of the film's signature comedy techniques is **anachronism** — placing modern things in an ancient setting:
  - **Change of address forms** — Kuzco tells Pacha to pick up postal forms, even though the story is set thousands of years before postal services existed.
  - **Waterslide** — Kuzco's dream summer home includes a waterslide, a thoroughly modern invention.
  - **Fourth-wall breaking** — Kuzco pauses the movie to narrate his own story directly to the audience.
- This deliberate mixing of ancient and modern is what gives The Emperor's New Groove its unique comedic identity and makes it different from other Disney films that try to stay faithful to their historical settings.
- For English learners, this anachronistic humor is useful because it highlights modern American expressions and concepts in a playful way — making the vocabulary more memorable.
