Covers vocabulary, expressions, connected speech (preposition reduction, T+Y to CH), and cultural themes of honor and identity.

## 1. Scene 1 — Mulan Meets Mushu

- **Mulan:** Who am I fooling? It's going to take a miracle to get me into the army.
- **Mushu:** That's close enough! A ghost! Get ready! Salvation is at hand! For I have been sent by your ancestors to guide you through your masquerade. Come on — if you're going to stay, you're going to work. So heed my words, because if the army finds out you're a girl, the penalty is death.
- **Mulan:** Who are you?
- **Mushu:** Who am I? Who am I? I am the guardian of lost souls! I am the powerful, the pleasurable, the indestructible Mushu!
- **Mulan:** Uh... my ancestors sent a little lizard to help me?
- **Mushu:** Hey — dragon. Dra-gon. Not lizard. I don't do that tongue thing.
- **Mulan:** You're, um... intimidating? Awe-inspiring?
- **Mushu:** Tiny?
- **Mulan:** Tiny.
- **Mushu:** Of course! I'm travel-sized for your convenience. If I was my real size, your cow here would die of fright. Down, Bessie! My powers are beyond your mortal imagination. For instance, my eyes can see straight through your armor.
- **Mulan:** Oh! All right, that's it! Dishonor! Dishonor on your whole family! Make a note of this — dishonor on you, dishonor on your cow!
- **Mushu:** Stop! I'm sorry, I'm sorry — I'm just nervous. I've never done this before.
- **Mulan:** Then you're gonna have to trust me. And don't you slap me no more — we clear on that?
- **Mushu:** All right. Okey-dokey. Let's get this show on the road! Cri-Kee, get the bags — let's move it, hustle!

## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **To fool** — To trick or deceive someone into believing something that is not true.
  - *Example: Who am I fooling? It's going to take a miracle to get me into the army.*

- **Miracle** — A very lucky event that is surprising and unexpected. Also: an extraordinary event believed to be caused by divine power.
  - *Example: It's going to take a miracle to get me into the army.*

- **Salvation** — The act of being saved from danger, harm, or destruction.
  - *Example: Salvation is at hand!*

- **At hand** — Near; close by; about to happen.
  - *Example: Salvation is at hand!*

- **Masquerade** — A situation in which you pretend to be something that you are not. Also: a formal party where people wear masks and costumes.
  - *Example: I have been sent by your ancestors to guide you through your masquerade.*

- **To heed** — To listen to and follow someone's advice or warning carefully.
  - *Example: Heed my words, because if the army finds out you're a girl, the penalty is death.*
  - *Example: Hear my words and heed my warning.*

- **Penalty** — A punishment imposed for breaking a rule, law, or agreement.
  - *Example: The penalty is death.*

- **Guardian** — Someone who protects or watches over something or someone.
  - *Example: I am the guardian of lost souls!*

- **Soul** — The spiritual part of a person that is believed to continue existing after the body has died.
  - *Example: I am the guardian of lost souls!*

- **Pleasurable** — Enjoyable; giving pleasure or satisfaction. Mushu uses it to say he is pleasant company.
  - *Example: I am the powerful, the pleasurable, the indestructible Mushu!*

- **Indestructible** — Impossible to destroy.
  - *Example: I am the powerful, the pleasurable, the indestructible Mushu!*

- **Lizard** — A type of small reptile with four legs, a long body, and a long tail. Mulan confuses Mushu with a lizard, offending him.
  - *Example: My ancestors sent a little lizard to help me?*

- **Dragon** — A large, powerful mythical creature, usually depicted as breathing fire. In Chinese culture, dragons are symbols of power and good fortune.
  - *Example: Hey — dragon. Dra-gon. Not lizard.*

- **Intimidating** — Making you feel frightened or nervous; threatening.
  - *Example: You're, um... intimidating?*

- **Awe-inspiring** — Causing a feeling of great respect and wonder. If you feel great respect towards someone and maybe a bit of fear, you can say that person is awe-inspiring.
  - *Example: You're, um... intimidating? Awe-inspiring?*

- **Awe** — A feeling of wonder and respect, sometimes mixed with fear.
  - *Example: You might feel awe standing on the edge of the Grand Canyon.*

- **Tiny** — Extremely small.
  - *Example: Of course! I'm travel-sized for your convenience.*

- **Travel-sized** — Small enough to carry while traveling. Often used for small toiletry products (shampoo, toothpaste) that can be taken on airplanes.
  - *Example: I'm travel-sized for your convenience.*

- **Convenience** — The quality of being easy or useful; something that saves effort.
  - *Example: I'm travel-sized for your convenience.*

- **Fright** — A sudden, intense feeling of fear.
  - *Example: If I was my real size, your cow here would die of fright.*

- **Armor** — Protective metal clothing worn by soldiers in battle.
  - *Example: My eyes can see straight through your armor.*

- **To slap** — To hit someone or something quickly with the flat, open palm of your hand.
  - *Example: Don't you slap me no more — we clear on that?*

- **Nervous** — Feeling worried, anxious, or uneasy about something.
  - *Example: I'm just nervous. I've never done this before.*

- **Hustle** — To move or work quickly and energetically; to hurry.
  - *Example: Let's move it, hustle!*

- **Bunch** — A group of people or things.
  - *Example: You're the saddest bunch I ever met.*

- **Tranquil** — Calm and peaceful; free from disturbance.
  - *Example: Tranquil as a forest, but on fire within.*

- **Within** — Inside; on the inside of something.
  - *Example: Tranquil as a forest, but on fire within.*

- **To defeat** — To win a victory over someone in a battle or competition.
  - *Example: Let's get down to business — to defeat the Huns.*

- **Serpentine** — Curving and twisting like a snake. Related to the word "serpent" (a large snake).
  - *Example: The road up the mountain was serpentine.*

## 4. Mythology & Cultural Vocabulary

- **Ancestors** — Members of your family who lived before you, especially a long time ago.
  - *Example: I have been sent by your ancestors to guide you through your masquerade.*

- **Mortal** — A living being, especially a person, who is able to die (in contrast with gods or demigods who live forever).
  - *Example: My powers are beyond your mortal imagination.*

- **Honor** — A very important value in many cultures representing a mix of qualities such as respect, pride, and honesty.
  - *Example: We are counting on you to uphold the family honor.*

- **Dishonor** — The loss or absence of honor; shame or disgrace brought upon someone or their family.
  - *Example: Dishonor! Dishonor on your whole family!*
  - *Example: You dishonor me.*

- **Huns** — A group of nomadic warriors from the fourth and fifth centuries who invaded parts of Europe and Asia. In the movie, they are the enemy army that China must defend against.
  - *Example: Let's get down to business — to defeat the Huns.*

- **Emperor** — The ruler of an empire; in this case, the Emperor of China.
  - *Example: I'm ready to serve the Emperor.*

## 5. Insults & Descriptions

From the song "I'll Make a Man Out of You," Shang uses harsh insults to describe the untrained soldiers:

- **Spineless** — Having no determination or courage; unwilling to take risks. Literally: having no spine (backbone).
  - *Example: You're a spineless, pale, pathetic lot.*

- **Pale** — Having a lighter skin color than usual, often due to being very frightened, ill, or weak.
  - *Example: You're a spineless, pale, pathetic lot.*

- **Pathetic** — Causing feelings of pity or sadness; so bad or weak that it's almost pitiful.
  - *Example: You're a spineless, pale, pathetic lot.*

- **Lot** — A group of people or things (informal).
  - *Example: You're a spineless, pale, pathetic lot.*

## 6. Expressions & Idioms

- **Who am I fooling?** — A rhetorical question meaning "I'm not deceiving anyone" — expressing the realization that you can't trick anyone.
  - *Example: Who am I fooling? It's going to take a miracle.*

- **Get down to business** — To start talking about or dealing with a subject that needs to be discussed or worked on seriously.
  - *Example: Let's get down to business — to defeat the Huns.*

- **Make a man out of (someone)** — To transform someone into a strong, capable person. Based on the stereotype of men being strong, unemotional, and heroic.
  - *Example: Somehow I'll make a man out of you.*

- **Haven't got a clue** — A common expression meaning you have no knowledge or understanding about a certain subject.
  - *Example: You haven't got a clue.*

- **Let's get this show on the road** — An informal expression meaning "let's get started" or "let's begin moving."
  - *Example: Okey-dokey. Let's get this show on the road!*

- **Okey-dokey** — An informal, playful way of saying "okay."
  - *Example: Okey-dokey. Let's get this show on the road!*

- **Down (command)** — A command given to an animal (usually a dog) telling it to lie down. Mushu humorously uses it on Mulan's horse, calling it "Bessie" (a nickname for cows).
  - *Example: Down, Bessie!*

- **Make a note of this** — To write something down or remember it for future reference.
  - *Example: Make a note of this — dishonor on you!*

- **We clear on that?** — An informal way of asking "Do we understand each other?"
  - *Example: Don't you slap me no more — we clear on that?*

## 7. Connected Speech — Preposition Reduction ("To" → "Ta")

In natural speech, the preposition **"to"** is often reduced to just **"ta"** /tə/.

**From the movie:**
- Mulan says **"to help me"** → sounds like **"ta help me"** /tə hɛlp mi/

**More examples:**
- **"have to stay away from"** → "have ta stay away from"
- **"going to look like"** → "going ta look like"
- **"time to think"** → "time ta think"

**Why this matters:**
- Natives almost never say a full, stressed "to" /tuː/ in casual speech.
- If you listen for the full word "to," you'll miss it — because natives reduce it to a quick, unstressed "ta."
- This is one of the most common reductions in spoken English.

## 8. Connected Speech — T+Y Becomes CH

When a word ending in **T** is followed by a word starting with **Y** (or a "you" sound), the two sounds often merge into a **CH sound** /tʃ/.

**From the movie:**
- **"don't you"** → sounds like **"don'tchu"** /doʊntʃuː/
- Mushu says: *"Don't you slap me no more"* → sounds like *"Don'tchu slap me no more"*

**More examples:**
- **"won't you"** → "won'tchu"
- **"can't you"** → "can'tchu"
- **"what you"** → "whatchu"
- **"got you"** → "gotcha"
- **"let you"** → "letchu"

**Tip:** This is extremely common in American English. Once you start listening for it, you'll hear it everywhere.

## 9. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 9.1 Mulan — The Movie

- Mulan (1998) is a Disney animated film based on the Chinese legend of Hua Mulan, a young woman who disguises herself as a man to take her aging father's place in the army.
- The film is set in Imperial China during a war against the Huns, a group of invading warriors.
- Mulan is accompanied by Mushu, a small dragon sent by her ancestors to protect her, voiced by comedian Eddie Murphy.
- The movie addresses themes of identity, gender roles, family honor, and self-discovery.

---

### 9.2 Honor in Chinese Culture

- Honor is a central theme in Mulan and reflects its deep importance in Chinese culture.
- The concept of **family honor** means that one person's actions can bring pride or shame to the entire family.
- Mulan's father says: *"We are counting on you to uphold the family honor"* — showing the weight of this responsibility.
- When Mulan is caught being a woman in the army, it brings **dishonor** — which in some cultures is considered worse than death.
- Mushu's comedic line *"Dishonor on you, dishonor on your cow!"* is a humorous take on this very serious cultural concept.

---

### 9.3 Mushu's Comedy

- Mushu is one of Disney's most iconic comic relief characters.
- Much of his humor comes from the contrast between his grand, dramatic self-descriptions ("the powerful, the pleasurable, the indestructible") and his tiny, non-threatening appearance.
- He confuses Mulan's horse for a cow, calling it "Bessie" and commanding it to lie down.
- He uses modern slang and casual language ("okey-dokey," "let's get this show on the road," "hustle") in an ancient Chinese setting, creating an anachronistic humor.
- Eddie Murphy's voice performance brings energy and improvised-feeling comedy to the character.

---

### 9.4 "Be a Man" — Stereotypes and Change

- The song "I'll Make a Man Out of You" is built on traditional gender stereotypes — that being a "man" means being strong, brave, unemotional, and heroic.
- Shang insults his recruits by calling them "spineless," "pale," "pathetic," and saying they "haven't got a clue."
- The irony is that the best soldier in the group turns out to be Mulan — a woman.
- This phrase ("be a man") was once very common but is used less frequently nowadays as society's understanding of gender roles evolves.
- The movie subtly challenges these stereotypes by showing that courage, determination, and intelligence are not limited to one gender.
