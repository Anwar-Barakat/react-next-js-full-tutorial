# Learn English with Snow White Guide

A comprehensive English lesson based on scenes from the Snow White and the Huntsman (2012) live-action film, covering phrasal verbs (rip out, hand over), promise and trust expressions (keep your word, you have my word, give me what I was promised), emotional expressions (you deserve better, I failed you), grammar points (passive voice, possessive pronouns, avoiding repetition with auxiliary verbs), connected speech (flap T in "let her," reducing "her" to "er," reducing "of" to schwa), and cultural themes about fairy tale adaptations, the huntsman's moral conflict, and the evil queen archetype.

## 1. Scene 1 — Snow White Asks the Huntsman for Help

- **Snow White:** Will you help me? Please, please. The queen's going to kill me.
- **Huntsman:** Sure she is.
- **Snow White:** She's going to rip out my heart.
- **Guard:** Quick work. Hey. Well done. Hand her over.

- **Guard:** Why do you care? You want her, hey? Give me what I was promised. You did your job well. Now keep your word.
- **Snow White:** He'll kill us both.
- **Huntsman:** Shut up.
- **Snow White:** He will.
- **Huntsman:** I said shut up.
- **Snow White:** No, no.

- **Snow White:** She's going to betray you.

## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Stench** — A very bad, strong, and unpleasant smell. Literally, it refers to a terrible odor. Figuratively, it can mean the bad effect that follows an unpleasant event — something negative that stays with you.
  - *Example (literal): You'd have your own wing where no one would ever complain about your stench.*
  - *Example (figurative): I carried with me the stench of death and the anger of the lost.* (The huntsman means the terrible experience of war stayed with him throughout his life.)

- **Intend** — To plan or mean to do something.
  - *Example: What do you intend to do with her?*

- **Sustain** — To keep something going; to maintain life or strength.
  - *Example: She can take life away or sustain it.*

- **Fool** — A stupid person; someone who acts unwisely. Used as an insult.
  - *Example: She can't bring your wife back from the dead, you fool.*

## 4. Phrasal Verbs

- **Rip out** — To remove, tear, or pull something out of someone, especially in a violent, forceful manner.
  - *Example: She's going to rip out my heart.*

- **Hand someone over** — To give a person to someone else, usually under some form of authority or demand. Can also be used with things.
  - *Example: Hand her over.*
  - *Example: Hand over the mask or you'll get a taste of this.*

- **Let someone out of your sight** — To allow someone to go where you can no longer see or watch over them. Often used in the context of losing someone you were supposed to protect.
  - *Example: I let her out of my sight and she was gone.*

## 5. Expressions & Idioms

- **Why do you care?** — A blunt question implying that something is none of the other person's business. Used to push someone away from a matter that doesn't concern them.
  - *Example: What do you intend to do with her? — Why do you care?*

- **What's it to you?** — Another common question with the same meaning as "Why do you care?" — it implies the subject is none of your business.
  - *Example: What's it to you what I do on my weekends?*

- **Give me what I was promised** — A demand to receive something that was previously agreed upon. This uses the passive voice to refer to a past promise without specifying who made it.
  - *Example: Give me what I was promised. You did your job well.*

- **Keep your word** — To do what you said you would do; to fulfill a promise. The opposite — "break your word" — means to fail to keep a promise.
  - *Example: Now keep your word.*
  - *Example: I'll keep my word when the queen keeps hers.*

- **You have my word** — A common phrase used to make a promise or give an assurance.
  - *Example: But you will spare no effort? — Your Majesty, of course. You have my word.*

- **You deserve better** — Used to tell someone that they deserve a better person, situation, or outcome than what they received. Commonly used in the context of romantic relationships.
  - *Example: You both deserved better, and I'm so sorry I failed you.*
  - *Example: You deserve much better than me, Erica.*

- **I failed you** — Used when you weren't able to keep a promise you made or finish a task that was expected of you. An admission of having let someone down.
  - *Example: I'm so sorry I failed you.*
  - *Example: You know the penalty if you fail.*

## 6. Grammar — Passive Voice & Possessive Pronouns

**1. The Passive Voice — "I was promised":**

The passive voice is used when the focus is on the action or the receiver of the action, rather than on who performed it. It is especially useful when the doer is unknown, unimportant, or obvious from context.

- **Active:** My brother told me he would be on vacation.
- **Passive:** I was told my brother would be on vacation. (Who told me is not specified.)

- **Active:** A man on the street gave me a card.
- **Passive:** I was given a card on the street. (Who gave me the card is not clear.)

- **Active:** My dad promised me a new video game.
- **Passive:** I was promised a new video game. (Who promised is not specified.)

- *From the movie: "Give me what I was promised."* (The huntsman doesn't need to say who promised him — it's understood from context.)

**2. Possessive Pronouns — Avoiding Repetition:**

Possessive pronouns express ownership and help avoid unnecessary repetition of nouns:

- **My → Mine:** Whose books are these? They're my books. → They're **mine**.
- **Your → Yours:** My car broke down. Please lend me your car. → Please lend me **yours**.
- **His → His:** These DVDs are old. They're his DVDs. → They're **his**.
- **Her → Hers:** I'll keep my word when the queen keeps her word. → When the queen keeps **hers**.
- **Their → Theirs:** The family lives in this house. It's their house. → It's **theirs**.

- *From the movie: "I'll keep my word when the queen keeps **hers**."* (Instead of repeating "her word.")

---

## 7. Grammar — Avoiding Repetition with Auxiliary Verbs

Just like possessive pronouns, you can use **auxiliary verbs** (did, do, will, has, etc.) to avoid repeating the same verb phrase:

- **Full:** I wasn't going to read this book, but I read the book anyway.
- **Reduced:** I wasn't going to read the book, but I **did** anyway.

- **Full:** You run 5 kilometers and I'll run 5 kilometers too.
- **Reduced:** You run 5 kilometers and I'll **do** too.

- **Full:** Jonathan hasn't played guitar, but Eddie has played guitar.
- **Reduced:** Jonathan hasn't played guitar, but Eddie **has**.

- *From the movie: "I wasn't worth saving, that's for sure, but she **did** so anyway."* (Instead of repeating "she saved me anyway.")

## 8. Connected Speech — Flap T, Reducing "Her" & Schwa

**1. "Let her" — Flap T and reducing "her":**

- The **T** in **"let"** connects with the pronoun "her" and becomes a **flap T** (sounds like a soft D).
- The pronoun **"her"** is reduced to just **"er"** (the H is dropped).
- Together, "let her" sounds like **"letter."**
  - Full: **"I let her out of my sight."**
  - Connected: **"I ledder out of my sight."**

**2. "Out of" — Flap T and schwa reduction:**

- The final **T** in **"out"** also becomes a **flap T** and connects with the word "of."
- **"Of"** is reduced to a schwa sound: **"uh."**
- Together, "out of" sounds like **"ouda."**
  - Full: **"Out of my sight."**
  - Connected: **"Ouda my sight."**

**3. "Kill us" — L linking with a vowel:**

- The **L** in **"kill"** links directly with the vowel **U** in "us," creating one smooth unit: **"kill-us"** (no pause between the words).
  - Full: **"He'll kill us both."**
  - Connected: **"He'll killus both."**

**Tip:** When the final **L** of a word connects with a following vowel sound, the transition is very smooth — the tongue stays in the L position and glides into the vowel. This happens constantly in natural English speech.

## 9. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 9.1 Snow White — The Story & Adaptations

- Snow White is one of the most famous fairy tales in the world, originally published by the Brothers Grimm in 1812. The story of the beautiful princess, the jealous Evil Queen, the poisoned apple, and the seven dwarfs has been adapted countless times.
- The most well-known version is Disney's Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs (1937), which was Disney's first full-length animated feature film and a landmark in cinema history.
- The 2012 live-action film Snow White and the Huntsman, which this lesson is based on, reimagines the story as a darker, more action-oriented tale. In this version, the Huntsman (played by Chris Hemsworth) becomes a central character who develops feelings for Snow White (played by Kristen Stewart).
- The Evil Queen is played by Charlize Theron, who delivers the iconic line "Magic mirror on the wall, who is the fairest one of all?" — one of the most recognizable phrases in all of fairy tale literature.

---

### 9.2 The Huntsman's Moral Conflict

- The Huntsman is one of the most interesting characters in this adaptation because of his moral conflict. He is hired by the Evil Queen to capture Snow White, with the promise that his dead wife will be brought back to life.
- When he discovers the queen cannot keep her promise ("She can't bring your wife back from the dead, you fool"), he must choose between serving the queen and protecting Snow White.
- His line **"I'll keep my word when the queen keeps hers"** is a powerful moment — he uses the language of promises and honor to challenge the queen's authority. The possessive pronoun "hers" (instead of repeating "her word") makes the dialogue more natural and dramatic.
- The Huntsman's farewell speech in Scene 2 is emotionally rich vocabulary for English learners: "stench of death," "anger of the lost," "I wasn't worth saving," "you deserve better," "I failed you." Each phrase expresses a different shade of guilt, love, and regret.

---

### 9.3 The Evil Queen — A Classic Archetype

- The Evil Queen is one of the most enduring villain archetypes in storytelling. Her defining traits — vanity, jealousy, and cruelty — have appeared in countless stories across cultures.
- Her opening lines use formal, almost archaic English: "Magic mirror on the wall, who is the fairest one of all?" and the mirror responds with equally formal language: "Famed is thy beauty, Majesty, but hold — a lovely maid I see."
  - **"Thy"** is an old English word for "your"
  - **"Famed"** means famous or well-known
  - **"Maid"** means a young woman (archaic usage)
  - **"Fairest"** means the most beautiful (superlative of "fair")
- This formal, poetic language contrasts sharply with the more modern, casual dialogue in the rest of the film, creating a sense of ancient magic and timelessness around the queen and her mirror.

---

### 9.4 "L" Linking with Vowels in Connected Speech

- One of the connected speech patterns highlighted in this lesson is how the final **L** of a word connects smoothly with a following vowel sound:
  - **"Kill us"** → "killus" (one smooth unit)
  - **"All of us"** → "alluhvus"
  - **"Tell us"** → "tellus"
- This happens because the L sound is produced with the tongue touching the roof of the mouth, and when the next sound is a vowel, the tongue simply glides from the L position into the vowel without stopping.
- This linking pattern is one of the reasons English can sound like "one long word" to learners. Recognizing it helps you separate words in fast speech and dramatically improves your listening comprehension.
- **Practice tip:** Try saying these phrases slowly, then gradually speed up while keeping the L-to-vowel connection smooth: "tell us," "call it," "feel it," "all of them."
