# Learn English with The Little Mermaid Guide

A comprehensive English lesson based on scenes from Disney's The Little Mermaid (1989), covering vocabulary related to emotions and fear (cold feet, guppy, withdrawn), talking and gossip (blabber, gossip, prattle, idle, bore), Ursula's iconic song vocabulary (swoon, fawn, toll, gulp), connected speech (dropping final T, flap T, H-dropping in "her"), and cultural themes about Disney villains, wordplay, and deals with the sea witch.

## 1. Scene 1 — Ariel and Flounder Explore a Sunken Ship

- **Ariel:** Wait for me!
- **Flounder:** Hurry up!
- **Ariel:** No, I can't swim that fast. There it is! Isn't it fantastic?
- **Flounder:** Yeah, sure. It's great. Now let's get out of here.
- **Ariel:** Oh, you're not getting cold fins now, are you?
- **Flounder:** Me? No way. It's just it... it looks damp in there, yeah. And yeah, I think I may be coming down with something. Yeah, I got this cough.
- **Ariel:** Alright, I'm going inside. You can just stay here and watch for sharks, okay?
- **Flounder:** Yeah, you go. I'll stand. What? Sharks? Ariel! Ariel! I can't — I mean, Ariel, help!
- **Ariel:** Oh, Flounder! Do you really think there might be sharks around here?
- **Flounder:** Flounder, don't be such a guppy!
- **Ariel:** Guppy?
- **Flounder:** This is great! I mean, I really love this — excitement, adventure, danger lurking around every corner.

## 3. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Hurry up** — Used to tell someone to do something faster. The word "hurry" is also used in the phrase "to be in a hurry" — when you have to move faster because you're late for something.
  - *Example: Even though I told him to hurry up, he didn't move any faster.*

- **Damp** — Slightly wet, especially in a way that is not pleasant or comfortable.
  - *Example: It looks damp in there.* (ironic since they're already at the bottom of the ocean)
  - *Example: The room was damp and smelly so we opened the windows and gave it a good airing.*

- **Guppy** — A small tropical fish commonly kept in aquariums because of their bright colors. They are usually eaten by larger fish. When Ariel tells Flounder "don't be such a guppy," she means don't be so scared.
  - *Example: Flounder, don't be such a guppy!*

- **Lurk** — To stay hidden, waiting to attack or appear. Can be used literally (a person hiding) or figuratively (danger waiting).
  - *Example: Danger lurking around every corner.*

- **Sweetcakes** — A term of affection for someone, similar to "sweetheart" or "honey."
  - *Example: You've got it, sweetcakes.*
  - *Example: And so do you, sweetcakes.*

- **Withdrawn** — Feeling shy and quiet; not wanting to talk to people.
  - *Example: But they dote and swoon and fawn on a lady who's withdrawn.*

- **Gulp** — Two meanings: (1) To eat or drink something quickly or in large amounts. (2) To make a swallowing movement because you are afraid, surprised, or excited.
  - *Example: Take a gulp and take a breath.*
  - *Example: He gulped down his glass of water and answered the call.*

## 4. Talking & Gossip Vocabulary

- **Blabber** — To talk a lot, especially in a way that people find annoying.
  - *Example: The men out there don't like a lot of blabber.*
  - *Example: Better than being stuck in the middle of nowhere with you and your blabbering mouth.*

- **Gossip** — To talk about other people's private lives. Gossip usually carries the negative idea of sharing unkind, disapproving, or false things about a person.
  - *Example: They think a girl who gossips is a bore.*
  - *Example: Tell us what's going on! What's all the gossip?*

- **A bore** — Something or someone that is boring; a dull, uninteresting person or thing.
  - *Example: They think a girl who gossips is a bore.*
  - *Example: Oh, that small woman was such a bore.*

- **Idle** — Not particularly useful; having no purpose. Synonyms: aimless, futile.
  - *Example: What is idle prattle for?*
  - *Example: My grandma loves to engage in idle chatter with her neighbors.*

- **Prattle** — To talk about things that are silly or not important, and for a long time. When Ursula sings "what is idle prattle for," she questions the importance of talking a lot about unimportant things.
  - *Example: And after all, dear, what is idle prattle for?*
  - *Example: Carmen made everyone angry when she used the entire lunch meeting to prattle on about her new car.*

- **Zip** — Two uses: (1) Means "not at all" or "nothing at all." (2) In the phrase "zip it" — slang meaning to tell someone to stop talking in a rude and angry way.
  - *Example: No more talking, singing, zip!*
  - *Example: Just said zip it, you did! Unbelievable!*

## 5. Expressions & Idioms

- **Cold feet / Cold fins** — "To get cold feet" means to suddenly become too scared to do something you had planned to do. Ariel adapts this as "cold fins" since Flounder is a fish — a humorous wordplay.
  - *Example: You're not getting cold fins now, are you?*
  - *Example: I was going to try bungee jumping, but I got cold feet.*

- **No way** — Two uses: (1) A forceful way to refuse something (strong "no"). (2) An expression of surprise.
  - *Example: Me? No way!* (refusal)
  - *Example: No way!* (surprise)

- **Come down with something** — To start getting sick, like when you get the flu or catch a cold. Used when you begin feeling symptoms such as a headache, fever, or a cough.
  - *Example: I think I may be coming down with something.*
  - *Example: Man, I feel like I'm coming down with something.*

- **Don't be such a [something]** — A structure used to tell someone to stop behaving in a particular negative way. Similar expressions: "Don't be such a wimp" (coward), "Don't be such a party pooper" (killjoy).
  - *Example: Flounder, don't be such a guppy!*

- **Pay the toll** — Literally: to pay a small fee to use a road or cross a bridge. Figuratively: to pay the price for something you want — nothing comes for free.
  - *Example: If you want to cross a bridge, my sweet, you've got to pay the toll.*

- **On a roll** — Experiencing a successful or lucky period; when things are going well. Used when many good things are happening in sequence.
  - *Example: Now I've got her, boys! The boss is on a roll!*
  - *Example: John won five games in a row and it was obvious he was on a roll.*

- **Swoon and fawn** — Both words have similar meanings: to feel a lot of love for someone and give them a lot of attention. "Swoon" implies being overwhelmed by romantic feeling; "fawn" implies giving excessive flattery or attention.
  - *Example: But they dote and swoon and fawn on a lady who's withdrawn.*

## 6. Connected Speech — Final T, Flap T & H-Dropping

**1. Dropping the final T:**

- It is very common for native speakers **not to pronounce the final T** in many words, especially when the next word begins with a consonant.
- When the next word begins with a **vowel sound**, the N sound connects with the following vowel instead.
  - *Example: "Isn't it fantastic?"* → The final T in "isn't" is dropped, and the N connects with the I in "it": **"isn-it fantastic?"**
  - *Example: "I haven't got all day."* → The final T in "haven't" is dropped: **"haven' got all day"**

**2. The Flap T (T sounds like D):**

- When a final T is followed by a **vowel**, it often sounds like a **D** or a soft flap T, and connects smoothly with the next word.
  - *Example: "Got all"* → sounds like **"god-all"**
  - *Example: "I haven't got all day"* → sounds like **"I haven' god-all day"**
  - *Example: "Got her"* → sounds like **"god-er"**

**3. H-Dropping in "her":**

- It is very common for native speakers to drop the H sound in the word **"her"** and simply say **"er."**
  - *Example: "Now I've got her"* → sounds like **"Now I've god-er"**
  - The T in "got" becomes a flap D, and "her" loses its H, creating one smooth unit: **"god-er"**

**Tip:** These three features — dropping final T, the flap T, and H-dropping — often work together in the same phrase. Recognizing them is key to understanding fast native speech in movies and everyday conversation.

## 7. Ursula's Song — "Poor Unfortunate Souls"

Ursula's song is rich with vocabulary about communication and silence. She is trying to convince Ariel that her voice is worthless by arguing that men don't value women who talk:

**Words about talking (presented negatively by Ursula):**

- **Blabber** — talking too much, annoyingly
- **Gossip** — talking about others' private lives
- **Prattle** — talking about unimportant things
- **Idle** — useless, without purpose
- **Conversation** — general talking (presented as something men "avoid")

**Words about being silent (presented positively by Ursula):**

- **Withdrawn** — shy, quiet, not wanting to talk
- **Body language** — communication through gestures and posture, not words

**Words about romantic attention:**

- **Swoon** — to be overwhelmed by romantic feeling
- **Fawn** — to give excessive attention and flattery
- **Dote** — to give someone a lot of love and attention

**The irony:** Ursula's entire argument is deliberately manipulative. She claims men prefer silent women, but the real reason she wants Ariel's voice is because it's Ariel's most powerful asset — without it, Ariel will be unable to make Prince Eric fall in love with her, ensuring Ursula wins their deal.

## 8. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 8.1 The Little Mermaid — The Movie

- The Little Mermaid (1989) is a Disney animated film based on the fairy tale by Hans Christian Andersen. It is widely credited with launching the "Disney Renaissance" — a period of critically and commercially successful animated films in the 1990s.
- The story follows Ariel, the youngest daughter of King Triton, who lives in the underwater city of Atlantica. Ariel is fascinated by the human world and loves collecting human objects from sunken ships.
- After saving Prince Eric from a shipwreck, Ariel falls in love with him. Her father discovers her obsession with the surface world and destroys her collection in anger.
- Frustrated and vulnerable, Ariel makes a deal with Ursula the sea witch: she trades her beautiful singing voice for a pair of human legs, with three days to make Eric fall in love with her — or she belongs to Ursula forever.

---

### 8.2 Wordplay & Irony in the Dialogue

- The first scene is full of clever wordplay based on the characters being underwater:
  - **"Cold fins"** instead of "cold feet" — since Flounder is a fish with fins, not feet.
  - **"Damp"** — Flounder says the ship "looks damp in there," which is ironic because they're already at the bottom of the ocean where everything is wet.
  - **"Guppy"** — Ariel calls Flounder a "guppy" (a small, timid fish) instead of calling him a "wimp" or "coward."
  - **"Coming down with something"** — Flounder pretends to be getting sick as an excuse not to explore the ship.
- This technique of adapting common English expressions to fit the underwater setting makes the dialogue both funny and educational — learners can discover the real expressions behind the wordplay.

---

### 8.3 Ursula — The Iconic Disney Villain

- Ursula is one of Disney's most memorable villains, known for her dramatic personality, powerful voice, and manipulative intelligence.
- Her song "Poor Unfortunate Souls" is a masterclass in persuasion — she uses a combination of flattery, false logic, and pressure to convince Ariel to give up her voice.
- Ursula's argument that "men don't like a lot of blabber" and prefer "a lady who's withdrawn" is deliberately misleading. She knows that Ariel's voice is her greatest tool for connecting with Eric, and removing it is the key to winning their deal.
- The character was inspired by the drag queen Divine and is voiced by Pat Carroll. Her theatrical, over-the-top personality makes her dialogue perfect for learning expressive English vocabulary.

---

### 8.4 "Pay the Toll" & Deals in Fairy Tales

- Ursula's line "If you want to cross a bridge, my sweet, you've got to pay the toll" uses a **literal bridge metaphor** for the idea that everything has a price — nothing comes for free.
- **Toll** literally means a small fee paid to use a road or cross a bridge (many highways and bridges have toll booths). Figuratively, it means the cost or sacrifice required to achieve something.
- This concept of **deals and exchanges** is a recurring theme in fairy tales and Disney movies:
  - **Ariel** trades her voice for human legs (The Little Mermaid)
  - **Belle** trades her freedom for her father's release (Beauty and the Beast)
  - **Meg** sold her soul to save her boyfriend (Hercules)
  - **Tiana** must kiss a frog to break a curse (The Princess and the Frog)
- These deals always come with unexpected consequences, teaching the moral lesson that shortcuts and bargains with villains rarely work out as planned.
