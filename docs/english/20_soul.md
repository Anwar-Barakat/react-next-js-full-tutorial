## 2. Scene 1 — Joe Gets the News

- **Joe:** I'll be right back. Practice your scales.
- **Principal:** Sorry to interrupt, Mr. Gardner — you're doing my ears a favor.
- **Joe:** Hey, not you though — you're good. He's not.
- **Joe:** What can I do for you, Principal Arroyo?
- **Principal:** I wanted to deliver the good news personally. No more part-time for you — you're now our full-time band teacher. Job security, medical insurance, pension.
- **Joe:** Wow, that's, uh... great.
- **Principal:** Welcome to the MS-70 family, Joe. Permanently.
- **Joe:** Thanks.

## 7. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Stuck** — Unable to escape from a bad or boring situation; unable to move or make progress.
  - *Example: I play this jazz teacher who's sort of stuck.*
  - *Example: I've been stuck in that job for eons.*
  - *Example: I just felt stuck.*

- **Throwback** — Something that is associated with or reminds you of the past. On Instagram, "Throwback Thursday" is a day people upload old photos of themselves.
  - *Example: Jazz is a sort of a throwback of a trade.*
  - *Example: Her whole outfit was a throwback to the 1970s.*

- **Trade** — A particular business, industry, or profession; also the skills needed to do a particular job. Not an exact synonym of "job" — it implies specialized skills.
  - *Example: Jazz is a throwback of a trade.*
  - *Example: Some tricks of the acting trade.*

- **Scales** — In music: a series of notes going up or down in pitch, used as practice exercises for musicians.
  - *Example: I'll be right back. Practice your scales.*

- **Substitute teacher** — A teacher who steps in for the regular teacher when they are unavailable due to illness or other reasons.
  - *Example: Joe is a substitute teacher.*

- **Job security** — The assurance that you will keep your job for a long time without risk of being fired.
  - *Example: Job security, medical insurance, pension.*

- **Pension** — A regular payment made to a person after they retire from work.
  - *Example: Job security, medical insurance, pension.*

- **Permanently** — Lasting forever; not temporary.
  - *Example: Welcome to the MS-70 family, Joe. Permanently.*

- **Drummer** — A person who plays the drums.
  - *Example: I'm the new drummer in the Dorothea Williams Quartet.*

- **Quartet** — A group of four musicians who perform together.
  - *Example: The Dorothea Williams Quartet.*

- **To perform** — To play music, act, dance, or entertain in front of an audience.
  - *Example: I would die a happy man if I could perform with Dorothea Williams.*

- **To audition** — To perform in front of a judge or judges to be considered for a role in a play, movie, band, etc.
  - *Example: He finally goes and auditions.*

- **Craft** — A skill or activity that requires special training and practice; your profession or art form.
  - *Example: When you get lost in your craft.*

- **Nerves** — Feelings of anxiety or worry, especially before a performance or important event.
  - *Example: No nerves — he just went.*

- **Soul** — The non-physical part of a person that contains their character, thoughts, and feelings. In many religions, it continues to exist after the body dies.
  - *Example: An accident causes his soul to leave his body.*

- **Heaven / Paradise** — The place where good people are believed to go after they die.
  - *Example: Is this heaven?*

- **Hell** — The opposite of heaven; a place of punishment after death.
  - *Example: Is it H-E-double-hockey-sticks?*

- **H-E-double-hockey-sticks** — A childish way of spelling "hell" without saying it. A hockey stick looks like a capital L, so "double hockey sticks" = LL = hell.
  - *Example: Is it H-E-double-hockey-sticks?*

- **Coyote** — A wild animal similar to a small wolf, native to North America. Pronounced /kaɪˈoʊti/.
  - *Example: Quiet coyote!*

- **Mentor** — An experienced person who advises and helps a less experienced person.
  - *Example: She's had a lot of mentors trying to convince her that life is great.*

- **Suspect (of something)** — To think there is something wrong with something; to be distrustful.
  - *Example: She is very suspect of it.*

- **Depressing** — Making you feel very sad and without hope.
  - *Example: She thinks it sounds scary, painful, difficult, depressing, smelly.*

## 8. Music & Career Vocabulary

- **Gig** — A performance by a musician or group of musicians. Also used informally for any job, especially a temporary one.
  - *Example: There's this big gig that's coming up.*
  - *Example: The Beatles tribute band plays several gigs every weekend.*
  - *Example: What a sweet gig!*

- **Part-time** — Working for only part of the usual working day or week.
  - *Example: No more part-time for you.*

- **Full-time** — Working for the entire usual working day or week (typically 40 hours).
  - *Example: You're now our full-time band teacher.*

- **Tour** — A journey in which a musician or group visits several different places to perform.
  - *Example: We're kicking off our tour with the show at the Half Note tonight.*

- **Jazz** — A music genre that originated in the African American community, known for improvisation, complex harmonies, and a distinctive rhythm.
  - *Example: In his heart, he's a musician — a jazz musician.*

- **Pianist** — A person who plays the piano.
  - *Example: Joe is more specifically a pianist.*

## 9. Expressions & Idioms

- **I'll be right back** — Used to announce you're leaving a place for a short period. Often shortened to "be right back" or "BRB."
  - *Example: I'll be right back. Practice your scales.*

- **Doing my ears a favor** — A humorous way of saying you're relieved to stop hearing something unpleasant (like bad music).
  - *Example: Sorry to interrupt — you're doing my ears a favor.*

- **What can I do for you?** — A polite way to ask someone what they need or want from you.
  - *Example: What can I do for you, Principal Arroyo?*

- **At heart** — Describes someone's true, basic character, sometimes in contrast to what they currently do or pretend to be.
  - *Example: In his heart, he's a musician at heart.*
  - *Example: He's a businessman at heart.*

- **Pursue a dream / Chase a dream / Go after a dream** — To work hard to make your dreams come true. These verbs (pursue, chase, go after) literally mean to try to catch someone — used figuratively because dreams are "elusive."
  - *Example: Jazz music is the dream that Joe's been pursuing on nights and weekends.*

- **How have you been?** — A casual way of asking how life has been since the last time you saw someone. Often reduced to "how you been?"
  - *Example: Hello, how you been, Mr. G?*

- **Are you kidding me?** — An expression of surprise or disbelief.
  - *Example: Dorothea Williams — are you kidding me?*

- **I would die a happy man/woman** — A phrase expressing extreme happiness; saying something would make your life complete.
  - *Example: I would die a happy man if I could perform with Dorothea Williams.*

- **Kick off** — A phrasal verb meaning to start or begin something.
  - *Example: We're kicking off our tour with the show at the Half Note tonight.*

- **Get lost in (something)** — To become so focused on an activity that you forget everything else; to be completely absorbed.
  - *Example: When you get lost in your craft.*

- **Get turned around** — To get lost or confused about your location or direction.
  - *Example: It's easy to get turned around.*

- **Don't want any part of it** — To be unwilling to participate in or be involved in something in any way.
  - *Example: She just doesn't want any part of it.*

- **The great beyond** — Another term for heaven, the afterlife, or a mystical place where the soul goes after death.
  - *Example: His soul almost goes to the great beyond.*

- **Quiet coyote** — A playful way of asking someone (especially children or young souls) to be quiet, accompanied by a hand gesture resembling a coyote closing its mouth.
  - *Example: Quiet coyote! It's easy to get turned around.*

## 10. Connected Speech — Reductions & Linking

**1. "What can I do for you" as one chunk:**
- Natives say this as a single smooth phrase, not seven separate words.
- **"What"** has a stop T: /wɑʔ/
- **"can"** is reduced to /kən/
- **"for"** is reduced to /fɚ/
- Full phrase: /wɑʔkənɑɪduːfɚjuː/

**2. "Have been trying to convince her":**
- **"have"** is reduced to /əv/ and connects to "people": "people-uv"
- The **G** in **"trying"** isn't pronounced: "tryin'"
- **"to"** is reduced and merges with "trying": sounds like "tryna"
- The **H** in **"her"** is dropped: "convince-er"

**3. "But Joe has always dreamed of being something else":**
- The **T** in **"but"** is barely pronounced: /bʌʔ/
- **"has"** and **"always"** have clear Z sounds: /hæz/ /ɔːlweɪz/
- **"dreamed of"** links together: the D connects to "of" → "dreamed-of"

## 11. Pronunciation — Final S as Z Sound

When the letter **S** appears in a final position after a **vowel sound**, it is normally pronounced as a **Z sound** /z/:

- **has** → /hæz/
- **always** → /ɔːlweɪz/
- **dreams** → /driːmz/
- **is** → /ɪz/
- **was** → /wɑz/

**From the movie:**
- *"But Joe has always dreamed of being something else"* — note the clear Z sound in "has" and "always."

## 12. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 12.1 Soul — The Movie

- Soul (2020) is a Disney Pixar animated film about Joe Gardner, a middle-school music teacher who dreams of becoming a professional jazz musician.
- After finally getting his big break — a chance to perform with the legendary Dorothea Williams — Joe has an accident that separates his soul from his body.
- His soul ends up in "the great before," where new souls develop their personalities and interests before being born on Earth.
- There, Joe meets 22, a cynical soul who has never wanted to be born, and the two must work together to get Joe back to his life.
- The film explores deep themes about purpose, passion, and what makes life worth living.

---

### 12.2 Jazz Music as a "Throwback" Trade

- Jamie Foxx describes jazz as "a throwback of a trade" — meaning it's a music genre associated with the past rather than mainstream modern music.
- Jazz originated in the African American communities of New Orleans in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.
- While jazz is deeply respected as an art form, it no longer dominates popular culture the way it once did — making it a "throwback."
- Joe's passion for jazz, despite its niche status, is part of what makes his character compelling — he pursues his dream even when the world doesn't seem to value it.

---

### 12.3 Pursuing Your Dream

- A central theme of Soul is the tension between a stable career and following your passion.
- Joe is offered a permanent, full-time teaching position with job security, medical insurance, and a pension — but his real dream is to play jazz professionally.
- The movie uses words like "pursue," "chase," and "go after" to describe working toward a dream — these words literally mean to try to catch something, reflecting the idea that dreams are elusive and hard to grasp.
- Jamie Foxx says Joe has been "pursuing his dream on nights and weekends his entire adult life" — highlighting the dedication required to chase a dream while maintaining a day job.

---

### 12.4 The Great Beyond and The Great Before

- In the movie's mythology, **the great beyond** is where souls go after death — similar to heaven or the afterlife.
- **The great before** is where new souls develop their personalities, interests, and "spark" before being born on Earth.
- Joe's soul accidentally ends up in the great before instead of the great beyond, giving him a chance to find his way back to his body.
- The counselor says *"It's easy to get turned around"* — meaning it's easy to get confused between these two places.
- Joe is so afraid that he might be in hell that he spells it out as "H-E-double-hockey-sticks" — a childish, humorous euphemism that avoids saying the word directly.
