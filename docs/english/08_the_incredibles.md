## 1. Scene — Edna Mode Designs the Suits

- **Edna:** Cream and sugar, darling?
- **Edna:** I started with the baby. I cut it a little roomy for the free movement. The fabric is comfortable for sensitive skin. It can also withstand a temperature of over 1,000 degrees. Completely bulletproof, and machine washable, darling. That's a new feature.
- **Helen:** What on earth do you think the baby will be doing?!
- **Edna:** I'm sure I don't know, darling. Luck favors the prepared. I didn't know the baby's powers, so I covered the basics.
- **Helen:** Jack-Jack doesn't have any powers.
- **Edna:** No? Well, he'll look fabulous anyway.
- **Edna:** Your boy's suit — I designed it to withstand enormous friction without heating up or wearing out. A useful feature.
- **Edna:** Your daughter — still tricky, but I finally created a sturdy material that will disappear completely as she does.
- **Edna:** Your suit can stretch as far as you can without injuring yourself, and still retain its shape. Virtually indestructible — yet it breathes like Egyptian cotton.
- **Edna:** As an extra feature, each suit contains a homing device, giving you the precise global location of the wearer at the touch of a button.
- **Edna:** Well, darling, what do you think?
- **Helen:** What do I think? Bob is retired. I'm retired. Our family is underground! You helped my husband resume secret hero work behind my back!
- **Edna:** I assumed you knew, darling. Why would he keep secrets from you?
- **Helen:** He wouldn't. He didn't. He doesn't.
- **Edna:** Men at Robert's age are often unstable, prone to weakness.
- **Helen:** What are you saying?
- **Edna:** Do you know where he is?

## 2. Vocabulary & Key Phrases

- **Cream and sugar** — The two additional items normally added to coffee. In the USA, cream is thicker than regular milk — if you want milk, specify it.
  - *Example: Would you like cream and sugar with your coffee?*

- **Stylist** — A professional who designs clothing. In Edna's case, she designs suits for superheroes.
  - *Example: Edna Mode is the most famous stylist in the superhero world.*

- **Roomy** — Having more room or space; spacious. Formed by adding "-y" to the noun "room."
  - *Example: I cut it a little roomy for the free movement.*

- **Fabric** — The material from which clothes are made (e.g., silk, cotton, denim).
  - *Example: The fabric is comfortable for sensitive skin.*

- **Sensitive** — Easily affected or hurt; delicate.
  - *Example: The fabric is comfortable for sensitive skin, like a baby's.*

- **To withstand** — To resist something and remain unaffected.
  - *Example: It can also withstand a temperature of over 1,000 degrees.*

- **Bulletproof** — The quality of resisting and protecting from bullets.
  - *Example: Police officers often wear a bulletproof vest.*

- **Machine washable** — Can be cleaned in a washing machine without damage.
  - *Example: The suit is machine washable — that's a new feature.*

- **Appliance** — A tool or device at home that performs certain jobs (e.g., toaster, hairdryer, oven, washing machine).
  - *Example: A washing machine is a common home appliance.*

- **Feature** — A main characteristic of something that makes it special or able to work better.
  - *Example: One of the features of the new iPhone is its camera's night mode.*

- **On earth** — Added for emphasis in questions (how, why, what, where) to suggest there is no obvious answer.
  - *Example: What on earth do you think the baby will be doing?!*

- **Fabulous** — Wonderful, excellent, or very beautiful. Common informal use in fashion contexts.
  - *Example: He'll look fabulous anyway!*

- **Friction** — (Physics) The force that works against an object as it slides along another surface.
  - *Example: In air hockey, because the puck barely touches the table, there's almost no friction.*

- **Tricky** — Difficult; requiring skill or care.
  - *Example: Your daughter — still tricky, but I finally created a sturdy material.*

- **Sturdy** — Strong and resistant to injuries or damage.
  - *Example: I finally created a sturdy material that will disappear completely as she does.*

- **To stretch** — To extend and make something longer beyond its normal length.
  - *Example: Your suit can stretch as far as you can without injuring yourself.*

- **To retain its shape** — To keep its form or appearance after being used or stretched.
  - *Example: The suit will still retain its shape.*

- **Virtually** — Nearly or almost.
  - *Example: Virtually indestructible — yet it breathes like Egyptian cotton.*

- **Indestructible** — Impossible to destroy.
  - *Example: The suit is virtually indestructible.*

- **Yet** — In this context: used to add something surprising after what was just said.
  - *Example: Simple, elegant, yet it breathes like Egyptian cotton.*

- **To breathe** — (Referring to fabric) To allow air to pass through, preventing sweating.
  - *Example: The suit's fabric allows your skin to breathe.*

- **Homing device** — A mechanism (often GPS-based) that tracks the precise location of something or someone.
  - *Example: Each suit contains a homing device.*

- **Precise** — Exact; accurate and specific.
  - *Example: Giving you the precise global location of the wearer.*

- **Wearer** — The person who is wearing something.
  - *Example: The homing device shows the precise location of the wearer.*

- **Retired** — Having stopped working permanently, usually due to age or by choice.
  - *Example: Bob is retired. I'm retired.*

- **Underground** — Literally: below the earth's surface. Figuratively: hidden from the public; keeping a secret identity.
  - *Example: Our family is underground! (figurative — they hide their superpowers)*
  - *Example: During the Cold War, communists in the USA kept their operations underground.*

- **Behind my back** — Without someone knowing, normally in a way that is unfair or disrespectful.
  - *Example: You helped my husband resume secret hero work behind my back!*

- **To assume** — To think or guess that something is true without actual proof. (Be careful: this is a common false cognate in many languages.)
  - *Example: I assumed you knew, darling.*

- **Unstable** — Not steady or secure; likely to change suddenly (emotionally or physically).
  - *Example: Men at Robert's age are often unstable.*

- **Prone to** — Having a tendency to be affected by something (usually bad) or to do it.
  - *Example: People who are prone to getting the flu should get a flu shot.*

- **To resume** — To begin again after a pause or interruption.
  - *Example: Your husband resumed secret hero work.*

- **To injure** — To hurt or cause physical harm to someone.
  - *Example: Your suit can stretch without injuring yourself.*

- **Enormous** — Extremely large; huge.
  - *Example: Designed to withstand enormous friction.*

## 3. Phrasal Verbs

- **To heat up** — To become warm or hot; to increase in temperature.
  - *Example: The suit withstands friction without heating up.*
  - *Example: Can we eat? Your oven is faster than heating it up in the microwave.*

- **To wear out** — To lose good condition and quality due to being used. Also: to cause something to lose quality.
  - *Example: It's like their bodies are wearing out.*
  - **Adjective form:** worn out — having the appearance of being used.
  - *Example: Some people prefer worn out jeans.*

- **To cover (the basics / all the bases)** — To prepare for needs or possibilities; to give attention to important aspects.
  - *Example: I didn't know the baby's powers, so I covered the basics.*

## 4. Idioms & Sayings

- **Luck favors the prepared** — A saying meaning you are more likely to succeed if you are prepared in advance.
  - *Example: Edna designed the suit with every possible need in mind — luck favors the prepared.*

- **Saying** — A short, well-known statement that generally contains advice or wisdom.
  - *Example: "Never put off until tomorrow what you can do today" is a popular saying.*

- **Cover all the bases** — To prepare for every possibility; to give attention to every aspect of a situation.
  - *Example: We're just covering all the bases — being safe.*

## 5. Terms of Endearment

Words used to address a beloved or familiar person affectionately:

- **Darling** — Used to address a beloved person. Edna uses it constantly.
  - *Example: I'm sure I don't know, darling.*

- **Honey** — Informal term of endearment, very common in American English.
  - *Example: Listen, honey, I've got a quiet evening planned.*

- **Sweetie** — Similar to honey; affectionate way to address someone.
  - *Example: Sweetie, you have more power than you realize.*

## 6. Pronunciation — Silent E

Many four-letter English words end with a **silent E** — you should NOT pronounce it:

- **have** → /hæv/ (NOT "ha-veh")
- **save** → /seɪv/
- **made** → /meɪd/
- **lose** → /luːz/
- **time** → /taɪm/

**Important:** English is a non-phonetic language. Learners often over-pronounce sounds because of their habit in their mother tongue. Learn to depend on your **ears**, not your **eyes**.

## 7. Connected Speech — "Have Any"

In the line **"Jack-Jack doesn't have any powers"**, Helen connects "have" and "any" into one smooth sound:

- **have any** → /hævəni/ — sounds like one word: "havany"

This happens because:
1. The E at the end of "have" is silent
2. Natives naturally link the final consonant of one word to the beginning vowel of the next

**Practice:** "Jack-Jack doesn't have any powers" → "Jack-Jack doesn't havany powers"

## 8. Grammar — Adding "-y" to Form Adjectives

Native speakers often add **"-y"** to the end of nouns to turn them into adjectives or adverbs, even if the word doesn't officially exist in the dictionary:

- **room** → **roomy** (having lots of space)
- **sleep** → **sleepy** (feeling like you want to sleep)
- **rain** → **rainy** (having lots of rain)
- **freak** → **freaky** (strange, weird)
- **chocolate** → **chocolatey** (tasting like chocolate)

*Example: "I cut it a little roomy for the free movement."*

## 9. Cultural & Thematic Notes

### 9.1 Edna Mode's Character

- Edna Mode is a stylish, eccentric fashion designer who specializes in superhero suits.
- She is known for her dramatic personality, her constant use of "darling," and her perfectionist approach to design.
- Despite being small in stature, she is one of the most commanding characters in the movie.
- Her designs combine fashion with incredible technology — bulletproof, fireproof, machine washable, with GPS tracking.

---

### 9.2 Superheroes Living Underground

- In The Incredibles, all superheroes were forced by society to live "underground" — hiding their powers and living as normal citizens.
- Helen says "our family is underground" using the figurative meaning: keeping their superhero identities secret from the public.
- This creates the central conflict of the movie: the tension between wanting to be a hero and being forced to live a normal life.

---

### 9.3 Humor in the Suit Design Scene

- The comedy comes from the contrast between Edna's over-the-top superhero suit features (bulletproof, fireproof, 1,000 degrees) and the fact that they're being designed for a baby.
- Helen's reaction — "What on earth do you think the baby will be doing?!" — highlights the absurdity.
- Edna's calm response — "Luck favors the prepared" — shows her pragmatic, no-nonsense attitude.
- The line "He'll look fabulous anyway" about a powerless baby is peak Edna humor.

---

### 9.4 False Cognates — "Assume"

- "To assume" is a commonly confused word for many English learners.
- In many languages, a similar-sounding word means something different.
- In English, **to assume** means to think or guess something is true **without proof**.
- Edna says "I assumed you knew" — she guessed Helen knew, but she had no proof.
- This is different from "to presume" (which implies more confidence in the guess).
