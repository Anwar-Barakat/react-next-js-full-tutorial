## Company Research Framework

---

## 1. How to Research a Company Before an Interview

Researching a company before an interview is one of the most impactful things you can do. It shows professionalism, genuine interest, and helps you decide whether this company is the right fit for you. Here is a practical framework to follow:

- **Visit Their Website**
  - Explore the homepage, about page, and careers section
  - Understand their mission statement and core values
  - Look at their product or service offerings and try to use them if possible
  - Check if they have a developer-facing product, API documentation, or open-source projects

- **Explore Their Product**
  - Sign up for a free trial or demo if available
  - Use the product as a real user would and take notes on the experience
  - Think about how the product is built technically (what frameworks, what architecture)
  - Identify areas where you could contribute or suggest improvements

- **Research Their Tech Stack**
  - Check their job postings for clues about the technologies they use
  - Look at their GitHub organization for open-source repositories
  - Visit platforms like StackShare or BuiltWith to see what technologies they rely on
  - Match your skills against their stack and prepare to discuss overlaps

- **Read Glassdoor Reviews**
  - Look at overall ratings, but focus on recent reviews (last 6-12 months)
  - Pay attention to patterns rather than individual complaints
  - Read reviews from people in engineering or development roles specifically
  - Note common themes about management, culture, work-life balance, and growth

- **Check LinkedIn Team Profiles**
  - Look at the profiles of people on the engineering team
  - See what technologies they highlight and what projects they share
  - Check how long people tend to stay at the company (high turnover is a red flag)
  - Look at the hiring manager or interviewer profile to find common ground

- **Follow Recent News and Blog Posts**
  - Check their engineering blog for technical articles and company updates
  - Look for recent press releases, funding announcements, or product launches
  - Search for the company on tech news sites like TechCrunch, Hacker News, or Product Hunt
  - Follow them on social media to understand their public voice and culture

- **Understand Their Values**
  - Read their about page and careers page carefully
  - Note any specific values they highlight (innovation, collaboration, transparency)
  - Think about how your personal values align with theirs
  - Prepare examples from your experience that reflect those shared values

## 2. Questions to Research Before the Interview

Before walking into any interview, make sure you can confidently answer these questions about the company. Not knowing basic information signals a lack of interest, while having thoughtful answers shows you are serious about the opportunity.

- **What does the company do?**
  - Be able to explain their product or service in one or two sentences
  - Understand their value proposition and what problem they solve
  - Know whether they are B2B, B2C, or both
  - Understand their business model (SaaS, e-commerce, marketplace, etc.)

- **Who are their customers?**
  - Identify the target audience or user base
  - Look at case studies or testimonials on their website
  - Understand the industries they serve
  - Think about how the product impacts end users

- **What tech stack do they use?**
  - Identify the frontend framework (React, Vue, Angular, Next.js)
  - Identify the backend technology (Node.js, Laravel, Django, Go)
  - Know what databases they likely use (PostgreSQL, MongoDB, MySQL)
  - Understand their deployment and infrastructure (AWS, Docker, Vercel, etc.)

- **What is the team size?**
  - Check LinkedIn to estimate the engineering team size
  - Understand whether you would be on a small or large team
  - Think about how team size affects your role and responsibilities
  - Smaller teams often mean broader responsibilities, larger teams mean deeper specialization

- **What is the company culture?**
  - Look at how they describe their culture on the careers page
  - Read Glassdoor reviews for employee perspectives
  - Check if they mention remote work, flexibility, or specific work styles
  - Look for signs of a healthy engineering culture (code reviews, testing, documentation)

- **Recent funding or growth**
  - Check Crunchbase for funding history and investors
  - Look at whether they are a startup, scale-up, or established company
  - Recent funding often means rapid hiring and growth opportunities
  - Understand the financial stability of the company

## 3. How to Use Your Research in the Interview

Doing the research is only half the work. The real value comes from weaving your findings naturally into the conversation. Here is how to use your research effectively without sounding like you memorized a Wikipedia article.

- **Mention Specific Products or Features**
  - "I tried your platform and really liked how the dashboard handles real-time data"
  - "I noticed you recently launched feature X, and I think that is a smart direction"
  - This shows you took time to actually use the product, not just read about it
  - Be genuine; only mention things you actually explored

- **Reference Company Blog Posts or Technical Decisions**
  - "I read your engineering blog post about migrating to microservices, and I found the approach really interesting"
  - "I saw your team wrote about adopting TypeScript, and I have gone through a similar migration"
  - This demonstrates that you are engaged with their technical community
  - It also gives you conversation starters with the interviewers

- **Show Genuine Interest**
  - Ask thoughtful questions based on your research rather than generic ones
  - "I noticed your team is relatively small for the scale of the product. How do you manage prioritization?"
  - "Your careers page mentions a focus on developer experience. Can you share more about that?"
  - Genuine curiosity is more impressive than rehearsed enthusiasm

- **Connect Your Experience to Their Needs**
  - "I see you are using React and Next.js. I have been building projects with that stack and I feel confident contributing from day one"
  - "Your job posting mentions API design. In my previous project, I designed RESTful APIs with validation and authentication"
  - Map your skills and projects to their specific requirements
  - Use the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result) when connecting your experience

## 4. What is Expected from a Junior Developer?

Being a junior developer is not about knowing everything. It is about showing that you can learn, contribute, and grow. Every senior developer started exactly where you are now, so do not let imposter syndrome hold you back.

- **Write Clean Code with Guidance**
  - Follow the team's coding standards and conventions
  - Write readable, well-named variables and functions
  - Accept code review feedback gracefully and learn from it
  - Do not try to write "clever" code; write clear and simple code
  - Use consistent formatting and follow established patterns in the codebase

- **Learn Quickly**
  - Be a sponge; absorb knowledge from code reviews, documentation, and teammates
  - Do not wait to be taught everything; take initiative to learn on your own
  - When you encounter something new, research it before asking for help
  - Keep a learning journal or notes to track what you learn each week

- **Ask Good Questions**
  - Before asking, try to solve the problem yourself for 20-30 minutes
  - When you do ask, show what you have already tried
  - "I tried X and Y, but I am still stuck on Z. Could you point me in the right direction?"
  - Never be afraid to ask; it is far worse to stay stuck silently for hours

- **Complete Assigned Tasks**
  - Break tasks into smaller steps and tackle them one at a time
  - Communicate your progress regularly, even if no one asks
  - If a task is taking longer than expected, let your team know early
  - Deliver on your commitments and be reliable

- **Understand the Basics**
  - CRUD operations (Create, Read, Update, Delete) for both frontend and backend
  - Git fundamentals (branching, committing, pull requests, resolving conflicts)
  - Debugging skills (using browser dev tools, reading error messages, console logging)
  - Basic understanding of HTTP, APIs, databases, and how the web works
  - Familiarity with at least one frontend and one backend framework

- **Communicate When Stuck**
  - Do not disappear for days without updates
  - Let your team know when you are blocked, even if it feels embarrassing
  - Use daily standups or Slack to share your status
  - Being transparent about challenges is a sign of maturity, not weakness

## 5. What is Expected from a Mid-Level Developer?

The transition from junior to mid-level is one of the biggest leaps in your career. At this stage, you are no longer just executing tasks; you are taking ownership and becoming a reliable contributor that the team depends on.

- **Work Independently**
  - Take a feature from requirements to implementation without constant supervision
  - Research and choose appropriate solutions on your own
  - Know when to make decisions independently and when to escalate
  - Be comfortable navigating unfamiliar parts of the codebase

- **Own Features End-to-End**
  - Understand the full lifecycle of a feature from design to deployment
  - Handle both the frontend and backend work for your features (as a full-stack developer)
  - Think about edge cases, error handling, and user experience
  - Take responsibility for the quality of your work, including after deployment

- **Write Tests**
  - Write unit tests for your functions and components
  - Write integration tests for API endpoints and user flows
  - Understand the testing pyramid and when to use each type of test
  - Treat tests as a first-class part of your development process, not an afterthought

- **Review Code**
  - Provide constructive and specific feedback in code reviews
  - Focus on correctness, readability, performance, and security
  - Be respectful in your comments; suggest improvements rather than criticize
  - Learn from reviewing others' code just as much as writing your own

- **Mentor Juniors**
  - Help junior developers when they are stuck without doing the work for them
  - Share knowledge through pair programming sessions
  - Explain the "why" behind decisions, not just the "what"
  - Remember how it felt to be a junior and be patient

- **Understand Architecture Basics**
  - Know the difference between monolithic and microservice architectures
  - Understand common design patterns (MVC, Repository, Observer, Singleton)
  - Be aware of database design principles (normalization, indexing, relationships)
  - Understand how the different parts of your system communicate

- **Make Technical Decisions for Your Tasks**
  - Choose the right library or tool for a specific problem
  - Decide on data structures and algorithms for your features
  - Weigh trade-offs (performance vs readability, speed vs maintainability)
  - Document your decisions so the team understands your reasoning

## 6. What is Expected from a Senior Developer?

Being a senior developer is about much more than writing great code. It is about leading, mentoring, making decisions that affect the entire team, and thinking about the bigger picture. Senior developers multiply the effectiveness of everyone around them.

- **Design Systems**
  - Design entire systems and features from a high level before writing code
  - Create architecture diagrams, data flow diagrams, and technical specifications
  - Consider scalability, security, and performance from the start
  - Design APIs and database schemas that are flexible and maintainable

- **Make Architectural Decisions**
  - Choose between monolith vs microservices, SQL vs NoSQL, REST vs GraphQL
  - Evaluate new technologies and decide whether to adopt them
  - Make decisions that will impact the codebase for years
  - Balance ideal solutions with practical constraints (time, budget, team skills)

- **Lead Technical Direction**
  - Set coding standards, best practices, and development workflows
  - Drive the adoption of tools, frameworks, and processes
  - Create technical roadmaps aligned with business goals
  - Champion code quality, testing culture, and continuous improvement

- **Mentor the Team**
  - Conduct regular one-on-one sessions with team members
  - Create a safe environment for learning and making mistakes
  - Help team members set and achieve their career goals
  - Share knowledge through documentation, tech talks, and workshops

- **Handle Ambiguity**
  - Turn vague requirements into clear technical plans
  - Make progress even when not all information is available
  - Identify risks early and communicate them to stakeholders
  - Be comfortable saying "I do not know yet, but here is how I will figure it out"

- **Optimize Performance**
  - Profile and optimize database queries, API responses, and frontend rendering
  - Implement caching strategies (Redis, CDN, browser caching)
  - Monitor application performance and set up alerts
  - Know when optimization matters and when it is premature

- **Communicate with Stakeholders**
  - Translate technical concepts into business language for non-technical stakeholders
  - Provide accurate time estimates and explain trade-offs
  - Participate in planning meetings and help shape product direction
  - Write clear documentation, RFCs, and technical proposals

- **Think About Scalability and Maintainability**
  - Write code and design systems that other developers can easily understand and extend
  - Plan for growth in users, data, and team size
  - Avoid over-engineering but also avoid shortcuts that create technical debt
  - Leave the codebase better than you found it

## 7. How to Grow from Junior to Senior

The path from junior to senior is not just about time or years of experience. It is about deliberately building skills, seeking challenges, and growing in ways that compound over time. Here is practical advice for each stage of the journey.

- **Junior to Mid-Level (Years 1-3)**
  - Master your primary tech stack deeply; do not just use it, understand how it works under the hood
  - Build complete projects from scratch (not just tutorials) to practice end-to-end development
  - Start writing tests early; it is a habit that separates professionals from hobbyists
  - Learn Git workflows properly (feature branches, pull requests, rebasing)
  - Read other people's code regularly; open-source projects are a goldmine
  - Get comfortable with the command line and basic DevOps concepts
  - Start contributing to code reviews, even if your feedback is simple at first

- **Mid-Level to Senior (Years 3-6)**
  - Take ownership of larger, more ambiguous projects
  - Learn system design fundamentals (load balancing, caching, message queues, database scaling)
  - Start mentoring junior developers and sharing your knowledge
  - Understand the business side of software; learn why features are built, not just how
  - Practice technical writing (documentation, RFCs, blog posts)
  - Learn to estimate tasks more accurately and communicate timelines
  - Build expertise in at least one area (performance, security, accessibility, DevOps)

- **The Importance of Soft Skills at Every Level**
  - Communication is the most underrated skill in software development
  - Learn to give and receive feedback constructively
  - Practice explaining complex technical topics in simple terms
  - Develop empathy for your teammates, your users, and future developers who will read your code
  - Learn to manage your time, prioritize tasks, and say no when necessary
  - Build relationships across teams, not just within engineering
  - Develop your writing skills; clear writing reflects clear thinking

- **General Advice for Continuous Growth**
  - Stay curious and never stop learning, but avoid tutorial hell
  - Contribute to open source to learn from experienced developers
  - Attend meetups, conferences, or online communities to expand your network
  - Keep a brag document that tracks your accomplishments, skills learned, and impact
  - Do not compare your chapter 1 to someone else's chapter 10
  - Seek feedback actively; do not wait for performance reviews
  - Remember that career growth is not always linear, and that is perfectly okay

## 8. How to Write a Follow-Up Email After an Interview

Sending a follow-up email within 24 hours of your interview is a small gesture that can make a big difference. It shows professionalism, reinforces your interest, and keeps you top of mind.

- **Timing**
  - Send your follow-up email within 24 hours of the interview
  - If the interview was in the afternoon, sending it the next morning is perfectly fine
  - Do not wait more than 48 hours; the impact diminishes significantly after that
  - If you interviewed with multiple people, send a personalized email to each one

- **What to Include**
  - A genuine thank you for their time and the opportunity
  - A specific reference to something you discussed (shows you were engaged)
  - A brief reinforcement of why you are a good fit for the role
  - An expression of your continued interest in the position
  - Keep it to 4-6 sentences maximum; do not write a novel

- **Follow-Up Email Template**
  - Subject: Thank you for the interview - [Your Name] - [Position]
  - "Hi [Interviewer Name],"
  - "Thank you for taking the time to speak with me today about the [Position] role. I really enjoyed our conversation, especially the discussion about [specific topic you discussed]."
  - "After learning more about the team and the technical challenges you are working on, I am even more excited about the opportunity to contribute. My experience with [relevant skill/project] aligns well with what you are looking for."
  - "Please do not hesitate to reach out if you need any additional information. I look forward to hearing from you."
  - "Best regards, [Your Name]"

- **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
  - Do not send a generic, copy-paste email that could apply to any company
  - Do not ask about salary or benefits in the follow-up email
  - Do not follow up multiple times if you do not hear back immediately
  - Do not apologize for things you think you got wrong in the interview; stay confident

## 9. How to Evaluate a Job Offer

Getting a job offer is exciting, but it is important to evaluate it carefully before accepting. A job is more than just a salary; it is where you will spend a significant portion of your life, and it shapes your career trajectory.

- **Salary**
  - Research market rates for your role, experience level, and location
  - Use platforms like Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, PayScale, or LinkedIn Salary Insights
  - Consider the cost of living in the area if the role is on-site
  - Think about the salary relative to your current financial needs and goals

- **Benefits**
  - Health insurance, dental, and vision coverage
  - Retirement plans or pension contributions
  - Paid time off (vacation days, sick days, personal days)
  - Education budgets, conference allowances, or certification reimbursements
  - Equipment allowance (laptop, monitor, desk setup for remote work)

- **Growth Opportunities**
  - Is there a clear career path from your current level to the next?
  - Does the company invest in training, mentoring, and professional development?
  - Will you work on challenging problems that stretch your skills?
  - Are there opportunities to lead projects or teams in the future?

- **Tech Stack**
  - Will you be working with modern, relevant technologies?
  - Is the stack aligned with the direction you want your career to go?
  - Will you learn new skills, or will you be maintaining legacy systems?
  - Is the team open to adopting new tools and improving their processes?

- **Team Culture**
  - How does the team collaborate (pair programming, code reviews, standups)?
  - Is there a culture of learning, feedback, and continuous improvement?
  - How does the team handle disagreements and technical debates?
  - Do people seem happy and engaged, or stressed and burned out?

- **Work-Life Balance**
  - What are the expected working hours?
  - Is overtime common or occasional?
  - How does the team handle on-call responsibilities?
  - Do people actually use their vacation days?

- **Remote Options**
  - Is the role fully remote, hybrid, or on-site?
  - If hybrid, how many days per week are required in the office?
  - Does the company have a strong remote work culture with good async communication?
  - Are remote employees treated equally to on-site employees in terms of opportunities?

## 10. What to Do If You Get Rejected

Rejection hurts, but it is a normal and inevitable part of the job search process. Every successful developer has been rejected multiple times. What matters is how you respond to it.

- **Ask for Feedback**
  - Reply to the rejection email and politely ask if they can share any feedback
  - "Thank you for letting me know. I appreciate the opportunity. If possible, could you share any feedback that might help me improve for future interviews?"
  - Not all companies will respond, but the ones that do can give you valuable insights
  - Take the feedback seriously and work on the areas they mention

- **Keep Learning**
  - Use the rejection as motivation to improve, not as a reason to give up
  - If you struggled with a technical question, study that topic thoroughly
  - If the interview exposed a gap in your skills, create a plan to fill it
  - Practice coding challenges regularly on platforms like LeetCode, HackerRank, or CodeWars

- **Do Not Take It Personally**
  - Rejection is often about fit, timing, or competition, not about your worth as a developer
  - The company might have had an internal candidate or changed their requirements
  - You might have been their second choice, which means you were very close
  - Your value as a developer is not determined by any single interview outcome

- **Improve and Try Again**
  - Review your interview performance honestly and identify what you can improve
  - Practice mock interviews with friends, mentors, or platforms like Pramp
  - Refine your resume, portfolio, and GitHub profile based on what you learn
  - Apply to more companies; increasing your volume increases your chances
  - Many companies allow you to reapply after 6-12 months, so keep the door open

- **Maintain Your Confidence**
  - Keep a list of your achievements and skills to remind yourself of your value
  - Talk to other developers who have been through the same experience
  - Remember that the job search is a numbers game; persistence pays off
  - Celebrate small wins along the way (getting an interview is already an achievement)

## 11. Negotiation Tips

Negotiating a job offer can feel uncomfortable, especially early in your career, but it is a normal and expected part of the hiring process. Companies almost always have room to negotiate, and they will not rescind an offer just because you asked.

- **Know Your Market Value**
  - Research salary ranges for your role, experience, and location thoroughly
  - Use multiple sources (Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, LinkedIn, industry surveys)
  - Talk to other developers in your network about compensation ranges
  - Factor in your specific skills, certifications, and unique experience
  - Understand that market value varies significantly by region and company size

- **Never Accept the First Offer Immediately**
  - Always take at least 24-48 hours to evaluate the offer, even if you love it
  - Say something like: "Thank you so much for the offer. I am very excited about this opportunity. Could I have a couple of days to review the details?"
  - Rushing into a decision can lead to regret later
  - Use the time to research, think, and prepare your counter-offer if needed

- **Be Polite but Firm**
  - Approach negotiation as a collaborative conversation, not a confrontation
  - Use phrases like: "Based on my research and experience, I was hoping for a salary in the range of X to Y"
  - Do not make ultimatums or threats; they damage the relationship before it starts
  - If they cannot meet your number, ask what flexibility exists in other areas
  - Be honest about your expectations and respectful of their constraints

- **Consider Total Compensation, Not Just Salary**
  - Base salary is important, but it is only one part of the picture
  - Consider bonuses, stock options or equity, and profit sharing
  - Factor in health benefits, retirement matching, and insurance
  - Remote work flexibility has real financial value (no commute, lower cost of living)
  - Professional development budgets, conference allowances, and education stipends
  - Extra vacation days can be worth thousands of dollars in quality of life
  - Flexible working hours and work-from-home options impact your daily happiness

- **Practical Negotiation Strategies**
  - If you have competing offers, you can mention them tactfully: "I am also considering another offer at X. I would love to join your team, but I want to make sure the compensation is competitive"
  - Negotiate after you have the written offer, not during early interviews
  - If salary is non-negotiable, try negotiating other things: signing bonus, extra PTO, remote days, equipment budget, or an earlier performance review
  - Always get the final offer in writing before accepting
  - Express gratitude throughout the process; you want to start the relationship on a positive note

---

> **Remember:** Your career is a marathon, not a sprint. Every interview, whether it leads to an offer or a rejection, is an opportunity to learn and grow. Stay consistent, stay curious, and trust the process. You are closer to your goal than you think.
